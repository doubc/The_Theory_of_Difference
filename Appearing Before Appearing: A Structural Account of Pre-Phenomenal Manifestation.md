## **Title**

**Appearing Before Appearing: A Structural Account of Pre-Phenomenal Manifestation**

*Subtitle candidate*: Toward a Generative Ontology of the Pre-Subjective State

---

## **Abstract**

Phenomenology has provided powerful accounts of the structures of appearing — the way in which objects and experiences manifest themselves to a subject. What it has not provided is an account of the structural genesis of the subject to whom appearing is possible. This paper introduces the concept of *xiàng* (象) — pre-phenomenal manifestation — as a structural level that precedes both biological self-organization and phenomenological constitution, including the radical phenomenology of immanence developed by Michel Henry. Drawing on a formal axiomatic framework (*WorldBase*), we trace six structural thresholds through which differential organization generates the conditions for *xiàng*: the transformation of separation into interface, closure into self-sustenance, trace into retention, recurrence into replication, co-existence into structural selection, and coupling into function. The convergence of these six conditions in a single organizational unit constitutes the *pre-subjective state*: the structural configuration that is the necessary precondition of subjectivity without being subjectivity itself — an organizational structure prior to any form of self-givenness, whether intentional or immanent. We argue that this account fills a generative gap in both phenomenological theory and enactivist cognitive science, provides a structural ground for Husserl's passive synthesis and Merleau-Ponty's body schema, and situates natural selection as a biological instantiation of the more general structural logic of differential continuation.

**Keywords:** manifestation, pre-subjective state, *xiàng*, differential organization, generative ontology, enactivism, passive synthesis, autopoiesis, structural selection, pre-reflective consciousness, Michel Henry, generative phenomenology

## **Section 1: The Problem — A Gap in the Genesis of Appearing**

Phenomenology has long insisted that the question of appearing — how anything shows itself as anything — cannot be answered by reducing it to objective processes in the world. Husserl's foundational move was to bracket the natural attitude and attend instead to the structures of intentional consciousness through which objects are constituted as meaningful. Merleau-Ponty extended this by grounding appearing in the lived body, showing that perceptual experience is not the product of a disembodied mind but of a body already oriented toward its world. More recently, Zahavi has argued that even the most basic form of self-awareness — the pre-reflective self-givenness of experience — is a structural feature of consciousness itself, not an added act of reflection. Across these variations, phenomenology converges on a shared commitment: appearing is always appearing *for* a subject, and the subject is the irreducible locus from which any account of manifestation must begin.

This commitment is not a prejudice but a methodological consequence. Phenomenology begins where experience is already underway, and it traces the structures that make experience what it is. What it does not — and perhaps cannot, by its own lights — address is the question of how the subject capable of such experience came to be structured in the way that it is. This is not a psychological or evolutionary question about the origins of individual minds. It is a structural question about the generative conditions under which something capable of appearing-for-a-subject can arise at all. Phenomenology presupposes a subject; it does not explain one.

The enactivist tradition, developed by Varela, Thompson, and Maturana, represents the most sustained attempt to bridge this gap from within the cognitive sciences. By grounding cognition in the autopoietic organization of living systems, enactivism connects the structures of experience to the material dynamics of self-producing organisms. The living body is not merely the vehicle of experience but its generative source: sense-making emerges from the operational closure and structural coupling of an organism with its environment. This is a genuine advance. It ties subjectivity to a form of organization rather than to a substance, and it opens the possibility of a naturalized account of appearing that does not simply reduce experience to neural events.

Yet autopoiesis, for all its power, remains bound to the biological. The conditions Maturana and Varela specify — metabolic closure, membrane production, self-regeneration — are conditions for *living* systems. They do not tell us why organizational closure of this kind should give rise to anything like experience, nor do they explain what is structurally distinctive about the threshold at which a self-organizing system first becomes capable of bearing a perspective. The gap between chemistry and cognition, between physical self-organization and the minimal form of subjectivity, remains insufficiently theorized. Enactivism tells us that mind is rooted in life; it does not fully explain why life, and not merely complex chemistry, crosses the relevant threshold.

This paper proposes that the gap can be addressed by identifying a structural layer that precedes both biological organization and phenomenological constitution — a layer at which differential organization first achieves what we will call *manifestation*: the capacity to maintain a boundary, sustain its own circulation, retain its path, reproduce its pattern, undergo selection, and develop internal functional differentiation. We call this layer *xiàng* (象, pronounced *xiàng*)¹, a term drawn from classical Chinese thought but here given a precise structural definition that departs from its traditional usages. *Xiàng* is not phenomenon in the phenomenological sense. It is not constituted by consciousness, nor does it presuppose a subject to whom it appears. It is the structural state that differential organization reaches when it crosses a threshold of organizational density sufficient to sustain itself in an open environment, retain the traces of its own history, and begin differentiating its internal components by their contribution to its continuation.

The argument proceeds in four steps. We first introduce the axiomatic framework from which the concept of *xiàng* is derived (Section 2). We then trace the six structural thresholds through which differential organization generates the conditions for *xiàng* (Section 3). We then show how the convergence of these thresholds produces what we call the *pre-subjective state* — the structural precondition of subjectivity that phenomenology presupposes but does not itself generate (Section 4). Finally, we draw out the implications of this account for phenomenology, enactivism, and the broader project of a generative ontology of mind (Section 5).

One clarification is necessary at the outset. To say that *xiàng* precedes phenomenological constitution is not to claim that it is more fundamental in a metaphysical sense, or that consciousness can be reduced to structural dynamics. The claim is more modest and more specific: that between the level of bare physical existence and the level at which a subject capable of intentional experience is in place, there is a structural layer whose organization has not been adequately theorized, and whose absence leaves a gap in any account of how appearing becomes possible. Filling this gap is the task of the present paper.

## **Section 2: The Framework — WorldBase Axioms and the Concept of *Xiàng***

Any structural account of manifestation must begin somewhere. The account offered here begins with a minimal set of axiomatic constraints on the nature of differential organization — constraints that are not derived from empirical observation but from the question of what conditions must hold for a world of differences to be a world at all, rather than an undifferentiated collapse. These constraints constitute the core of a formal ontology called *WorldBase*, of which the present paper develops one specific projection: the structural genesis of *xiàng*.

We do not require the full axiomatic system here. The argument that follows rests on four constraints, which we introduce not as stipulations borrowed from an external framework but as answers to a prior question: what conditions must hold for a world of differences to be a world at all, rather than an undifferentiated collapse or an unstable flux with no tendency toward local organization?

These constraints are not empirical generalizations derived from the observation of physical systems, nor are they arbitrary formal stipulations. They are transcendental in a precise, non-Kantian sense: they are the minimal structural conditions that must hold for any differential organization to be possible at all — conditions whose denial would not merely describe a different kind of world but would dissolve the very notion of a structured world of differences. If relations could act globally without mediation (against A1), no bounded region could form and organization would be impossible. If differences admitted no discrete structure (against A2), no stable distinction could be drawn and the notion of a differential relation would be empty. If change followed no path of minimal displacement (against A4), every configuration would be equally accessible from every other, and no organizational history could accumulate. If no configurations were more stable than others (against A6), nothing would tend to persist and the concept of a persistent structure would be vacuous. The four constraints are therefore not chosen but discovered — they are what remains when one asks what the minimum commitments of any coherent account of differential organization must be.

The first constraint is **locality** (A1): differential relations are always bounded in their reach. No exchange between two positions in a differential structure is globally instantaneous; every relation is mediated by the finite topology of the structure in which it occurs. This ensures that organization is always *somewhere* rather than everywhere at once, and that the formation of bounded regions is a structural possibility rather than an imposed condition.

The second is **binary instantiation** (A2): the most basic form of differential occupation is binary — a position is either occupied or unoccupied, a relation either obtains or does not. This is not a claim about the ultimate grain of physical reality but about the minimal formal structure that makes discrete differences possible, such that any continuous approximation is a derived rather than primitive description.

The third is **minimal variation** (A4): when a differential structure undergoes change, it does so along the path of least total deviation from its current state. This is not a teleological principle but a structural one: among the possible transitions available to a configuration, those that minimize aggregate displacement are the ones that the structure's own organization makes most accessible.

The fourth is **stability preference** (A6): configurations that minimize internal differential tension are more likely to persist than those that do not. Together with A4, this gives differential structures a weak but real tendency toward self-consolidation — not because they *want* to persist, but because the configurations that do persist are precisely those whose internal relations are mutually reinforcing rather than mutually destabilizing.

These four constraints are sufficient to ground the generative chain that follows. Their application to the derivation of chemical bond typology and electron-shell capacity is developed in companion technical work; what concerns us here is a different set of consequences — those bearing on the conditions under which differential organization first achieves the capacity for manifestation.

The concept of *xiàng* (象) requires careful introduction, because it is easily misread through available theoretical lenses, each of which captures something but misses the essential point.

It is not *phenomenon* in the Husserlian sense. A phenomenon, for Husserl, is an object as it appears to a constituting consciousness; it is always already the correlate of an intentional act. *Xiàng* precedes intentionality. It does not appear *to* anything; it is the structural state that differential organization reaches before any subject capable of constituting appearances is in place.

It is not *form* in the Aristotelian sense. Aristotelian form is the principle of actuality that determines what a thing is; it presupposes a metaphysics of substance and essence that the present framework deliberately avoids. *Xiàng* is not what a thing *is* but what a differential organization *does* when it crosses a threshold of organizational density.

It is not *pattern* in the sense used by complexity theory and dynamical systems research. Patterns, as typically understood in those fields, are observer-relative: they are regularities that a sufficiently positioned observer can detect in a system's behavior. *Xiàng* is observer-independent. It is a structural property of the organization itself, not a description imposed on it from outside.

The positive definition is this: *Xiàng* is the structural state that a differential organization reaches when it simultaneously satisfies the following conditions — it maintains a boundary that regulates rather than merely separates; it sustains its own circulation in an open rather than closed environment; it retains the traces of its own path in a way that biases future organization; it reproduces its key relational pattern across successive instantiations; it undergoes differential selection among co-existing organizational variants; and it develops internal functional differentiation in which some components contribute asymmetrically to the continuation of the whole. When all six conditions are met within a single organizational unit, that unit has crossed the threshold of *xiàng*. It has achieved what we will call *pre-phenomenal manifestation*: it is no longer merely present in the world, but organized in a way that makes it a candidate for bearing a perspective — without yet bearing one.

Before proceeding to the generative chain, a clarification is necessary to forestall a persistent misreading. The six conditions that jointly constitute *xiàng* are not points on a continuum of organizational complexity; they mark qualitative transformations, each of which fails to obtain in structures that satisfy only the preceding conditions. Stability is not yet manifestation: a structure may be stable under closed conditions without having crossed the threshold of interface-regulated exchange. Closure is not yet self-sustenance: a loop may return to itself without being capable of reconstituting its organization under open-environment perturbation. A trace is not yet retention: a residue may persist without continuing to bias the probability distribution of future configurations. Recurrence is not yet replication: a pattern may re-appear under similar conditions without the organizational pattern itself providing the template for its own reconstitution. And co-existence is not yet selection: multiple patterns may simultaneously obtain without their differential continuation probabilities having begun to diverge. The threshold of *xiàng* is crossed only when all six conditions converge in a single organizational unit. This is not an arbitrary stipulation but a consequence of the generative logic traced in the following section: each condition is not merely added to the preceding ones but is necessitated by them under open-environment constraints, and the full convergence produces a qualitative change in organizational capacity that none of the conditions produces alone.

This definition has two features worth emphasizing. First, it is threshold-based rather than gradational. The six conditions are individually insufficient; it is their convergence in a single organizational unit that constitutes *xiàng*. A structure may satisfy five of the six conditions and still not have crossed the threshold. This is not an arbitrary stipulation but a consequence of the generative logic: as we will show in Section 3, each condition is the structural precondition of the next, and the full convergence produces a qualitative change in organizational capacity that none of the conditions produces alone.

Second, the definition is entirely low-semantic. None of the six conditions involves meaning, intention, value, identity, or narrative. *Xiàng* is a structural achievement, not a semantic one. This is precisely what makes it available as the ground floor of a generative account of subjectivity: it provides the organizational skeleton that higher-semantic structures — including phenomenological consciousness — can be shown to presuppose, without those structures being smuggled in at the foundational level.

We turn now to the generative chain through which these six conditions arise.

## **Section 3: The Generative Chain — Six Structural Thresholds**

The concept of *xiàng* is not a primitive. It does not appear all at once, nor is it arbitrarily stipulated. It is the convergent result of six structural thresholds, each of which is a necessary condition for the next, and each of which represents a qualitative transformation in what differential organization is capable of doing. We trace these thresholds in sequence, noting at each step both the structural logic that necessitates the transition and the point of contact — and divergence — with existing theoretical frameworks.

### **3.1 From Separation to Interface**

The first threshold is the transformation of a boundary from a mere separation into a functional interface. In the most minimal sense, any differential structure that is not perfectly homogeneous will exhibit regions of greater and lesser density, proximity, and relational intensity. These regions are separated from one another in the trivial sense that they are not identical. But separation of this kind is not yet a boundary in any organizationally significant sense. It does not regulate what passes between regions; it only marks that they differ.

A boundary becomes an interface when it begins to perform selective mediation: when certain exchanges are facilitated, others impeded, and the pattern of facilitation and impedance is itself stable enough to persist across successive interactions with the environment. The interface does not seal the organization off from its surroundings — complete closure would prevent the differential input necessary for sustained organization. Nor does it dissolve the distinction between inside and outside — complete openness would prevent any stable internal configuration from forming. The interface is the structural form of a regulated opening: it makes the environment available to the organization on terms that the organization's own structure partially determines.

This transformation is necessitated by A1 and A4 jointly. Locality (A1) ensures that differential relations are always bounded; minimal variation (A4) ensures that the boundary configuration that minimizes total internal displacement is the one that is preferentially stabilized. The result is not a wall but a filter: a structural feature that makes the organization's internal relations relatively more stable than they would be in the absence of regulated exchange.

Why should a separation give way to interface rather than simply persisting as passive differentiation, or dissolving entirely? The answer lies in the structural dynamics of an open environment. Open-environment conditions are not an optional or contingent feature of the structures under consideration: they are a direct consequence of locality (A1), which entails that no differential structure can be globally isolated — there is always a beyond, and exchange across any boundary is structurally inevitable. A purely passive separation is unstable under conditions of ongoing exchange: differential gradients across the boundary will drive exchange that tends to equalize the separated regions, eroding the very differentiation that constituted the boundary. Complete closure — the elimination of exchange — would prevent this erosion, but at the cost of cutting off the differential input necessary for the organization's own continuation: a fully sealed structure cannot absorb the perturbations it needs to reconstitute its internal relations. The only configuration that persists under open-environment conditions is one in which the boundary itself begins to regulate exchange — facilitating some transfers, impeding others — in a way that maintains the differential structure on both sides. This is not an additional mechanism imposed on the structure from outside; it is the structural consequence of minimal variation (A4) operating on a localized differential organization subject to environmental perturbation. The boundary that persists under these conditions is, by that very fact, an interface. Separation that does not become interface does not persist.

The phenomenological resonance here is with Husserl's concept of the *horizon* (Horizont) — the structured field of co-given but non-thematic possibilities that surrounds any focal object of experience. The horizon, like the interface, is not a wall but a regulated opening: it makes the world available to consciousness in a structured way, not as an undifferentiated flood of stimulation. But the analogy has a precise limit. Husserl's horizon is a feature of intentional consciousness; it is constituted by the acts of a subject already in place. The interface we are describing is prior to any subject. It is a structural property of differential organization itself, and it arises from the dynamics of that organization rather than from the constitutive activity of a mind.

### **3.2 From Closure to Self-Sustenance**

The second threshold is the transformation of a closed loop into a self-sustaining circulation. Closure, in the minimal sense available from the WorldBase framework, means that a differential relation can return to itself: a path through the structure loops back to its starting configuration, producing a cycle rather than a linear dissipation. Closure is a necessary condition for any persistence beyond the momentary, and in this sense it is a precondition for everything that follows. But closure alone is insufficient for *xiàng*. A closed loop that can only maintain itself under perfectly stable external conditions is not self-sustaining; it is merely temporarily sheltered.

Why should closure give way to self-sustenance rather than remaining as simple cyclic return? The answer follows from the interface condition established in 3.1. A closed loop that operates within a fully isolated structure can, in principle, continue indefinitely — but once a boundary has become a regulated interface, the organization is continuously exposed to environmental perturbation that alters its internal differential configuration. Under these conditions, a loop that can only reconstitute itself under the specific configuration in which it originally formed will encounter configurations that differ from its formation conditions. At that point, it faces two structural outcomes: it either fails to reconstitute and dissolves, or it reconstitutes along the path of least internal displacement (A4) from its current — perturbed — configuration, incorporating the perturbation into its own continuation. Stability preference (A6) ensures that the second outcome is selectively retained: configurations capable of reconstituting under varying conditions are more stable than those that cannot. The result is not a loop that avoids perturbation but a circulation that maintains itself *by means of* perturbation — incorporating environmental variation into its own reconstitutive dynamics rather than requiring its absence. This is self-sustenance, and it is not an optional elaboration of closure but its only stable form under open-environment conditions.

Self-sustenance requires something more: the capacity to maintain organizational continuity under conditions of ongoing exchange with an open environment. A self-sustaining organization does not merely loop; it loops *while* absorbing perturbations, processing differential inputs through its interface, and reconstituting its key relational patterns in the face of continuous variation. The difference between closure and self-sustenance is the difference between a system that persists because nothing disturbs it and a system that persists because its own organization actively reconstitutes the conditions of its persistence.

This transformation is necessitated by the conjunction of A4 and A6 with the interface condition established in 3.1. Once a boundary has become a regulated interface, the organization is no longer isolated from environmental variation. It must either reconstitute its internal relations in the face of that variation or dissolve. Stability preference (A6) ensures that configurations capable of reconstitution are selectively retained; minimal variation (A4) ensures that reconstitution follows the path of least internal displacement. The result is an organization that does not merely tolerate perturbation but incorporates it into its own continuation.

The connection to autopoiesis here is direct and the divergence is precise. Maturana and Varela's autopoietic systems are operationally closed: their defining characteristic is that they continuously produce the very components that constitute them, maintaining their organization through metabolic self-production. This is a powerful and well-developed account of biological self-sustenance. The divergence is that autopoiesis is defined in terms of *component production* — a specifically biological mechanism — whereas the self-sustenance we are describing is defined purely in terms of *relational reconstitution*. It does not require metabolism, membrane production, or any other biological process. It requires only that the organization's key relational patterns be capable of re-establishing themselves through the dynamics of minimal variation and stability preference. This makes the threshold of self-sustenance available to non-biological systems — including, as we will note in Section 5, artificial systems — in a way that autopoiesis is not.

### **3.3 From Trace to Retention**

The third threshold is the transformation of a trace into a retention. A trace, in the most minimal sense, is the residue of a past differential relation: something that occurred has not been completely erased, and its remnant continues to occupy some position in the structure. Traces are ubiquitous in any differential structure that is not perfectly reversible. They are, in themselves, inert: they mark that something happened, but they do not, by themselves, do anything with that mark.

Retention is a different matter. Retention occurs when a trace is not merely present but *operative*: when the residue of a past path continues to bias the probability distribution of future organizational configurations. A retained path does not simply record that a certain sequence of relations obtained; it makes that sequence — or sequences structurally similar to it — more likely to obtain again. The past, in a structure that has crossed the threshold of retention, is not merely gone; it continues to exert a structural force on what comes next.

This transformation is necessitated by the conjunction of self-sustenance with the directionality introduced by A4. A self-sustaining organization, by definition, reconstitutes its key relational patterns across successive perturbations. Each act of reconstitution leaves a differential residue — a slight asymmetry in the configuration of the organization's internal relations that reflects the particular path by which reconstitution was achieved. Under minimal variation, these asymmetries are not erased in subsequent reconstitutions; they are incorporated as the starting configuration from which the next reconstitution proceeds. The cumulative result is a structure whose current configuration is partially determined by the history of its own reconstitutions: a structure that has, in a precise structural sense, a past.

This is the point of deepest contact between the present framework and phenomenological theory, and it is worth dwelling on the comparison carefully. Husserl's analysis of time-consciousness in the *Lectures on the Phenomenology of Internal Time-Consciousness* introduces the concept of *retention* to describe the way in which just-elapsed phases of experience remain co-present in the living present of consciousness. The retained phase is not remembered — it is not the object of a recollective act — but it is not simply gone either. It continues to co-constitute the present moment as its just-having-been. This structure of retention, Husserl argues, is what makes temporal experience possible at all: without the retention of the just-past, each moment of experience would be isolated from every other, and the flow of consciousness would be unintelligible.

The structural retention we are describing is both analogous to and categorically different from Husserlian retention. The analogy is precise: in both cases, a past phase continues to co-constitute a present configuration without being the object of a thematic act. The difference is equally precise: Husserlian retention is a feature of the living present of intentional consciousness. It is the way in which time-consciousness constitutes temporal objects for a subject. The structural retention of *xiàng* is prior to any subject and prior to any consciousness. It is not the retention of an experienced phase but the retention of a differential path — the way in which the history of an organization's reconstitutions continues to bias its future configurations. This is retention without a retaining subject: memory without a rememberer.

One further structural consequence of self-sustenance requires explicit statement, because it marks the threshold at which retention becomes genuinely distinct from operative trace. A trace may be operative — it may affect the current configuration of a structure — without being *re-invocable* across multiple successive rounds of reconstitution. The question is whether self-sustaining structures necessarily generate re-invocable traces, or whether operativity and re-invocability can come apart.

The answer follows from the recursive structure of self-sustaining reconstitution. In a self-sustaining organization, each reconstitution takes as its starting point the current configuration of the organization — a configuration that already incorporates the differential residues of all prior reconstitutions. Minimal variation (A4) ensures that the next reconstitution proceeds from this starting point along the path of least displacement, which means that any asymmetry introduced by a prior reconstitution is not erased but carried forward as a bias in the next. The trace left by reconstitution $n$ shapes the starting configuration for reconstitution $n+1$, whose residue in turn shapes reconstitution $n+2$, and so on. Re-invocability is therefore not a separate mechanism added to retention; it is the direct consequence of the recursive structure of self-sustaining reconstitution operating under minimal variation. A self-sustaining organization that retains at all retains re-invocably — not because it has a dedicated retrieval system, but because the path-dependent dynamics of its own reconstitution continuously re-engage the biases introduced by prior paths. This is what makes retention the structural precondition of replication: a structure that retains in this sense does not merely carry its past into the present; it makes its past available to its future.

### **3.4 From Recurrence to Replication**

The fourth threshold is the transformation of recurrence into replication. Recurrence is the re-appearance of a similar organizational configuration across multiple occasions. It is common, and it requires no special explanation: if the conditions that produced a configuration obtain again, a similar configuration will tend to re-appear. Recurrence of this kind is condition-dependent and fragile; it tells us nothing about whether the organizational pattern itself has acquired any capacity to reproduce itself.

Replication is structurally distinct. Replication occurs when an organizational pattern acquires the capacity to re-establish its key relational structure across successive instantiations, not merely because external conditions happen to repeat but because the pattern itself — through the retained path structure established in 3.3 — provides the internal template from which reconstitution proceeds. A replicating pattern does not merely recur; it *re-generates itself*, using the bias introduced by its own retained history as the organizational scaffold for its next instantiation.

The distinction matters because replication, unlike recurrence, is relatively condition-independent. A replicating pattern can re-establish itself under conditions that differ from those in which it originally formed, provided the deviation does not exceed the range within which its retained path structure can guide reconstitution. This robustness is precisely what makes replication the carrier of organizational history: it is the mechanism by which a pattern's past becomes available to its future across varying conditions.

It is important to be clear about what replication does *not* require. It does not require perfect fidelity — the replicated pattern need not be identical to its predecessor, only structurally continuous with it in its key relational features. It does not require a dedicated copying mechanism — the replication is achieved through the dynamics of minimal variation operating on a retained path structure, not through a specialized molecular machinery. And it does not require a discrete individual unit — the pattern that replicates may be distributed across a region of differential structure rather than localized in a single bounded entity. These clarifications are important because they distinguish the structural concept of replication from the biological concept of genetic reproduction, while showing that the latter is a special case of the former.

Why should replication give way to selection rather than remaining as the parallel continuation of equivalent patterns? The answer lies in the organizational differences that replication itself introduces and sustains. Replication, as established in 3.4, is not perfect fidelity but structural continuity across varying conditions — and the conditions under which different patterns replicate are never identical. Patterns differ in the robustness of their retained path structures, the stability of their interfaces, and the efficiency of their self-sustaining circulations. These differences are not incidental; they are the cumulative product of each pattern's own reconstitutive history. Once multiple patterns capable of replication co-exist within a shared differential environment, their organizational differences translate directly into differences in continuation probability: some patterns re-establish themselves more reliably than others across successive rounds of environmental perturbation. The co-existence of replicating patterns with unequal continuation probabilities is not a contingent feature of particular environments — it is the structural consequence of replication operating under locality (A1), which ensures that no two patterns occupy identical positions in the differential structure, and minimal variation (A4), which ensures that their reconstitutions follow paths shaped by their distinct organizational histories. Differential continuation is therefore not something that happens *to* replicating patterns from outside; it is the internal structural consequence of what replication is.

### **3.5 From Co-existence to Selection**

The fifth threshold is the transformation of co-existence into selection. When multiple organizational patterns capable of replication co-exist within a shared differential environment, they do not automatically continue with equal probability. Patterns differ in the robustness of their retained path structures, the stability of their interfaces, the efficiency of their self-sustaining circulations, and the fidelity of their replication. These differences translate directly into differences in continuation probability: some patterns are more likely than others to re-establish themselves across the next round of environmental perturbation.

We use the term *structural selection* — rather than simply selection — to mark a precise distinction from Darwinian natural selection and from competition in the ordinary sense. Structural selection requires no direct antagonism between co-existing patterns, no contention over shared resources, and no strategic awareness on the part of the patterns themselves. It operates wherever differential continuation probabilities arise from organizational differences under open-environment conditions, regardless of whether dedicated biological mechanisms for inheritance and reproduction are in place. Darwinian selection is a powerful and well-studied instantiation of this more general structural logic, not its definition.

Selection, as we use the term here, is simply the cumulative consequence of these differential continuation probabilities. It is not a mechanism that acts on patterns from outside; it is the statistical result of the patterns' own organizational differences playing out across successive rounds of perturbation and reconstitution. No agent selects; no criterion is applied; no value is assigned. The patterns that continue are simply those whose organizational properties make continuation more likely under the prevailing conditions.

This is the point at which the framework makes contact with evolutionary theory, and the relationship requires careful statement. Lewontin's classic formalization of Darwinian evolution specifies three conditions: variation among individuals in a population, heritability of that variation, and differential fitness — differential reproduction as a function of heritable variation. These three conditions are sufficient for cumulative evolutionary change. What the present framework shows is that Lewontin's conditions are not the *definition* of selection but a *biological instantiation* of a more general structural logic. Variation corresponds to the differential organizational properties established through the thresholds of 3.1–3.4; heritability corresponds to replication; differential fitness corresponds to the differential continuation probabilities that arise from organizational differences under open-environment conditions. The biological case is special in having dedicated mechanisms — genetic inheritance, cellular reproduction — that implement this general logic with high fidelity. But the logic itself is available wherever differential organization has crossed the thresholds of interface, self-sustenance, retention, and replication. The implications of this for evolutionary theory will be noted briefly in Section 5.

The selection logic established here operates not only between organizational units but within them — and this internal operation is what generates the threshold of function. Once an organization has crossed the threshold of structural selection, the same differential continuation probabilities that distinguish one pattern from another also begin to distinguish one internal component from another. Not all internal relational patterns contribute equally to the maintenance of the interface, the stability of the self-sustaining circulation, the reliability of retention, or the fidelity of replication. Those that contribute more are preferentially retained across successive reconstitutions; those that contribute less are more readily displaced. The cumulative result of this internal differential retention is not a new mechanism but a new organizational fact: some components have come to occupy a position in the organization that is asymmetrically important to its continuation. This asymmetric importance, stabilized through the organization's own selective history, is what the following section calls function.

### **3.6 From Coupling to Function**

The sixth and final threshold is the transformation of coupling into function. The selection logic established in 3.5 operates not only between organizational units but within them. When an organization has crossed the threshold of structural selection, the same differential continuation probabilities that distinguish one pattern from another also begin to differentiate one internal component from another within a single organization. Some relational patterns contribute more than others to the maintenance of the interface, the stability of the self-sustaining circulation, the reliability of retention, or the fidelity of replication. These differential contributions are not assigned from outside; they emerge from the dynamics of structural selection operating within the organization's own history. Components whose relational patterns consistently contribute to continuation are preferentially retained; those whose contributions are marginal or destabilizing are more likely to be displaced in successive reconstitutions. The threshold of function is crossed when this internal differential retention stabilizes into a durable asymmetry — when some components have come to occupy a position in the organization that is not merely contingently important but reliably and repeatedly confirmed as important by the organization's own reconstitutive history.

Function, as we use the term here, is this asymmetric contribution, stabilized through the organization's own selective history. A component has a function within an organization when its relational pattern is preferentially retained because of its contribution to the organization's continuation — not because it was designed for that purpose, not because an observer assigns it a role, but because the dynamics of the organization's own history have repeatedly confirmed its contribution and incorporated it into the organization's retained path structure.

This concept of function is explicitly low-semantic. It does not involve purpose, meaning, or normative assessment. It is closer to what Cummins calls a *systems function* — the contribution of a component to the capacities of the system of which it is a part — than to what Millikan calls a *proper function*, which involves a history of selection *for* a specific effect. But it differs from Cummins's account in being grounded in a generative structural history rather than in a synchronic systems analysis. The function of a component in a *xiàng*-level organization is not simply what it does now but what it has been repeatedly confirmed as doing across the organization's history of reconstitution and selection.

The emergence of internal functional differentiation marks the completion of the generative chain. An organization that has crossed all six thresholds — interface, self-sustenance, retention, replication, selection, and functional differentiation — is no longer merely a differential structure that happens to persist. It is an organization with a regulated boundary, a self-sustaining dynamic, a structural memory, a capacity for pattern reproduction, a history of differential continuation, and an internal architecture shaped by that history. This is *xiàng* in its full sense. And it is, as we will now argue, the structural precondition of subjectivity.

## **Section 4: The Pre-Subjective State — Structural Preconditions of Subjectivity**

The six thresholds traced in Section 3 do not individually constitute *xiàng*. Each is a necessary condition; their convergence in a single organizational unit is the sufficient condition. When an organization simultaneously maintains a functional interface, sustains its circulation in an open environment, retains the traces of its own path, replicates its key relational patterns, undergoes internal selection, and develops functional differentiation, it has reached what we call the *pre-subjective state*: the organizational configuration that is the structural precondition of subjectivity without being subjectivity itself.

The pre-subjective state requires careful positioning with respect to two existing theoretical frameworks that it most closely resembles: Zahavi's account of pre-reflective self-consciousness, and the autopoietic account of the minimal self. The positioning is not merely terminological; it concerns the depth at which the generative account of subjectivity is pitched.

### **4.1 The Pre-Subjective State and Pre-Reflective Self-Consciousness**

Zahavi, following Sartre, argues that consciousness is characterized by a pre-reflective self-givenness that is not the product of a higher-order monitoring act. Every conscious experience, on this view, is implicitly aware of itself as an experience: there is something it is like to have it, and this self-givenness is not added to the experience by a subsequent act of reflection but is intrinsic to the experience's own structure. This pre-reflective self-consciousness is, for Zahavi, the most basic form of subjectivity: it is what it means for there to be a subject of experience at all.

The pre-subjective state we are describing is not pre-reflective self-consciousness. It is the structural condition that makes pre-reflective self-consciousness possible. The difference can be stated precisely: pre-reflective self-consciousness is a feature of *experience* — it is the way experience is given to itself. The pre-subjective state is a feature of *organization* — it is the way a differential structure is organized such that it is capable of bearing experience at all. Pre-reflective self-consciousness presupposes a subject already organized in the way that the pre-subjective state describes; it does not explain how that organization came to be.

To put it in the terms of Zahavi's own framework: the minimal self — the most basic form of subjectivity, characterized by pre-reflective self-givenness — is not itself the starting point of a generative account of mind. It is the *explanandum* of such an account. The pre-subjective state is the *explanans*: the structural configuration that, when it achieves sufficient organizational density, makes the minimal self a structural possibility rather than a brute given.

This is not a criticism of Zahavi's project. The phenomenological analysis of pre-reflective self-consciousness is a genuine achievement, and nothing in the present account undermines it. The point is that phenomenological analysis operates within a domain that already presupposes the pre-subjective state. It takes as its starting point an organization that has already crossed the six thresholds; it then describes the structures of experience that such an organization makes possible. What it does not — and by its own methodological commitments cannot — do is explain how the organization that makes experience possible came to be structured in the way that it is. That is the task of the present account.

### **4.2 The Pre-Subjective State and Henry's Auto-Appearing**

The account developed here must also be distinguished from Michel Henry's phenomenology of life, which represents the most radical attempt within the phenomenological tradition to identify a layer of manifestation that precedes the subject-object structure of intentional consciousness. For Henry, the most fundamental form of appearing is not the appearing of objects to a subject but the self-appearing (*auto-apparaître*) of life to itself — an absolute immanence in which life is given to itself prior to any transcendence, any distance, any intentional directedness toward a world. This self-appearing is, for Henry, the condition of all other forms of manifestation: it is what makes it possible for anything to appear at all.

The structural parallel with *xiàng* is genuine and should be acknowledged. Both accounts identify a layer of manifestation that precedes intentional consciousness; both resist the reduction of this layer to the structures of experience that phenomenology typically analyzes; both insist that this layer is the generative ground of higher forms of appearing. The divergence, however, is categorical.

Henry's auto-appearing is an *immanent* structure: it is the self-givenness of life to itself, and it operates entirely within the domain of consciousness — at its most fundamental, pre-reflective level. It is, in Husserl's terms, a phenomenological concept: it describes how consciousness is given to itself before it is directed toward any object. The pre-subjective state as we describe it is an *organizational* structure: it is a property of differential organization itself, prior to any form of self-givenness, prior to any form of consciousness, prior to anything it would be like to be. It does not appear to itself; it has not yet crossed the threshold at which there is any appearing-to at all. Where Henry radicalizes phenomenology by pushing its analysis to its immanent ground, the present account steps outside phenomenology's domain entirely — not to negate it, but to identify the structural layer that phenomenological analysis, including Henry's, necessarily presupposes.

The distinction can be pressed one level further. Henry's auto-appearing, however radical its departure from intentional consciousness, retains the structure of *self-givenness*: life appears *to itself*, even if this appearing-to is absolute immanence rather than transcendence toward an object. The self-givenness is what makes Henry's account phenomenological at its core — there is, for Henry, always a minimal "to whom" of appearing, even when that "to whom" is life's own absolute interiority. *Xiàng* does not appear to anything — not even to itself. It has not yet crossed the threshold at which any form of givenness, whether immanent or transcendent, whether directed outward or folded inward, is operative. The pre-subjective state is the organizational condition for the possibility of any appearing-to; it is not itself an instance of appearing-to. This is the categorical difference: Henry locates the ground of manifestation within a radicalized phenomenology of immanence; the present account locates it outside phenomenology's domain entirely — in the structural organization that any appearing-to, including Henry's absolute immanence, must presuppose.

### **4.3 The Pre-Subjective State and the Minimal Self of Autopoiesis**

Thompson's *Mind in Life* develops an account of the minimal self grounded in the autopoietic organization of living systems. For Thompson, a living system is a self that constitutes itself through its own metabolic activity: it is not merely a system that processes information but an organization that brings itself into being and maintains itself in being through its own operational closure. This self-constitution is the basis of sense-making: the living system relates to its environment not neutrally but in terms of what is relevant to its own continuation. The minimal self, on this account, is the living body as a self-producing, sense-making organization.

The pre-subjective state shares with Thompson's minimal self the emphasis on organizational self-constitution and the grounding of sense-making in the dynamics of continuation. But it differs in two respects that are structurally significant.

The first difference concerns the level of analysis. Thompson's minimal self is defined at the biological level: it requires metabolic closure, membrane production, and the specific biochemical dynamics of living systems. The pre-subjective state is defined at a more fundamental level: it requires only the six structural thresholds traced in Section 3, none of which presupposes biological organization. This means that the pre-subjective state is available as a theoretical resource wherever differential organization achieves the requisite density — including in non-biological systems. Whether non-biological systems that achieve the pre-subjective state thereby achieve something like experience is a further question that the present account deliberately leaves open. What it establishes is that the structural preconditions of subjectivity do not require biological instantiation.

The second difference concerns the relationship between organization and history. Thompson's autopoietic self is characterized primarily by its present operational closure: it is a self because it currently produces itself. The pre-subjective state is characterized by the convergence of present organization with accumulated history: the retained path structure (3.3), the replication capacity (3.4), and the functionally differentiated internal architecture (3.6) are all products of the organization's own selective history, not merely features of its current operational state. This means that the pre-subjective state is irreducibly historical: it cannot be fully described by a synchronic analysis of its current organization. It carries its past within it, in the form of the biases introduced by its retained path structure and the functional differentiation shaped by its selective history.

### **4.4 The Pre-Subjective State as Structural Precondition**

The pre-subjective state is not a weak or incomplete form of subjectivity. It is a distinct organizational level with its own structural logic, and its character is most precisely captured by a series of paired contrasts: it has a boundary, but not an identity; it has self-sustenance, but not a will; it has retention, but not recollection; it has replication, but not self-representation; it has selection, but not valuation; it has functional differentiation, but not meaning-assignment. Each of these contrasts marks a precise boundary between the structural and the semantic. The boundary of the pre-subjective state regulates exchange; it does not demarcate a self from an other in any sense that requires the self to be known as such. Its self-sustenance maintains organizational continuity; it does not involve any orientation toward continuation as a goal. Its retention biases future configurations; it does not involve any experiential holding of the just-past. Its replication reconstitutes key relational patterns; it does not involve any re-presentation of a prior state to a subject. Its selection differentiates continuation probabilities; it does not involve any assessment of what is better or worse. Its functional differentiation stabilizes asymmetric contributions to organizational continuation; it does not assign those contributions any significance beyond their structural role.

What the pre-subjective state does have is the organizational skeleton that all higher-level achievements presuppose. Identity requires a boundary stable enough to maintain the distinction between self and other across time; the pre-subjective state provides the interface. Intentionality requires an organization capable of relating to its environment in terms of its own continuation; the pre-subjective state provides the self-sustaining dynamic and the functional differentiation that makes some environmental features relevant and others not. Narrative requires a structure capable of retaining its own history in a form that can be organized into a temporal account; the pre-subjective state provides the retained path structure that is the material substrate of any such account.

In this sense, the pre-subjective state is the *structural floor* of subjectivity: the lowest level at which the organizational conditions necessary for any form of subjectivity are simultaneously in place. Below this floor, differential organization can be complex, stable, and even self-sustaining in a minimal sense, but it lacks the convergent organizational density that makes it a candidate for bearing a perspective. Above this floor, the organizational achievements of subjectivity — pre-reflective self-consciousness, intentionality, embodied sense-making, narrative self-constitution — can be understood as successive elaborations of the structural skeleton that the pre-subjective state provides.

This account has one further consequence that deserves explicit statement. The pre-subjective state is not a fixed threshold but a range. Organizations can achieve the six structural conditions to varying degrees of stability, robustness, and internal differentiation. The transition from the pre-subjective state to the minimal self of phenomenological consciousness is not a sharp boundary but a zone of increasing organizational density in which the structural conditions for experience become progressively more fully realized. This means that the question "at what point does a pre-subjective organization become a subject?" does not have a single precise answer — not because the question is confused, but because the organizational continuum that connects the two does not contain a sharp discontinuity. What the present account provides is not a criterion for drawing the line but a structural description of the territory on both sides of it and the generative logic that connects them.

## **Section 5: Implications and Open Questions**

The account developed in the preceding sections has consequences that extend beyond the specific theoretical context in which it was developed. We draw out three directions here, with the understanding that each represents a research program rather than a completed argument.

### **5.1 Implications for Phenomenology**

The relationship between the present account and phenomenology is not one of opposition but of complementary depth. Phenomenology, as we noted in Section 1, begins where experience is already underway. It takes as its starting point an organization that has already crossed the six structural thresholds and asks: what are the structures of the experience that such an organization makes possible? This is a legitimate and important question, and the phenomenological tradition has developed powerful conceptual tools for answering it. Nothing in the present account undermines those tools or the insights they have generated.

What the present account adds is a generative prehistory for the structures that phenomenology describes. Consider Husserl's analysis of passive synthesis — the pre-intentional processes by which the raw material of experience is organized into coherent wholes before any active constitutive act takes place. Husserl identifies several forms of passive synthesis, including the associative binding of similar elements, the temporal synthesis of the living present, and the habituation through which past experience shapes present receptivity. These processes are, for Husserl, the lowest level of intentional life: they operate below the threshold of active attention but within the domain of consciousness.

The retained path structure of the pre-subjective state (3.3) is the structural substrate of passive synthesis. The way in which a *xiàng*-level organization's current configuration is biased by the history of its own reconstitutions is the structural mechanism that, at the level of conscious experience, appears as the passive synthesis of habitual association. Husserl's passive synthesis does not arise from nowhere; it is the phenomenological expression of a structural dynamic that is already operative at the pre-subjective level. This does not reduce passive synthesis to structural dynamics — the phenomenological description retains its own validity and its own explanatory domain. But it connects passive synthesis to a generative ground that phenomenology itself cannot provide, because phenomenology's own method prevents it from descending below the level of experience.

A parallel observation applies to Merleau-Ponty's body schema. The body schema, for Merleau-Ponty, is the pre-reflective organization of the body's motor capacities into a coherent practical field: it is the way in which the body knows how to act in its environment without explicit deliberation. The body schema is not a representation of the body; it is the body's own practical self-organization, continuously updated through motor engagement with the world. The functional differentiation of the pre-subjective state (3.6) is the structural antecedent of the body schema: it is the way in which an organization's internal components come to be asymmetrically positioned with respect to the organization's continuation, before that asymmetry is expressed as the differentiated practical capacities of a lived body.

These connections do not collapse the distinction between the structural and the phenomenological. They show, rather, that the phenomenological and the structural are descriptions of the same generative process at different levels of organizational density. The structural account describes the process from below, in terms of differential organization and its thresholds; the phenomenological account describes it from within, in terms of the structures of experience that the process makes possible. A complete account of the genesis of appearing requires both.

A further connection deserves acknowledgment. Husserl's genetic phenomenology, particularly as developed in *Experience and Judgment* and the later manuscripts on passive synthesis, already attempts to trace the genesis of constitutive structures — the emergence of object-constitution from pre-predicative experience, the sedimentation of habitual dispositions, the temporal formation of associative fields. The present account can be understood as extending this genetic project one level further down: where Husserl traces the genesis of object-constitution from pre-predicative givenness, we trace the genesis of the organizational conditions that make pre-predicative givenness possible in the first place. The two projects are complementary in their direction and discontinuous in their domain. Husserl's genetic phenomenology remains within the domain of experience — it traces how one form of experience emerges from another, more primitive form. The generative account offered here operates below the threshold of experience entirely, tracing how the organizational conditions for any experience come to be structured. This is not a criticism of genetic phenomenology but a specification of where its own generative account must bottom out: in the structural organization that makes the having of any experience — pre-predicative or otherwise — possible at all.

### **5.2 Implications for Enactivism and Cognitive Science**

The enactivist tradition has made the concept of the minimal self central to its account of cognition. On the enactivist view, cognition is not the processing of pre-given information by a brain but the sense-making activity of an organism engaged with its environment. The minimal self — the living body as a self-producing, sense-making organization — is the subject of this activity, and the task of cognitive science is to understand how the structures of cognition are grounded in the dynamics of embodied, environmentally embedded life.

The present account supports the enactivist emphasis on organizational self-constitution and embodied sense-making, but it extends the theoretical reach of these concepts in two directions.

The first extension concerns the level of analysis. As noted in Section 4, the pre-subjective state is defined at a level of structural generality that does not presuppose biological organization. This means that the enactivist account of sense-making, which is currently tied to the specific dynamics of living systems, can be generalized to any differential organization that achieves the pre-subjective state. Whether artificial systems that achieve this state thereby achieve something like sense-making is an empirical question that the present account does not settle. What it provides is the structural framework within which the question can be precisely posed: an artificial system achieves the structural preconditions of sense-making when and only when it crosses all six thresholds simultaneously, not merely when it exhibits behavioral flexibility or environmental responsiveness. This is a more demanding criterion than is typically applied in discussions of artificial cognition, and it has the virtue of being structurally grounded rather than behaviorally stipulated.

The second extension concerns the relationship between cognition and history. Enactivist accounts of cognition have tended to emphasize the present dynamics of organism-environment coupling — the way in which cognitive processes are constituted by the ongoing interaction between an organism and its environment. The historical dimension of cognition — the way in which an organism's past interactions shape its present cognitive capacities — has received less systematic theoretical treatment. The retained path structure and the selective history of functional differentiation that characterize the pre-subjective state provide a structural account of this historical dimension: cognitive history is not merely a sequence of past events but a cumulative bias in the organization's differential structure that continuously shapes its present and future configurations. This connects enactivism to a broader account of organizational memory that does not require neural implementation and is therefore available as a theoretical resource across the full range of cognitive systems.

### **5.3 Toward an Evolutionary Dimension**

The structural account of selection developed in Section 3.5 opens a dialogue with evolutionary theory that we can only gesture toward here. Lewontin's three conditions for Darwinian evolution — variation, heritability, and differential fitness — are, as we noted, a biological instantiation of the more general structural logic of differential continuation. The present account suggests that natural selection is not the origin of the selection logic but its biological amplification: the logic of differential continuation under open-environment conditions is already operative at the pre-subjective level, and biological evolution represents the case in which this logic is implemented with sufficient fidelity and cumulative force to produce the diversity of living forms.

If this is correct, it has consequences for debates about the scope and limits of Darwinian explanation. The question of whether natural selection can explain the origin of life — the transition from complex chemistry to the first self-replicating, self-sustaining organizations — has been a persistent difficulty for evolutionary theory, precisely because natural selection in the standard biological sense presupposes the very organizational features it is supposed to explain. The pre-subjective state framework suggests a resolution: selection in the structural sense is already operative below the threshold of biological organization, and the origin of life is the point at which structural selection achieves sufficient organizational density to instantiate the specifically biological mechanisms — genetic inheritance, cellular reproduction — that Darwinian evolution then operates on. The origin of life is not the origin of selection but the origin of a particularly powerful implementation of a more general structural dynamic. This connection will be developed in a separate treatment.

### **5.4 Open Questions**

The account developed here is a beginning, not a completion. Three open questions deserve explicit acknowledgment.

The first concerns the transition from the pre-subjective state to the minimal self of phenomenological consciousness. We have argued that the pre-subjective state is the structural precondition of subjectivity, and that the transition between them is a zone of increasing organizational density rather than a sharp boundary. What we have not specified is whether this transition requires an additional, seventh structural threshold — a qualitative transformation analogous to the six traced here, in which some further organizational condition is crossed that is jointly sufficient for the emergence of pre-reflective self-givenness — or whether it is a continuous elaboration of the existing six thresholds under conditions of sufficient density, with no discrete crossing point. The present framework is compatible with both possibilities. The first would mean that the hard problem of consciousness can be reframed as the identification of a seventh structural threshold; the second would mean that it dissolves into a question of organizational density. Distinguishing between them requires both a more precise characterization of what pre-reflective self-givenness structurally consists in and a more complete formalization of the generative chain developed here — both of which are tasks for future work.

The second open question concerns the relationship between *xiàng* and the specific organizational forms that instantiate it. The account given here is deliberately abstract: it describes the structural conditions for *xiàng* without specifying the physical, chemical, or biological substrates in which those conditions can be realized. The connection between the abstract structural account and the concrete organizational forms that satisfy it — including the chemical bond structures whose axiomatization is developed in companion work — is a task for subsequent research.

The third open question is methodological. The present account is developed within a formal axiomatic framework, and its claims are in principle derivable from that framework. But the derivations have not been fully formalized here; they have been presented in a discursive form that makes the logical structure visible without making it machine-checkable. The formalization of the generative chain — the rigorous derivation of each threshold from the preceding conditions and the WorldBase axioms — is a project currently underway and will be reported separately.
