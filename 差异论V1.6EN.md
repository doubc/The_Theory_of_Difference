Volume I: Ontological Foundations
Preface to the Volume: Where You Begin Determines What You End Up Seeing
This book starts from an ordinary but stubborn puzzle.

When we try to explain society, we almost immediately run into words that have been taken for granted yet seldom
questioned. Words like interest, power, culture, institution, rationality, identity, state, market. They are used
constantly in the social sciences, like a set of well-worn tools that have become second nature. The problem is, once a
tool becomes too easy to use, people forget to ask: where did this tool come from? Why does it hold up? What makes it
distinct from other tools? If the boundaries of these words are themselves shaky, then the explanations built on top of
them — how solid are they really?

My skepticism toward many social theories begins right here.

It is not that they lack explanatory power. On the contrary, it is precisely because they tend to have too much
explanatory power that they deserve extra scrutiny. When a theory can rapidly slot complex reality into a familiar
framework and deliver a tidy narrative, it naturally feels reassuring. But reassurance can also be a kind ofocclusion.
The faster we accept a starting point, the less we interrogate its origins. What we end up with is not deeper
understanding but more proficient repetition.

What this book sets out to do is neither to demolish all existing theories nor to claim it has finally found the
ultimate answer. Its aim is to push one layer further back: before concepts like interest, power, culture, and
institution, is there a more fundamental starting point — one that carries fewer preset conflicts?

The answer this book offers is: yes. That starting point is Difference.

The “Difference” discussed here is not a catchy slogan, nor is it the vague truism that “the world is just different.”
It is closer to the following claim:

Anything that can be a thing, any relation that can be a relation, any subject that can be recognized, any institution
that can operate, any narrative that can take effect — all presuppose that some Difference has already come into being.

Without Difference, you cannot distinguish this from that, inside from outside; you cannot establish boundaries, speak
of structure, define change, or even say that something “exists.” It is not that complete things come first and we then
attach a layer of Difference to them. The more accurate sequence is: recognizable Difference comes first, and only then
do stable things, subjects, and institutions get named.

This book departs from that starting point, but it does not intend to stop at the declaration that “Difference is the
ontological ground.” That would be too easy and too hollow. The truly difficult task is not to state a bold thesis, but
to develop that thesis into a framework that actually works: Can it explain the formation of society, the stability of
institutions, the conflicts of identity, the ruptures of history, the fluctuations of markets, the alliances and
dissolution of states? Can it show why some Differences cluster together, some get suppressed, some flow back, some
continue to accumulate in invisible places? Can it let us see that society is not composed of static entities, but of
Difference-relations that are constantly flowing, transforming, clustering, and rupturing?

If this framework has any value, it lies not in making the world seem more mysterious, but in giving clearer structural
explanations to phenomena that are usually dismissed as “coincidence” or “just human nature.”

For instance: Why do some institutions, the more successful they become, the more easily they turn into sources of
rigidity later on? Why do Differences, once suppressed, tend to return in more dangerous forms? Why does society seldom
move directly toward the best state, but instead tends to settle first into an arrangement that can temporarily
stabilize the situation? Why does the market not merely reflect reality, but participate in shaping it? Why are
alliances between states often born not from mutual affection, but from a shared confrontation with some common
contrast? Why can a theory, once widely believed, change the very object it originally sought to describe?

These questions will not be treated in this book as isolated topics. They will be placed back into the same underlying
framework, understood as manifestations of the same set of Difference-movement mechanisms at different scales.

The first volume of this book is the foundation of the entire framework. It does not rush to explain everything;
instead, it puts back on the table some of the most easily skipped questions: Why does Difference matter? How does
structure form out of discreteness? Why does social change tend to happen not as smooth gradients but through events
that rupture the flow? Where do compressed Differences go? Why does change more often proceed along the path of least
resistance? After imbalance, why does a system usually not reach the optimal solution directly, but instead slide first
toward a nearest equilibrium that can temporarily stabilize things?

These questions may seem abstract, but they are far from detached from reality. On the contrary, they determine how we
understand the most concrete things. How you understand reform, crisis, institution, conflict, bubble, crash, alliance,
dissolution — all depends on what you place at the starting point.

If the starting point is wrong, then the stronger the subsequent explanatory power, the further it tends to stray.

So this book does not begin with “what is society.” It begins with a more upstream question:

Before we have described the world in terms of “interest,” “power,” “culture,” and “rationality,” in what way does the
world first present itself to us?

My answer is: it first presents itself through Difference.

And once you accept this starting point, many seemingly scattered questions begin to illuminate one another.

Chapter 1: The Ontological Status of Difference
I. Three Familiar Starting Points, Three Unresolved Difficulties
In 1945, Japan was defeated. The Korean Peninsula was split along the 38th parallel into two occupation zones. At that
moment, the two sides of the line shared almost the same language, similar institutional memories, a common colonial
experience, comparable demographic structures, and similar ways of life. They were not two worlds that had long been
strangers to each other; they were the same society, suddenly cut in two by a line.

Decades later, the two sides of that line became one of the starkest contrasts in human history.

The south became an industrialized, urbanized nation deeply embedded in global markets; the north took a path of extreme
closure and highly concentrated power. All the subsequent differences between the two sides can, of course, be traced to
a chain of historical causes. But that is precisely the problem:

Which type of cause should we treat as the most fundamental starting point?

On this question, the social sciences have long had roughly three familiar modes of departure.

The first takes material conditions as the starting point.
The second takes ideas or ideology as the starting point.
The third takes the choices of rational individuals as the starting point.

None of these starting points is unfamiliar, and each is powerful in its own right. But all three share the same hidden
problem: they too quickly take something “already recognized” as the origin, seldom pushing one step further to ask —

What makes it possible for this thing to be recognized in the first place?

The Difficulty of Materialism Is Not That It Is Wrong, but That It Is Too Fast
One of the most powerful paths in the social sciences places material modes of production at the foundation. People must
first eat, clothe themselves, and labor before they can engage in politics, law, religion, or culture.

This claim has an extremely strong sense of reality and explains a great deal of historical phenomena. The Industrial
Revolution changed modes of production, and thereby changed class structures, state capacity, and political
institutions — there is no denying that.

But the problem lies right there.

If you take “the material” as the most fundamental starting point, you need to answer: why do societies with similar
material conditions diverge onto radically different institutional paths? If the answer is “because they adopted
different policies afterward,” then the question has merely been pushed back: why did they adopt different policies? If
you then answer “because the leaders had different ideas” or “because the international configuration was different,”
you have effectively conceded that the material is no longer the sole starting point.

At a deeper level, the concept of “the material” is not self-evident. The reason we can call certain things “material
conditions” is that we are already performing a distinction: separating them from ideas, institutions, narratives,
symbols, and the like. Without this distinction, “the material” cannot step forward on its own and declare, “I am the
foundation.”

In other words, materialism does not lack explanatory power; rather, it presupposes a premise that has not yet been
explained:

The reason the material can be treated as an independent dimension depends on Difference having already been drawn.

The Difficulty of Ideationalism Is Not That It Is Too Abstract, but That It Often Stops Halfway
Opposite materialism, another powerful path places ideas at the front. Weber’s argument about the Protestant ethic is a
classic example: ideas are not merely reflections — they can become forces that drive changes in social structure. What
a society believes, what it considers legitimate, how it understands labor, wealth, salvation, and order — all of these
profoundly shape institutions and behavior.

This path also captures something real. Many major social changes cannot be fully explained by bread alone. Revolution,
nationalism, religious reform, Cold War confrontation, market confidence, currency collapse — none of these can do
without the organizing force of ideas.

But the difficulty of idealism is equally obvious:

Where do ideas come from? Why did this set of ideas become dominant here, while another did not?

If you chase this question all the way back, you find that ideas never emerge from a vacuum. Their spread depends on
media; their legitimacy depends on institutions; their appeal depends on social circumstances; their visibility depends
on who speaks them, who is willing to listen, and who has the capacity to turn them into reality.

Thus, although idealism emphasizes the importance of the spiritual and the narrative, it still tends to stop halfway —
it explains how ideas work, but not why ideas can be distinguished, recognized, and organized into an effective picture
of the world.

And all of this still leads back to that more fundamental question:

The reason a certain idea can become “this set of ideas,” the reason it can be distinguished from other ideas and other
descriptions of reality, depends on Difference.

The Difficulty of the Rational-Actor Paradigm Is Not That It Lacks Refinement, but That Its Premises Are Too Clean
The third starting point takes the individual as a rational agent pursuing self-interest or utility under constraints.
Modern economics and most social-science modeling work are built on this path. Its strength lies in its high clarity:
preferences, constraints, choices, equilibrium, incentives — once these are laid out, many problems can be precisely
analyzed.

But its premises are also too clean.

It assumes the “individual” as already complete, self-consistent, knowing what it wants, and capable of making choices
in response to changing circumstances.

The problem is that real individuals are not given in this way. An individual’s preferences, identity, goals, fears, and
judgment are themselves gradually formed within Difference-relations. Who he is often depends on who he differs from,
who he is close to, what position he has been placed in, and what narrative has named him.

In other words, the rational-actor paradigm skips an extremely critical step:

The reason an individual can become “a calculable, choice-making individual” is itself the result of some
Difference-structure having already taken shape.

In this sense, the rational actor is not the original starting point, but more like an emergent form that appears once
Difference-relations have stabilized.

II. The Question Truly Being Skipped Is Not “Which Matters More,” but “What Comes First”
Material conditions, ideas, and rational individuals — all three matter. The issue is not to pick a winner among them,
but to ask:

Before these already-recognized things, what happened first?

For “the material” to become the material, there must be a distinction between it and “the non-material.”
For “ideas” to become ideas, there must be distinctions between them and other ideas, and between them and descriptions
of reality.
For “the individual” to become an individual, there must be distinctions between him and others, between him and his
environment, between him and groups.
For “institutions” to become institutions, there must be distinctions between rules and non-rules, inside and outside,
permitted and not permitted.
For “identity” to become identity, there must be a distinction between “us” and “them.”

These distinctions are not secondary operations, not something attached to things after they already fully exist. A more
accurate statement is:

Without these distinctions, many things we assume already exist could not be established as “themselves” at all.

Therefore, this book places Difference in a more upstream position — not to elevate a fashionable word, but because it
carries fewer preset conflicts than “the material,” “ideas,” or “the rational individual.”

You can certainly argue that Difference is not the ultimate ontological ground, but it is hard to deny: the moment any
social analysis begins to operate, it is already using Difference.

This is precisely where this book begins.

III. What Is Difference: It Is Not an Accessory Feature, but a Condition of Being
It is necessary here to make “Difference” more precise.

The Difference discussed in this book is, first of all, not moral inequality, nor the statistical gap between high and
low, nor the vague “diversity” celebrated in cultural studies. These are merely specific manifestations of Difference,
not Difference itself.

Difference, as used here, refers to:

The condition by which an object, position, relation, or state can be distinguished from other objects, positions,
relations, or states.

Put more plainly: it is the reason something is this rather than that.

If a world had absolutely nothing distinguishable — no boundaries, no positional differences, no property differences,
no relational differences — it would be very hard to say there were “multiple things” there. More precisely, even the
word “there is” would lose its operative meaning. Because “there is” itself means separating something from the state of
“not having it” or “not being it.”

So Difference is not a label attached after things already fully exist; it is closer to the precondition for things to
be established at all.

This brings us to the first core claim of this chapter:

Definition 1.1: Difference is not an accessory feature of things, but the precondition for things to be established,
distinguished, and recognized.
One must be especially careful here not to misunderstand this definition as a form of mystical ontology. Difference is
not some super-entity floating above the world, nor does it aim to replace all concrete objects of analysis. It is a
minimal precondition: you may discuss material, institutions, ideas, and subjects after it, but the moment you discuss
any of these, you have already presupposed that Difference exists.

This places Difference logically in a more upstream position than most concepts in the social sciences.

IV. Without Difference, There Is No World: This Is a Logical Starting Point, Not a Figure of Speech
Consider an extreme thought experiment.

Suppose there exists a universe in which there is no positional difference, no qualitative difference, no velocity
difference, no discernible boundary whatsoever. Every point is perfectly identical; every moment is perfectly identical;
there is no this and that, no inside and outside.

Does such a universe “exist”?

Ordinary intuition might say: perhaps it exists, but we simply cannot recognize it.

But the problem is precisely this: if it is in principle indistinguishable, unidentifiable, and undescribable, then what
difference does “existing” versus “not existing” make for it?

The position this book adopts here is:

If a state has nothing distinguishable between it and “the state of not being it,” then calling it an “existence” is
analytically empty.

This is not a word game but the minimal logic of existence claims. Because the moment you say “it exists,” you have
already separated it from other states. And the act of separation itself depends on Difference.

Therefore, the second core claim of this chapter can be stated as:

Proposition 1.1: Without Difference, there is no distinction; without distinction, there is no world.
The “world” here does not mean the universe in the astronomical sense, but any collection of being that can be
identified, described, and analyzed. As long as you are still saying “there is something here,” you are already within
Difference.

V. Existence Does Not Precede Difference; It Is Established Through Difference
Many people will instinctively object: don’t things come first, and then Difference? For example, first there are two
apples, and then we notice “this one is bigger than that one.”

This objection seems natural, but on closer inspection, it has already smuggled in the very thing it is trying to
refute.

Because when you say “two apples,” you have already distinguished “this one” from “that one.” Without this distinction,
you could not say “two”; you could only say “an undifferentiated mass.” And the moment you can say “this one” and “that
one,” Difference has already been used.

Going further, even the category “apple” itself is established through Difference. It is called an apple because it
differs from oranges, pears, stones, and wood.

The precondition of classification is not sameness, but the prior availability of Differences that can be gathered. What
we call sameness is often just Differences compressed to a degree where people feel they can be grouped together.

In other words, “identity” (sameness) is not the opposite of Difference. It is more often the result of Difference being
organized, compressed, and named in some particular way.

Therefore, the third core claim of this chapter is:

Proposition 1.2: Difference precedes classification; classification precedes the stable naming of things.
This does not mean that in reality there is literally an abstract “Difference” hanging in the air before apples appear.
It means that in the order of analysis, only once there are distinguishable Differences can those stable “things,”
“types,” “subjects,” and “institutions” be established afterward.

VI. Three Layers of Difference: This Step Must Be Made Clear
To prevent the rest of this book from using “Difference” too loosely, a layered treatment must be introduced here.

The first layer is constitutive Difference.
This is the most fundamental sense: the condition by which anything can be distinguished from anything else. Without
this layer of Difference, there can be no object, position, relation, or boundary to speak of.

The second layer is distributional Difference.
This already enters the social world. Gaps in wealth, power, resources, opportunity allocation, risk exposure,
visibility, and discursive authority — these are all distributional Differences. They are not what makes the world
“exist,” but what gives the social world its internal uneven structure.

The third layer is interpretive Difference.
This is the narrative and cognitive layer. The same set of real Differences can be seen as different things by different
people. Some see class differences; some see identity differences; some see regional differences; some see
civilizational differences. The key here is not which interpretation is “absolutely correct,” but that Difference always
has to be brought into visibility through some narrative framework.

These three layers will resonate repeatedly in the chapters to come. The reason they are planted here in Chapter 1 is
precisely to prevent the single word “Difference” from having to carry too many un-layered tasks later on.

VII. Difference Is Dynamic: The World Is Not Laid Out Static, but Is Constantly Being Distinguished, Compressed,
Transferred, and Reorganized
If Difference were merely a static precondition, much of what follows in this book would not hold. Because the most
important fact about the social world is not that “Difference exists,” but that “Difference is moving.”

New Differences constantly arise. The emergence of technology, changes in institutions, the formation of new groups, the
spread of new narratives — all of these create new boundaries and new distinctions in the world. Old Differences get
compressed, renamed, transferred to invisible places, or stably organized into some institution.

Therefore, this chapter must add one more claim:

Proposition 1.3: Difference is not a static background, but one of the most fundamental dynamic facts in the world.
The world is not composed of a pile of pre-arranged entities that occasionally undergo some change. A picture closer to
reality is: the world is constantly forming new Differences, compressing old ones, making some Differences explicit, and
making others implicit. What we will later discuss under clustering, conservation, institutions, identity, and markets —
all of this is about the same phenomenon at different scales.

VIII. Placing Difference at the Starting Point Does Not Mean Difference Can Explain Everything
At this point, a boundary must be drawn immediately.

Difference is the starting point of this book, but that does not mean it can directly replace all concrete analysis.
Placing Difference first is meant to let the subsequent concepts — material, ideas, subjects, institutions, narratives —
settle more solidly, not to flatten them all.

In other words, Difference is the starting point, not a shortcut.

The moment a theory writes its core concept as “capable of explaining everything,” it effectively explains nothing. The
Theory of Difference will not take that road.

The chapters ahead will continuously distinguish: which propositions can be strongly derived from Difference, which are
merely structural arguments, and which are only conceptual illustrations.

This boundary-awareness is itself part of the Theory of Difference:

If a theory does not even have its own boundaries, the first thing it lacks is the Difference from “what is not itself.”

IX. The Landing Point of This Chapter: Why We Must Begin Here
This chapter must arrive at a conclusion that is clear enough.

Placing Difference at the starting point is not meant to appear deeper than other theories. It is meant to allow us to
reorganize the entire framework of social analysis without presupposing the priority of any particular kind of entity.
It frees us from having to first decide whether the material is primary, or ideas are primary, or individuals are
primary. Instead, it asks us to acknowledge: whatever starting point you choose, you are already using Difference.

From this starting point, the following questions become tractable:

How does Difference move from a dispersed state toward structure?
Why does structure not remain static, but get broken and reorganized over time?
Why do compressed Differences not disappear?
Why does social change more often occur along the path of least resistance?
Why, once an old order is unbalanced, does the new situation tend to first settle into a nearest arrangement that can
temporarily hold things together, rather than directly reaching the optimal state?
These questions will be taken up beginning in the next chapter.

This chapter has done only one thing: opened the door.

It lets us temporarily leave those starting points that have become so familiar they are no longer questioned, and move
to a more upstream position to take a fresh look: before the social world is described as “interests,” “institutions,”
“culture,” or “markets,” how was it first established?

The answer of this book is: it was first established as Difference.

This is not the end.

It is only where the real work of the book begins.

Chapter Echo
You will see a certain judgment return repeatedly from different angles:

Without Difference, the world cannot be established; once Difference is established, it will not remain static, but will
continue moving along paths of clustering, compression, transfer, reflux, and reorganization.

The question for the next chapter is the next step in this process:

Since Difference is not a static background, how does it cluster together? How does it move from dispersion to
structure?

Chapter 2: Clustering and the Formation of Structure
I. The World Will Not Stay Dispersed Forever: The Question Is Not Whether Difference Exists, but Why Difference Clusters
Together
If Chapter 1 established only half the story, what it says is: the world presupposes Difference.
But establishing only this half is far from sufficient.

Because a world composed of Difference does not automatically equal a structured world. Difference can be scattered,
fragmented, and mutually unmeshing; it can also be dense, stable, and mutually reinforcing. In other words, the
existence of Difference is not what is remarkable. What is truly remarkable is:

Why Difference does not remain dispersed forever, but clusters together at certain times, in certain places, and at
certain scales, forming a structure capable of sustained existence.

This is the real question of this chapter.

We deal every day with these “things that have clustered together,” yet seldom ask in reverse how they came to be. A
family, a company, a city, a nation, an institution, a state, a market order — on the surface, they all look like
finished objects. But if you break them apart, you find they are not entities that fell from the sky. They are stable
structures formed when a mass of Differences, under certain conditions, interlock, constrain, and reinforce one another.

In other words, the world does not merely “have Difference.” Something more critical is constantly happening:

Difference is clustering.

Without clustering, Difference is only a scattered state.
With clustering, Difference begins to form structure.
Once structure forms, the subsequent layers — institutions, subjects, identity, markets, states — have a chance to
emerge.

So what this chapter addresses is not “whether Difference exists,” but “how Difference moves from dispersion to
structure.”

II. A Simple but Important Distinction: Being Piled Together Is Not the Same as Being Clustered Together
A distinction must first be drawn here. Many phenomena that look like “lots of things together” are not actually
clustering.

A pile of sand on the ground is accumulation, not clustering.
A mall full of people is crowding, not necessarily clustering.
A book full of concepts does not automatically constitute a theory.
A country full of people does not automatically mean a stable community has formed.

Why?

Because being “together” is far from enough. What truly matters is: whether these Differences have established some
sustained connection, whether they have changed each other’s mode of being, and whether they have jointly generated some
new quality that none of the individual parts possessed on its own.

Without these, it is just piling, squeezing, and temporary collision.
With these, it can be called clustering.

Therefore, this chapter offers its first definition.

Definition 2.1: Clustering is not the simple juxtaposition of Differences, but the process by which Differences, under
some shared constraint, form stable connections and thereby generate higher-level structure.
There are three key words in this definition: shared constraint, stable connection, higher level.

“Shared constraint” means that not every Difference wanders in its own direction; instead, some external pressure,
internal need, or common contrast pushes them in one direction.
“Stable connection” means these Differences do not scatter at first touch, but form relatively enduring links.
“Higher level” means the result of clustering is not “addition as-is,” but the emergence of a new structure that did not
exist when any single Difference stood alone.

Without these three conditions, there is no clustering in the true sense.

III. Why Do Differences Cluster: Because the World Always Contains Pressures That Push Them Together
Differences do not naturally love to cluster together. The reason they cluster together is usually not because they
“spontaneously like order” from within, but because some pressure makes the dispersed state increasingly hard to
sustain.

This pressure can come from a common threat.
It can come from a shared survival need.
It can come from mutual benefit.
Or it can come from a more immediate reality: the cost of existing alone is too high, and clustering makes survival
easier.

Most important clusters in human history have this structure behind them.

The formation of clans and tribes was not because humans are naturally organizational, but because the costs for
isolated individuals in hunting, defense, and raising offspring were too high.
The formation of states was not because humans inherently aspire to the state, but because sustained warfare, taxation,
governance, and border maintenance required some higher-intensity organizational capacity.
The formation of money was not because humans suddenly wanted to invent an abstract system, but because as the scale of
exchange expanded, barter became increasingly costly.
The formation of firms was not because people in the market suddenly fell in love with hierarchy, but because when
transactions become frequent enough, long-term organization is cheaper than repeated ad hoc market exchanges.
The formation of alliances was not because states admire each other, but because they commonly face some threat.

This tells us something:

Clustering does not happen gently; it is often a pressure-driven organizational response.

If a world contains no pressure that draws Differences together, then Differences can certainly remain dispersed. But
reality is not like that.
Reality always contains forces that compel Differences to rearrange: danger, opportunity, competition, efficiency,
survival, fear, desire, the need for order, the need for meaning. Under these forces, certain Differences begin to draw
closer to each other and form more stable connections.

IV. The First Condition for Clustering: There Must Be a “Common Contrast”
The Theory of Difference needs to introduce a very important concept here: common contrast.

A common contrast is not simply “everyone is different.” Rather: multiple Difference-units, though different from each
other, simultaneously face some larger external Difference, and are therefore compelled or attracted to draw closer to
one another.

This is extremely common in human reality.

A family naturally contains Differences: different personalities, ages, roles, resources. But when it faces its external
environment, these internal Differences partially contract, and the family is organized into a relatively unified unit.
A company contains countless Differences: departments, hierarchies, interests, styles, knowledge backgrounds all differ.
But facing competitors, market pressure, and survival challenges, these Differences get pushed into a larger framework
by some common contrast.
A country contains class, regional, ethnic, industrial, and narrative divisions, but when facing war or strong external
threat, internal Differences are often temporarily rearranged, and the country is organized as a higher-level unit of
action.

This shows: clustering is not about first eliminating internal Differences and then forming structure. Rather,

it is because a sufficiently strong common contrast has appeared that internal Differences are temporarily compressed,
coordinated, or organized.

Therefore, the second key definition of this chapter is:

Definition 2.2: A common contrast is an external Difference that multiple Difference-units face together; it provides
direction, pressure, and boundaries for clustering.
Without a common contrast, ad hoc connections between Differences may certainly occur, but sustained structures are hard
to form.
With a common contrast, Differences have reason to include each other within the same framework, even if they remain
different internally.

This judgment will resonate repeatedly in international politics, institutional analysis, and identity formation.

V. Clustering Does Not Eliminate Internal Differences; It Reorganizes Them
A common misunderstanding must be avoided here: clustering does not smooth out internal Differences.

Many people, the moment they hear “forming structure,” tend to imagine: the more internally uniform, the better; the
more homogeneous, the more stable. Reality is not that simple. Many stable structures do not rely on eliminating
Differences but on

reorganizing Differences.

If everyone in a company were the same, it would actually be very hard to operate. It can operate because some people
are responsible for decision-making, some for execution, some for sales, some for finance, some for technology. In other
words, what it needs is not the absence of Difference, but organized Difference.
A country is the same. It does not need all its citizens to become the same kind of person; it needs to incorporate
different regions, different occupations, different functions, and different narratives into a maintainable
order-structure as far as possible.
The market is even more typical. A market is not a collection of homogeneous individuals, but a price system formed by
organizing a mass of agents with different valuations, different information, different risk preferences, and different
time preferences through transactions.

This shows that the most essential act of clustering is not flattening Differences, but:

Letting some Differences be preserved
Letting some Differences move to secondary positions
Letting some Differencestake on structural functions
Letting some Differencessubmit to higher-level coordination
Therefore, the third judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 2.1: Clustering does not eliminate Difference; it rearranges Difference into a sustainable structure.
This proposition is important, because later discussions of institutions, hierarchies, subjects, and markets will all
depend on it.
All structures that can truly sustain their existence are not structures “without Difference,” but structures “with
Difference rearranged.”

VI. Once Clustering Occurs, Something New Appears in the World: Structure
When Differences begin to cluster in a stable way, the most important result is not “more in quantity,” but the
emergence of a new level of object: structure.

Structure is not a shell layered on top of Difference. Structure is the overall form that emerges when
Difference-relations are organized into some stable state.

A family is structure.
A state is structure.
A market is structure.
A legal order is structure.
Companies, armies, churches, political parties, universities, monetary systems — all are structures.

A structure has at least three characteristics.

First, it is not any single part. If youdisassemble it, it does not equal any one of its members.
Second, it exerts constraining force back on its components.
Third, it possesses properties that none of its individual parts have when standing alone.

A firm’s “corporate culture” is a classic example. It is not any single employee’s personality, nor any slogan written
on the wall; it is an overall atmosphere clustered from countless behaviors, rewards and punishments, role expectations,
and historical sedimentation.
This atmosphere in turn constrains how employees speak, act, what gets rewarded, and what gets excluded.

Therefore, this chapter offers its fourth definition.

Definition 2.3: Structure is an overall arrangement formed after Differences undergo stable clustering, capable of in
turn constraining the Difference-units that compose it.
With this definition, many things later in the book no longer need to be understood as “some abstract noun,” but can be
understood as: the overall state of Difference after being organized.

VII. Once Structure Forms, It Changes Difference Itself
This is the most underappreciated aspect of clustering.

Many people think: first there is Difference, Differences cluster, structure forms, and then structure is just the
result. This is wrong. Once structure forms, it reshapes, compresses, amplifies, and filters the original Differences.

In other words, clustering is not a one-time event. It creates a feedback loop.

Once a school is established, the Differences between students and teachers are no longer merely “individual ability
differences”; they get redefined by the school system: who is a good student, who is a problem student, what counts as
superior, what counts as falling behind.
Once a market forms, the Differences between buyers and sellers are no longer raw preference differences; they get
reorganized by the price system.
Once a state forms, many individual Differences get renamed by the state: citizen versus non-citizen, legal versus
illegal, served versus draft-dodger, taxpayer versus welfare recipient.
Once an identity community forms, the previously loose Differences among members get re-divided in the process of “who
truly counts as one of us.”

Therefore, the fifth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 2.2: The structure formed by clustering is not merely the result of Difference; it also reshapes Difference
itself.
This sentence may look like something that belongs in later chapters on institutions, identity, and narrative. But it
must be planted here from the start. Because if structure cannot reshape Difference in return, then structure is merely
a passive result and cannot generate real historical momentum.

The reason structures in reality matter is precisely that once they appear, they begin rewriting the Differences that
compose them.

VIII. The Selection of Clustering Is Not Random: Not All Differences Can Grow into Structure
One final question remains unanswered.

Some Differences are too private to beshared recognized.
Some Differences are not easily usable as a basis for mobilization and organization.
Some Differences cannot be clearly spoken, explained, or stably named, and thusrarelyexpand into collective structure.

First, it must be visible

Skin-color differences are often immediately visible;
Religious differences are highly visible in some societies and morehidden in others.

Being visible is not just sensory seeing; it also includes being recognized by social language, recorded by
institutions, and repeatedly named by the public.

Second, it must be usable
The mere existence of a Difference is not enough; it must also be deployable for organizing action, allocating
resources, constructing boundaries, and building legitimacy.
A ruler may use it; a rebel may also use it.
An institution may organize around it; a narrative may also expand around it.

In other words, once a Difference enters the social world, it is seldom purely neutral. It quickly becomes a resource to
be contested, mobilized, and encoded.

Third, it must be narratable
If a Difference cannot be narrativized, it usuallyrarely becomes a lasting public structure.
Only when a Difference can be told as a story of “why we are different,” “why this matters,” or “why we must do this”
does it readily acquire durability.

Proposition 2.3: Differences capable of forming structure typically possess recognizability, usability, and
narratability simultaneously.

answer it arrived at is not mysterious: Difference. Difference makes it possible for the world to be distinguished;
Clustering lets Difference grow into Structure; Events cut through Structure; Conservation ensures that Difference
cannot be zeroed out without cost; and Subjects are continuously produced through the maintenance of boundaries.

If Volume 1 was like digging underground, then what Volume 2 must do is bring those underground load-bearing structures
back to the surface.

That is to say, starting from this volume, the book will no longer primarily deal with the foundational question of “why
the world can hold together,” and will instead begin to address questions closer to the heartland of social science:
power, wealth, identity, institutions, narrative, conflict, reform, collapse, order, governance, markets, and the state.
These will not be treated as independent topics to be explained separately. Rather, they will be understood within a
single framework—as different forms, different flows, and different organizational outcomes of Difference at the social
scale.

One thing must be especially emphasized here.

This book does not attempt to rewrite social science as a hard physics. It will not flatten people, institutions, and
history into a few equations, much less pretend that the social world can be precisely closed off like a laboratory
system.
Social science is not physics; history is not a repeatable experiment; institutions are not particles; and subjects are
not neutral units that can be stripped of narrative and memory. If the Theory of Difference has any value, it lies not
in making society resemble nature more closely, but in discovering:

The social world is not identical to the natural world, yet it is still subject to certain deeper structural
constraints.

These constraints manifest in more convoluted, more opaque ways at the social scale, and are more frequently obscured by
narrative and institutional mediation. The task of Volume 2, therefore, is not to directly transplant axioms onto social
phenomena, but to establish a mapping:
to show what forms Difference primarily takes in society, how these forms transform into one another, how they become
locked in by institutions, how they acquire direction through narrative, and how they suddenly become visible through
Events.

Volume 2 starts here: first distinguishing the most basic forms of Difference in the social world.

Because if you cannot even clarify “what Difference typically looks like in society,” then all subsequent analysis of
conflict, institutions, narrative, markets, and the state will easily slide back into loose metaphor.

So the first chapter of this volume will not yet discuss revolution, markets, or international order. Instead, it will
first address a more fundamental but more easily overlooked question:

What forms does Difference most commonly take in society?
Chapter 6: Three Basic Forms of Social Difference
I. If you don’t first distinguish the forms of Difference, the subsequent analysis will keep contradicting itself
In everyday language, when people say “difference,” they tend to use it as a catch-all.

Income difference, status difference, identity difference, power difference, education difference, cognitive difference,
regional difference, cultural difference, generational difference—all of these can be called “difference.”
This usage is fine in casual conversation, but problematic in theory. Because if the main forms of Difference are not
first separated, the analysis will keep running into a particular kind of confusion:
You think you are talking about wealth, but have already slipped into identity; you think you are talking about power,
but end up discussing legitimacy; you think a society is “becoming more equal,” but it has merely pushed Difference from
one dimension to another.

In other words, Difference can certainly have countless specific manifestations, but in social analysis, without a
sufficiently stable primary classification, the entire framework will keep diverging.

So Volume 2 must begin with a foundational task:

Distinguish the most common, most critical, and most readily convertible forms of Difference in the social world.
This classification cannot have too many categories, or it loses its axis; nor can it have too few, or it flattens
distinctions that actually matter.
This book adopts a three-part classification:

Power Difference
Wealth Difference
Identity Difference
These three are not the only differences in society, but they are the three with the greatest organizing force.
More importantly, they do not simply sit side by side. They continuously convert into one another, mask one another, and
support one another, forming the most central set of dynamic structures in the social world.

II. The First Form: Power Difference
Let us begin with the most direct and most easily felt form.

Definition 6.1: Power Difference is the inequality among subjects in their capacity to influence the behavior,
resources, rules, and boundaries of others.
Put more bluntly: who can make whom move, who can make whom stop, who has the final say, and whose intentions will be
treated as “things to be carried out.”

Power Difference does not exist only in the state and its apparatus of violence.
It is certainly most visible in the state, the military, the police, the courts, corporate leadership, organizational
authority, and the governance structures of major platforms—but it does not stop there.
There is Power Difference within families; there is Power Difference in schools; there is Power Difference in
intellectual communities; and even in social relationships that appear most egalitarian, there is often a difference in
“who is better able to define the situation.”

The core of Power Difference is not just “commanding,” but the capacity to define the situation.
Who can decide what counts as a problem, what counts as a violation, what counts as normal, what counts as
transgression, what counts as legitimate, what counts as not worth discussing—this capacity itself is power.

So Power Difference has a very important characteristic:
it does not always appear in the form of coercion,
but it is always connected to boundaries, rules, and obedience.

If Wealth Difference is about “who can get what,”
then Power Difference is closer to “who can decide how others get things, whether they get them at all, and according to
what rules.”

This is also why Power Difference is often harder to see than Wealth Difference.
Wealth can sometimes be recorded on the books; power often hides within procedures, qualifications, permissions,
positions, discourse, and default orders.
Many societies mistake themselves for being “very flat” not because Power Difference is scarce, but because Power
Difference has been sufficiently naturalized through institutionalization.

So this chapter pins down one judgment:

Proposition 6.1: The core of Power Difference is not violence itself, but the unequal capacity to define rules,
boundaries, and the direction of obedience.
III. The Second Form: Wealth Difference
Compared to Power Difference, Wealth Difference is easier to see and easier to quantify.

Definition 6.2: Wealth Difference is the inequality among subjects in the degree to which they possess exchangeable
resources, accumulable resources, and disposable resources.
Money is certainly the most conspicuous manifestation of Wealth Difference.
But wealth is not only cash.
Land, housing, shares, equipment, knowledge, technology, savings, resource access, educational opportunities, mobile
capital, inheritance conditions—all of these are components of Wealth Difference.

Wealth Difference matters not only because it determines “who is richer,” but because it determines:

Who has a wider range of choices
Who can afford to wait longer
Who can more easily defer losses to others
Who is better able to convert present disadvantages into future advantages
Who is more capable of surviving market fluctuations, institutional loopholes, and crises
In other words, the true meaning of Wealth Difference is not the high or low numbers on a ledger, but the difference in
the breadth of one’s life path and action space.

This point is critical.
Because if Wealth Difference is understood only as “income is not the same,” you will underestimate its structural
significance.
What Wealth Difference truly changes is often not consumption capacity at a given point in time, but a subject’s
malleability over time: how big a mistake it can afford, how long it can endure uncertainty, whether it can buy when
others are forced to sell, whether it can stay calm while others are anxious.

So this chapter offers its second judgment:

Proposition 6.2: The deeper significance of Wealth Difference is not different levels of consumption, but the unequal
distribution of actionable space and the capacity to wait among subjects.
This is also why many societies that appear to have “narrowed the income gap” have not actually reduced the structural
consequences of Wealth Difference.
As long as disposable space, risk tolerance, and temporal endurance remain highly unequal, Difference has merely changed
statistical tables without truly shrinking.

IV. The Third Form: Identity Difference
The first two forms are relatively easy to understand. The third is the hardest, because it is most easily made mystical
and most easily dismissed.

Definition 6.3: Identity Difference is the inequality and distinction among subjects at the level of belonging,
legitimacy, recognition, and “who counts as one of us.”
Identity Difference is not the same as Wealth Difference.
It does not necessarily manifest directly as more or fewer resources.
Identity Difference is also not entirely the same as Power Difference.
It does not necessarily manifest directly as who can command whom.

But it determines something else:
Who is counted as “normal,” “legitimate,” “naturally belonging here,” “qualified to speak,” “worthy of protection.”

That is already profound enough.

A person may be wealthy but not necessarily recognized as “one of us.”
A person may hold a position but not possess the deepest cultural legitimacy.
A group may have unremarkable material conditions yet occupy a central position in terms of identity, making it more
easily treated as “the true representative of the nation,” “the orthodox inheritor of tradition,” “the normal template
of society.”
Another group, even after acquiring resources, may still be framed as marginal, exceptional, foreign, suspect, or merely
tolerated.

This is what makes Identity Difference the most intractable.
It is not about “what I have,” but about “what I count as.”
And once “what counts as” enters social structure, it profoundly affects resource distribution, rule design, boundary
demarcation, and the escalation of conflict.

Therefore, this chapter must establish this point:

Proposition 6.3: The core of Identity Difference is not individual subjective feeling, but the structural position of
who is recognized as “legitimately existing.”
This is also why, whenever the discussion later turns to identity conflict, it cannot be resolved through interest
negotiation alone.
Interests can be redistributed,
but once “who counts as one of us,” “who is qualified to be treated equally,” and “whose narrative counts as normal” are
touched, the level of conflict will immediately escalate.

V. Why These Three Forms Matter Most: Because most important conflicts in society circulate among them
Once these three forms of Difference are laid out clearly, many previously blurry social phenomena suddenly come into
focus.

Often, people think they are facing a wealth problem, only to discover later that what was really at stake was power.
Sometimes people think it is a power struggle, only to find that what is truly irreconcilable is identity.
In other cases, a society’s conflict first erupts over identity, but deep underneath lies a long-unaddressed wealth
difference.
In still more complex situations, these three differences do not take turns appearing, but borrow each other’s forms,
cover for each other, and convert into one another.

This is why Volume 2 cannot begin by directly discussing conflict, institutions, markets, and the state.
Because if these three forms are not first established, all subsequent analysis will keep contradicting itself.

So this chapter offers its third key judgment:

Proposition 6.4: Most important conflicts in the social world do not unfold within a single form of Difference; they
continuously convert among Power, Wealth, and Identity Difference.
Once this judgment holds, many later chapters in the book will share a common fulcrum.
The Conservation law works not because there is only one kind of Difference in the world, but because they convert into
one another.
The institutional double-edge holds not because institutions deal with only one kind of problem, but because they often
amplify one kind of Difference while suppressing another.
Identity conflict is terrifying not because it grows out of nothing, but often because other differences have been
pushed and squeezed over a long period, finally clustering, becoming visible, and closing off at the identity level.

VI. Power, Wealth, and Identity Are Not Placed Side by Side—They Have Natural Channels Between Them
At this point, things must be pushed one step further.

These three differences are not three columns in an abstract classification table.
There are very stable, very high-frequency channels of conversion between them.
And many social changes are essentially these channels at work.

The First Channel: Wealth Converts into Power
Money can buy positions, time, information, organizational resources, influence, thresholds, and the right to design
rules.
Once Wealth Difference accumulates to a certain degree, it almost inevitably begins seeking an outlet in power.
Because merely possessing wealth is not stable; what is truly stable is turning wealth into the capacity to “make others
also operate according to your way.”

So Wealth Difference rarely satisfies itself with staying in the market alone.
It has a natural impulse to reach into politics, institutions, public opinion, and organization.

The Second Channel: Power Converts into Wealth
This one is usually easier to see.
Those with power can more easily access resources, gain privileges, set entry thresholds, decide who gets to
participate, and decide who profits.
Therefore, once Power Difference loses its constraints, it quickly generates Wealth Difference.

Why does power always have a rent-seeking impulse?
Because the structural position of power is naturally close to resource distribution outlets.
Without strong constraints, it will almost certainly flow toward wealth along the path of least resistance.

The Third Channel: Both Wealth and Power Convert into Identity
This is the most easily underestimated channel, and also the deepest one.

When wealth accumulates to a certain degree, it ceases to be merely money, and begins to transform into taste, social
circles, family pedigree, educational thresholds, cultural capital, and “who counts as respectable.”
When power accumulates to a certain degree, it too ceases to be merely the capacity to command, and begins to transform
into “who represents orthodoxy,” “who counts as the state itself,” “who is naturally more deserving of obedience.”

So both wealth and power ultimately seek a more stable form: identity-ification.
Because once identity is formed, Difference no longer operates only at the level of interests—it gets written into
people’s intuitions, memories, daily behavior, marriage patterns, language, and moral judgments.
At that point, Difference becomes more stable, deeper, and less easily changed by a single institutional adjustment.

So the fourth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 6.5: Natural channels of conversion exist among Power, Wealth, and Identity Difference, and the most stable
direction is often the continuous precipitation of wealth and power into identity.
This sentence will become very important later.
It reveals that many of the most immovable differences in society did not originally exist in the form of identity; they
are often sediment deposited by wealth and power over long periods of operation.

VII. Why Identity Difference Is the Most Stubborn: Because Once Formed, It Feels Less Like “Having” and More Like
“Being”
Here we must single out Identity Difference and give it slightly more weight.

Wealth Difference makes people feel unequal.
Power Difference makes people feel oppressed.
Identity Difference often makes people feel: that their very way of being has been placed into a hierarchy.

A person who is poor knows what they lack.
A person without power knows what they cannot obtain.
But if a group has been placed in a subordinate position in terms of identity, it faces a problem that is not simply
about “lacking something,” but about “whether I truly belong here at all.”

Once conflict rises to this level, many things that were previously negotiable become much harder to negotiate.
Because while wealth and power also touch on subject boundaries, once Identity Difference is engaged, subjects enter a
self-maintenance state more quickly, prioritizing boundary protection rather than calculating gains first.

So this chapter must lay a foundation in advance for the later chapters on identity conflict:

Proposition 6.6: Identity Difference is stubborn not because it is the most illusory, but because it is most easily
understood by subjects as the boundary of existence itself.
This is why, among all forms of Difference, not every kind triggers the same level of response.
The intensity of Identity Difference often comes from the fact that it touches not distribution, but boundary.

VIII. The Three Differences Have Different Visibility, Which Misleads Social Judgment
There is a very common illusion in society:
visible differences are treated as real problems;
invisible differences are easily treated as “no longer existing.”

Wealth Difference is usually easier to quantify, and therefore easier to enter public discourse.
Power Difference is often more concealed, because it hides in procedures, organizations, permissions, and tacit
structures.
Identity Difference is more complex: sometimes very conspicuous, but sometimes packaged as “tradition,” “common sense,”
“cultural habit,” or “natural order.”

This leads to an extremely important consequence:

A society often does not lack a certain kind of Difference; it is merely that this Difference is temporarily not
conspicuous enough.
For example, Wealth Difference may have been visibly reduced, leading the society to mistakenly believe it has become
more equal;
but at the same time, Power Difference may be becoming more concentrated, and Identity Difference more sensitive.
Or, for instance, open identity conflict may have been suppressed, leading people to believe “this problem has been
solved”;
but in reality, Identity Difference has merely shifted into a more implicit space.

So the fifth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 6.7: The three forms of social Difference have different visibility, and the difference in visibility itself
misleads social judgment about the real structure.
This point will echo repeatedly in what follows.
Many societies oscillate between “thinking the problem is solved” and “the problem suddenly exploding” not because
change is too fast, but because they have long focused only on the most conspicuous differences while ignoring those
that are deeper, more stable, and slower.

IX. Truly Mature Analysis Does Not Ask “Which Difference Is Most Important,” but “How Do They Support Each Other Right
Now”
Many theories like to argue about which is “the most fundamental.”
Some say wealth is most fundamental.
Some say power is most fundamental.
Some say identity is most fundamental.
These debates are not meaningless, but if analysis stops at this level, it often narrows.

The attitude of the Theory of Difference here is closer to:

Do not rush to ask which is permanently most important; first ask: at this moment, in this structure, in this society,
which Difference is most visible, which is deepest, and which is supplying energy to which.
In some periods, Wealth Difference is in the foreground, Power Difference provides cover from backstage, and Identity
Difference handles legitimization.
In some periods, Identity Difference surges to the foreground, Wealth Difference recedes into the background, and Power
Difference reshapes rules from behind.
In other times, Power Difference first suppresses everything, only to force both wealth and identity issues out
together.

So truly mature analysis does not begin by choosing sides, but by seeing how the three currently form an integrated
structure.

Therefore, this chapter offers its sixth judgment:

Proposition 6.8: The three differences have no fixed hierarchy; their importance depends on how they support, mask, and
convert into one another within a specific structure.
This is what truly opens Volume 2.
Because from here, we no longer see society as a chaotic mass of “lots of differences,” but begin to see its skeleton:

Most important problems in the social world can actually be understood as the manifestation and conversion of Power,
Wealth, and Identity Difference at different stages and in different positions.

X. The Landing Point of This Chapter: From Here, the Social World Begins to Have Main Coordinates
Volume 2 necessarily starts here.

Without this chapter, the later discussions of Conservation, Conflict, Institutions, Markets, and the State would keep
tangling their lines.
With this chapter, the entire book gains a primary coordinate system.

From now on, when the book encounters a social phenomenon, it will first ask:

Which form of Difference is primarily visible here?
Are the other two forms supporting it, being concealed by it, or being converted by it?
What Difference does this society think it is solving, and where has it actually pushed the Difference?
Is the currently most visible problem the same kind of Difference as the deeper structural problem?
This is the true function of this chapter.
It is not playing a classification game; it is establishing coordinates for all subsequent analysis.

Starting from the next chapter, we will no longer simply say “there are three kinds of Difference,” but will begin to
ask:

Since the three forms of Difference convert into one another, why don’t they disappear? And why does this conversion so
often come with new conflict, closure, and reflux?
This brings us to the true central axis of Volume 2: Social Conservation and Difference Conversion.

Chapter 7: Social Difference Conservation and Conversion
I. The real difficulty is not whether Difference will cause problems, but why Difference always manages to come back in
a different guise
It is at this chapter that Volume 2 truly enters its central axis.

The previous chapter distinguished the three most critical forms of Difference in the social world: Power Difference,
Wealth Difference, and Identity Difference. The purpose of doing so was not to label reality, but to prevent all
subsequent analysis from collapsing into a single undifferentiated mass.
Because if the main forms of Difference are not first separated, all later judgments about institutions, reform,
conflict, and governance will easily amount to explaining one kind of Difference with another.

But classification is only the first step. The truly difficult problems lie ahead.

Can a society compress Wealth Difference? Of course it can.
Can an institution weaken overt Identity Difference? Of course it can.
Can a power structure be recut through reform, revolution, redistribution, or rule-of-law arrangements? It can.

The problem is not whether things can be moved.
The problem is: after one form of Difference is pushed down, why do we always see it reappear somewhere else, or see
some new Difference of highly similar structure growing back?

This is not merely a lament, but one of the most stubborn empirical facts in all of social science.

The old aristocracy is overthrown, and a new elite forms.
Public monopolies of power are dismantled, and new covert control networks emerge.
The wealth gap narrows, and identity boundaries become sharper.
Overt hierarchical systems are abolished, and qualification thresholds, cultural capital, and social-circle
stratification become harder to touch.
One rule flattens a problem, and the problem grows back through another seam.

If this happened only occasionally, one could call it a governance failure.
If it happens again and again, it can no longer be treated as mere coincidence.

So this chapter must explicitly establish one judgment:

Social Difference does not automatically disappear because of compression along one dimension; its more common fate is
to undergo conversion along other dimensions, other positions, and other channels.
This is the problem of Conservation and Conversion at the social scale.

II. Social Conservation does not mean there is always the same amount of inequality in the world—it means Difference
cannot be zeroed out for free
Before proceeding further, “Conservation” must once more be precisely stated.

Because once this book speaks of Conservation, it can easily be misheard as a crude fatalism:
as if the total amount of Difference is fixed, all reform efforts are futile, and every attempt at equality is wasted.

That is not what this book means—and precisely the opposite.

What this book calls Conservation is not “all inequality is permanently fixed.”
Nor is it “no effort can improve society.”
Nor is it “compressing one form of Difference is always meaningless.”

What this book truly says is:

Definition 7.1: Social Difference Conservation means that Difference cannot be zeroed out without cost; any significant
compression tends to be accompanied by transfer, reorganization, or new clustering.
There are three key points in this definition.

First, what Conservation constrains is not “specific forms,” but “Difference itself does not simply vanish.”
Second, Conservation does not deny improvement; it denies “the fantasy of costless improvement.”
Third, Conservation does not mean doing nothing; it means that any truly effective governance must care about where
Difference is pushed to, not merely whether it looks smaller right in front of you.

So Social Conservation is not a splash of cold water.
It is more like a warning:
Do not mistake “things look flat now” for “the underlying Difference is gone.”

If you ignore this warning, you will almost certainly be struck by the reflux later.

III. Conversion Is the Everyday Form of Conservation in the Social World
If Difference cannot simply vanish, then what is its most common fate?

Not freezing in place.
Not permanently remaining the same.
But conversion.

Definition 7.2: Conversion is the process by which Difference, while maintaining its structural tension, moves from one
form, position, or mode of manifestation into another form, position, or mode of manifestation.
This sounds abstract, but it is actually very concrete.

Wealth converting into power is conversion.
Power converting into identity is conversion.
Open conflict converting into underground non-cooperation is conversion.
On-the-books risk in a market converting into off-the-books implicit risk is conversion.
Overt exclusion converting into gentler, more polite, but harder-to-grasp boundary filtering is also conversion.

This shows that in the social world, Conservation rarely manifests as “look, it is still right there, unchanged.”
Conservation more often manifests as: Difference has changed face, changed shell, changed position, but it continues to
operate.

So the first core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 7.1: Conversion is not an exception to Conservation, but its normal form in the social world.
This sentence is very important.
Because once accepted, many previously scattered phenomena begin to connect.
You will no longer see institutional loopholes, identity rebounds, organizational internal friction, shadow markets, and
cultural backlash as independent problems, but will begin to realize: they are often just the continued movement of
Difference in different locations.

IV. The Four Most Common High-Frequency Conversion Channels in the Social World
One risk of misrecognition is that, while the Conservation law has force, it can easily be stated too broadly, as if
anything can be stuffed into “conversion.”
Here, this book tightens the aperture: rather than saying vaguely that “it always converts,” it explicitly identifies
the channels that are most common, most stable, and most worth observing first.

The First Channel: Overt Difference Transfers into Covert Difference
This is the most common channel.

If a society suppresses open conflict, what tends to emerge is not the complete disappearance of conflict, but conflict
transferring into places that are harder to measure and harder to govern.
On the surface, things are quiet; underground, things grow.
On the surface, there is unity; behind the scenes, there is increasing division.
Public discourse has been smoothed out, and genuine tension begins accumulating within organizations, informal networks,
daily interactions, and memory structures.

This is why many systems that appear increasingly tidy on the surface are increasingly brittle inside.
Not because they lack Difference, but because overt expression of Difference has been compressed and channeled into
covert space.

The Second Channel: Wealth Difference Converts into Power Difference
When direct appropriation cannot easily be legitimized, what wealth most often does is not disappear, but seek pathways
to control rules, organizations, and entry thresholds.
It transforms into educational thresholds, credentialing systems, institutional capture, agenda-setting, organizational
influence, and rule-design capacity.
Once wealth begins converting into “who is qualified to make the rules,” it is no longer merely about being rich—it is
attempting to become the permission for others to live according to its way.

The Third Channel: Power Difference Converts into Wealth Difference
This channel is equally frequent.
Once power lacks constraints, it tends to exploit its structural proximity to resource outlets, approval outlets,
information outlets, and discretion outlets, turning control into profit distribution.
So a situation that originally looked like “just a power imbalance” quickly sprouts material accumulation and
distribution skew.

The Fourth Channel: Economic Difference Precipitates into Identity Difference
This is the slowest and deepest channel.

If economic difference exists stably over a long period, it ceases to be merely about income levels and gradually embeds
itself in lifestyles, educational trajectories, marriage circles, cultural taste, linguistic habits, and judgments about
“who counts as respectable.”
At this point, Difference begins to be identity-ified.
Once identity-ified, it is no longer just about “who gets a bit more or less,” but about “who is naturally more like the
center, and who is naturally more like the periphery.”

So the second key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 7.2: Social Difference Conservation most commonly manifests through four high-frequency channels: overt to
covert, wealth to power, power to wealth, and economic precipitation into identity.
These four channels are not exhaustive, but they are sufficient to form the primary working framework for Volume 2.
The subsequent analysis of institutions, identity, markets, and international relations will all continuously unfold
around these four channels.

V. Why Conversion Does Not Occur Evenly: Because Difference Always Follows the Path of Least Resistance
Volume 1, Chapter 4 already established this principle. This chapter must advance it into a genuinely usable tool for
social analysis.

Once Difference is blocked at its original position, it does not scatter evenly in all directions.
It more commonly follows several distinctive directions:

Places less subject to formal rule supervision
Places easier to legitimize
Places harder to measure and record
Places more consistent with old structural inertia
Places closer to subjects’ instinctive defenses
That is to say, Difference conversion is not random wandering—it flows preferentially along low-resistance channels in
society.

So the third core judgment can be stated as:

Proposition 7.3: Social Difference conversion is directional; it occurs preferentially along channels of low resistance,
low visibility, low constraint, and low cost.
Once this sentence holds, the Conservation law truly transforms from a “grand principle” into an analytical tool.

When you see a certain Difference being suppressed, you cannot merely ask “is it still there?”—you must immediately
pursue:

Where is it most easily redirected?
Which position has the lowest resistance?
Which form can most readily absorb it?
Which space is hardest to see immediately?
What form does the current institutional setup most readily allow it to regrow in?
This is where the Theory of Difference is most practical.
It does not give you answers on your behalf, but it can rapidly narrow the problem.

VI. Why Conversion Leads to Misjudgment: Because New Differences Are Usually Seen Later Than Old Ones
The reason Conservation and Conversion are consistently underestimated in reality is not that they are too complex, but
that they always exploit a time lag.

When old Difference is compressed, the effect is usually immediately visible.
When new Difference forms, it tends to be slower, more hidden, more dispersed.
So for a period, the entire society believes: there are fewer problems.

This is the source of misjudgment.

The public wealth gap narrows, and people think there is more equality;
years later, they discover that educational stratification, social-circle closure, and qualification thresholds have
hardened.
Public identity expression is unified, and people think the community is more cohesive;
later, they discover that private boundary awareness has grown stronger and resentment deeper.
The financial system looks cleaner on the surface, and people think the risk is smaller;
still later, they discover that risk has merely moved from within the regulatory ledger into darker positions.

Therefore, this chapter must nail down one more point:

Proposition 7.4: Social Conservation most commonly creates illusions through time lag—the old Difference is seen
shrinking first, and the new Difference appears with delayed visibility.
This is why many reforms look like successes in their first few years.
Not because they are fake, but because the other half of Conservation typically appears later.
And once it does appear later, people tend to treat it as a separate problem, forgetting that it and the earlier
compression belong to the same chain.

So truly rigorous social analysis cannot merely observe what happens on the surface when a policy is first implemented.
It must pursue: where have these differences been pushed after three years, ten years, a generation?

VII. Why Conservation Is Not the Same as Conservatism: Because What Matters Is Not Whether Difference Exists, but Where
It Is Placed
At this point, the most common misreading must be actively blocked.

Some people, upon hearing Conservation, will say:
Does that mean all reform is useless?
If Difference always comes back, why bother?

This is precisely a failure to understand.

What is truly important about Conservation is not telling you “don’t move,” but forcing you to acknowledge:
The problem has never been whether Difference will exist, but where Difference is placed, in what form it exists, who
bears it, whether it is mobile, whether it is visible, and whether it can be corrected.

This is where politics and institutions can truly make a difference.

The permanent existence of Difference does not mean all differences are the same.
Open, debatable, adjustable Difference is not the same as covert, frozen, identity-ified, unnegotiable Difference.
Mobile Wealth Difference is not the same as Difference that has precipitated into identity fate.
Power Difference that can be constrained by rule of law is not the same as power difference that has fully permeated the
capillaries of society.

So Conservation does not crush reform.
On the contrary, it sets a higher standard for reform:

Proposition 7.5: The key to judging whether a reform succeeds is not whether it “eliminates Difference,” but whether it
directs Difference toward more visible, more mobile, and more correctable forms.
This is the true political implication of the Theory of Difference.
Not “nothing can be changed,” but “do not hide Difference in more dangerous places and imagine you have completed
governance.”

VIII. Minimal Variation and Nearest Equilibrium: Why Difference, After Conversion, Always Stops First in Some Familiar
Structure
Conservation and Conversion themselves still have not explained the most troublesome point.

After Difference shifts, why does society often not open up an entirely new configuration, but instead fall back into
some familiar structure?
Why do many reforms end up looking like “new shell, same core”?
Why does revolution so often produce new concentration?
Why, when institutions fail, do old networks, old habits, old narratives, and old boundaries always take over first?

Here, this book must formally integrate “Minimal Variation—Nearest Equilibrium” into the central axis of Volume 2.

Once Difference is pushed out of its old position, it certainly seeks a new resting place.
But it will not arbitrarily pick an ideal point in infinite space.
It typically, following the principle of minimal variation, first goes to places that:

Require the least wholesale reconstruction
Connect most easily to the old order
Are most readily understood and exploited by existing subjects
Can most quickly form a new balance
That is to say, it first lands on a Nearest Equilibrium.

So the sixth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 7.6: The first landing point after Difference conversion is usually not the optimal solution, but the
arrangement closest to the old structure and most easily capable of maintaining temporary stability.
This judgment will greatly enhance the explanatory power of the rest of the volume.

Why does redistribution sometimes become a new expansion of administrative power?
Because after Difference is pushed out of the wealth dimension, what can first stabilize the situation is
reconcentration in the power dimension.
Why does suppressing public identity expression turn into more private, more closed circle communities?
Because that is the Nearest Equilibrium.
Why, after high-risk market products are shut down, does capital quickly chase another type of product that looks safer
but has a similar structure?
Because capital is not seeking the safest future—it is seeking the nearest equilibrium that still functions.

This transforms Conservation and Conversion from a generalization that “it always comes back” into something that can
further answer: after it comes back, where does it first stop?

IX. Once Conversion Is Complete, the New Difference Does Not Simply Replace the Old—It Brings New Structural
Consequences
One more step must be taken here.

Many people, when speaking of conversion, tend to think of simple substitution:
as if the old Difference steps down and the new Difference steps up, like a shift change.

Reality is not that clean.

Once a new Difference forms, it brings new organizational methods, new legitimizing narratives, new defense mechanisms,
new blind spots.
That is to say, conversion is not “doing the same thing from a different position”—it generates new structural
consequences.

Once Wealth Difference precipitates into Identity Difference, the problem becomes harder to negotiate than before,
because it is no longer only at the distribution level but has entered the boundary level.
Once Power Difference converts into institutionalized procedures, violence may decrease, but opacity and the difficulty
of correction may increase.
Once open conflict transfers into covert space, surface order may strengthen, but the system’s diagnosability drops
sharply.

So the seventh judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 7.7: Difference conversion is not simple substitution—it generates new structural consequences that change
the level of conflict, the difficulty of governance, and the mode of imbalance.
This is also why the Theory of Difference must consistently maintain the perspective of “tracking conversion
consequences.”
You cannot assume that because the old problem is no longer in its original place, the matter is settled.
You must observe what the new Difference, once it grows out, turns the situation into.

X. The Landing Point of This Chapter: From Here, Social Problems Are No Longer Understood as “Whether They Exist,” but
as “Where, in What Form, and on Which Equilibrium They Rest”
At this point, the central axis of Volume 2 is established.

The previous chapter gave three basic forms of Difference.
This chapter has put them into motion:
Conservation shows they cannot be zeroed out without cost;
Conversion shows they continuously flow between different forms;
Low-resistance channels show where they preferentially go;
Nearest Equilibrium shows where they first stop after conversion;
And new structural consequences show that conversion is never a purely neutral relocation—it rewrites the nature of
subsequent problems.

So, from this chapter onward, social problems are no longer simply “does this problem exist,” but become sharper
questions:

What form does this Difference currently primarily take?
Has it just been suppressed, or has transfer already begun?
Is the most visible problem the original one, or a new form after conversion?
Which Nearest Equilibrium is the system currently sliding toward?
Is this equilibrium buffering imbalance, or merely pushing a larger imbalance further into the future?
This is where the Theory of Difference truly begins to work.

The next chapter will push this central axis further:

If Difference is conserved, converts, and slides toward the Nearest Equilibrium, how do major social changes actually
occur? Why are some ruptures absorbed locally while others cascade and amplify?
This leads to the discrete Event structure of change.

Chapter Echo
Remember the five most important sentences from this chapter.

The first: Social Conservation does not mean Difference is unchanging—it means Difference cannot be zeroed out without
cost.
The second: Conversion is not an exception to Conservation, but its normal form in the social world.
The third: Social Difference most commonly manifests through four high-frequency channels—overt to covert, wealth to
power, power to wealth, and economic precipitation into identity.
The fourth: The first landing point after Difference conversion is usually not the optimal solution, but the nearest
arrangement that can be stabilized.
The fifth: The key to judging whether reform succeeds is not whether it “eliminates Difference,” but whether it directs
Difference toward more visible, more mobile, and more correctable forms.
The entire rest of this volume will unfold forward from these five sentences.

Chapter 8: The Discrete Event Structure of Change
(The Theory of Difference, Volume 2, Chapter 8)

I. What truly changes structure is usually not the trend itself, but the moment when the trend is cut open
At this chapter, Volume 2 finally enters change itself.

The previous chapter established the central axis: Social Difference cannot be zeroed out without cost; it transfers,
precipitates, flows along low-resistance channels, and after transfer usually first falls into the nearest arrangement
that can be stabilized.
But this framework still lacks the most critical piece: in what form does change actually occur.

Because without answering this question, Conservation, Conversion, and Nearest Equilibrium are all just structural
analysis of “after the change.”
You have not yet truly explained: why do some social changes ferment slowly while others burst open suddenly?
Why can we always see the accumulation of causes after the fact, yet still feel “why now” and “how so fast” when it
actually happens?
Why do many situations look precarious for a long time without collapsing, while others that seem to hold suddenly lose
their shape at some point?

This shows that social change has not only a content problem, but also a form problem.
The same accumulation of Difference is sometimes absorbed locally and adjusted slowly; at other times, it is amplified
by some Event and rapidly cascades along organizational chains, psychological chains, institutional chains, and
narrative chains.

So what this chapter needs to solve is not “why change happens,” but:

Why does social change so often flow forward not continuously, but cut through old structures and rewrite new paths by
means of discrete Events?
II. Change Is Not Without Process, but Process Does Not Automatically Rewrite Structure
First, let us clarify what is most easily confused.

This book does not deny process.
On the contrary, the preceding chapters have consistently emphasized slow processes: accumulation, compression,
clustering, precipitation, reflux.
Without these processes, many later-exploding ruptures would never occur.

But this does not mean that process itself has already completed the transformation.

An institution can gradually become more and more rigid.
A society can gradually become more and more unequal.
A community can gradually diverge more and more in memory and narrative.
A market can gradually accumulate higher leverage and more fragile trust.
All of these are real processes.

The question is: whether they will one day actually rewrite structure is not something process itself can guarantee.

Many problems accumulate without exploding.
Many grievances rise without producing action.
Many assets remain overvalued without collapsing.
Many regimes hollow out while maintaining surface stability.
Many communities keep fracturing without truly breaking.

This reveals a very critical fact:

Proposition 8.1: Process changes conditions, but conditions themselves do not automatically equal structural change.
When structural change truly occurs, it usually requires something more discrete.
That is to say, a point where something “happens.”

This point is the Event.

III. What Is an Event: Not Something Newsworthy, but Something That Rewrites Subsequent Paths
Here we formally redefine “Event.”

In everyday language, Event often equals a big deal.
In news logic, Event equals something worth reporting, sharing, remembering.
This is not wrong, but for social theory it is insufficient.

Definition 8.1: An Event is a discrete occurrence that causes a significant change in the conditions of the system’s
subsequent path.
The most important part of this definition is not “big,” but “rewriting subsequent path conditions.”

That is to say, something becomes an Event not because of how sensational it is, but because after it, many paths that
were still open no longer remain open in the same way; many previously marginal paths are suddenly pushed to the front;
many problems that could be delayed can suddenly no longer be delayed.

Defining Event this way has two advantages.

First, it pulls “Event” back from being a purely journalistic phenomenon to a structural phenomenon.
Second, it allows us to include in Event analysis some occurrences that appear small but genuinely rewrite subsequent
paths.

A government official’s slip of the tongue may be just a slip;
but if it causes the public to suddenly believe “the boundary has opened” and triggers cascading action, it becomes an
Event.
An asset price plunge may be just a fluctuation;
but if it renders existing liquidity arrangements ineffective, breaks the trust chain, and causes large numbers of
subjects to simultaneously change behavior, it is an Event.
A small-scale conflict in some locality may originally be just a local friction;
but if it re-nails identity boundaries and forces previously suppressible Differences into full visibility, it becomes
an Event.

So the first core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 8.2: The definition of an Event does not depend on how spectacular it is, but on whether it rewrites
subsequent path conditions.
IV. Why Social Change Often Has a Discrete Character: Because Structure Is Not Smooth Putty, but Has Thresholds,
Lock-ins, and Obedience Chains
If social change frequently has an Event-like character, then we must answer: why is it not smooth and continuous?

This book gives a more constrained answer here: because social structure contains three things that compress slow
processes into discrete breakpoints.

First, Thresholds
Many structures do not degrade and collapse bit by bit.
They often remain viable within a certain range, but once they cross a line, their nature changes.

This line can be a psychological threshold.
For instance, public long-term dissatisfaction does not automatically produce uprising—until some humiliating Event,
some gunshot, or some symbol is touched, and the previous endurance structure is suddenly severed.

It can also be an institutional threshold.
For instance, a bank routinely absorbs withdrawal pressure, but as long as the run does not cross the liquidity
threshold, it can still pretend to be stable.
Once the threshold is crossed, old rules and soothing rhetoric are no longer sufficient.

It can also be a narrative threshold.
A society maintains a certain official narrative over a long period, even if people do not fully believe it in their
hearts;
but once some Event makes the feeling that “even pretending is no longer possible” spread, the narrative is no longer
the old narrative.

So structures change suddenly not because there were no slow processes beforehand, but because the slow processes were
approaching the threshold, and once the line is crossed, it is no longer the same situation.

Second, Lock-ins
Many systems resist change for a long time not because there is no problem, but because old paths are locked in.

Institutional lock-in.
Interest lock-in.
Cognitive lock-in.
Narrative lock-in.
Organizational lock-in.
These lock-ins allow many Differences to be changing, but the changes cannot immediately penetrate to the surface
structure.

But once the locking device malfunctions, the accumulated changes will suddenly burst out.
So lock-ins delay change and simultaneously amplify its discreteness.

Third, Obedience Chains
Social structure is not a plane of uniform force distribution—it contains numerous obedience chains:
superior–subordinate, center–periphery, leader–executor, platform–user, state–citizen, core narrative–peripheral
identity.

These chains ordinarily maintain structural stability, but they also make the structure highly sensitive to certain key
nodes.
Once a key node fails, change does not spread evenly—it travels rapidly along the chains.

So the second judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 8.3: Social change frequently manifests as discrete Events because slow processes, under the combined action
of thresholds, lock-ins, and obedience chains, are compressed into structural breakpoints.
This sentence will connect everything that follows: revolution, collapse, institutional disintegration, identity
escalation, and alliance rupture.

V. Discrete Does Not Mean Accidental: It Only Means “When It Happens” Cannot Be Smoothly Extrapolated
When Events are discussed, people easily swing to another extreme:
if it is an Event, does that mean everything depends on luck, coincidence, and unpredictable triggers?

Not exactly.

Discrete does not mean lacking structure.
It only means: you cannot use a smooth curve to extrapolate precisely “when and at what specific point it will happen.”

That is to say, structure can tell you:

Where tension is accumulating
Which paths are narrowing
Which boundaries are more brittle
Which lock-ins are loosening
Which equilibria are becoming harder to maintain
But structure cannot always tell you:
whether it will be this afternoon or next month;
whether it will be this slip of the tongue or that gunshot;
whether this institution will fall first or that one.

So the third key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 8.4: Discreteness does not deny structural constraints; it denies only one illusion—the belief that major
change can be precisely forecast by continuous extrapolation.
This is important for methodology.
It distinguishes “cannot precisely predict Events” from “can still analyze structural risk.”

You cannot always know in advance which straw will break the camel’s back.
But you can know how far the camel has already bent.
You cannot always know which day the market will break.
But you can know how far trust, leverage, and liquidity have been pushed.
You cannot always know which conflict will become a full identity escalation.
But you can know how far boundaries, memory, and narrative have been squeezed.

This is the Theory of Difference’s attitude toward “prediction”:
not precise prophecy of Events, but recognition of whether the system is approaching a discrete breakpoint.

VI. Three Basic Types of Events: Power Events, Wealth Events, Identity Events
To give the rest of the book a unified vocabulary, Events must also be classified here.
Otherwise “Event” will easily become another undifferentiated mass.

Version 1.6 offers three basic types, corresponding to the three main forms of Difference from the previous chapter.

The First Type: Power Events
Power Events are Events that directly rewrite obedience chains, rule distribution, and who is qualified to define the
situation.
Examples include coups, revolutions, election reversals, major constitutional rewrites, sovereign ruptures, and internal
leadership reorganizations.

The consequences of such Events are usually not “who got richer,” but “who decides” has changed.
What makes Power Events most potent is that they rapidly rewrite the distributional premises of all subsequent
Differences.
Because who holds power almost immediately determines which conversion channels will open and which will be blocked.

The Second Type: Wealth Events
Wealth Events are Events that directly rewrite resource prices, ownership structures, risk-tolerance chains, and
wait-capacity space.
Examples include financial crises, currency collapses, hyperinflation, large-scale asset bubble bursts, sudden
land-system changes, industrial base ruptures, and technology shocks causing asset revaluation.

The consequences of such Events usually first manifest as “who can still hold on, who falls first, who can still wait,
who is forced to act.”
Wealth Events rapidly change the temporal structure of many subjects:
those who could wait begin to be unable to wait,
those who dared take risks now seek only to preserve,
those who had no qualification to take over suddenly gain the opportunity.

The Third Type: Identity Events
Identity Events are Events that directly rewrite “who counts as one of us, who counts as marginal, who is qualified to
be recognized.”
Examples include declarations of independence, territorial changes, language-status shifts, religious-order rewrites,
repositioning of some symbol, large-scale racial or ethnic conflict escalation, and national-memory rewrites.

What is most distinctive about Identity Events is that they change not only distribution but also subjects’
understanding of “who we are.”
Once this level is touched, many things that could previously be treated as interest problems are re-elevated to
boundary problems.

So the fourth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 8.5: Discrete events in society can usually be classified as Power Events, Wealth Events, and Identity
Events; different types of Events first rewrite different Difference structures.
These three types of course frequently mix.
Many major Events are not purely one type.
Revolutions are often simultaneously Power, Wealth, and Identity Events.
Financial crises frequently trigger identity politics.
Territorial conflicts often conceal resource and power issues.

But classification still has value, because it helps us judge:
which structural main line an Event first cuts, and from which direction the chain reactions are most likely to proceed.

VII. Why Some Events Cause Only Local Ripples While Others Cascade and Amplify
This is also key to understanding historical ruptures and systemic collapses.

Not every Event becomes a turning point.
Some Events merely ripple through the old structure and are quickly absorbed.
Others, like removing a load-bearing beam, trigger chain collapses.

The difference is not how “big” the Event looks, but what position it strikes.

First, does it strike a key connection point?
In any system, there are positions that connect multiple Difference channels, multiple obedience chains, multiple
narrative layers.
These points may appear inconspicuous ordinarily, but once severed, the consequences are enormous.
Such positions typically include:

Authority trust nodes
Liquidity nodes
Identity boundary nodes
Organizational coordination nodes
Narrative credibility nodes
If an Event strikes these points, it is more likely to cascade.

Second, does it occur when the system is already near a discrete threshold?
The same Event, occurring at different times, can have completely different consequences.
An ordinary misspoken word may be just a mistake;
when the system is already at maximum fragility, it may become the igniter.

This is not because the word has more force, but because the system is already saturated with combustible material.

Third, does the system still have a Nearest Equilibrium capable of absorbing the shock?
If a system still has multiple viable equilibrium options, then after an Event, the system can more easily reorganize
locally rather than collapse entirely.
If a system has already locked itself in, with only a narrow maintenance path remaining, then any Event will more easily
evolve into a full rupture.

So the fifth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 8.6: Whether an Event cascades and amplifies does not depend on its magnitude, but on whether it strikes a
key connection point, whether it occurs at a moment of high tension, and whether the system still retains a Nearest
Equilibrium capable of absorbing the shock.
This proposition will be very practical in later institutional analysis, financial crises, and international politics.
It can explain why some events that do not seem particularly significant end up being history’s true incision.

VIII. The Paradox of Gradual Reform: It Wants to Avoid Events, but Is Itself Producing Events
This point must be addressed separately because it is so easily understated.

Many reformers want to do one thing:
prevent change from becoming a violent rupture, and instead gradually carry the system forward through incremental
adjustment.
This impulse is entirely understandable.
Compared to revolution, collapse, and full-scale conflict, gradual reform appears gentler, more rational, and less
costly.

But the problem is: gradual reform is not “without Events.”
On the contrary, gradual reform often attempts to replace one uncontrollable major Event with a series of controllable
minor Events.

Every policy adjustment is an Event.
Every rule rewrite is an Event.
Every redistribution of interests is an Event.
It is just that if these Events are spread out, buffered, and locally absorbed, they are less likely to cascade.

So the key to gradual reform is not “no Events,” but:

Whether Events are spread out
Whether there is absorption space
Whether the system is given enough time to form new Nearest Equilibria
Whether new equilibria are gradually laid down before the old one fully fails
So the sixth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 8.7: Gradual reform is not without Events; it attempts to avoid cascading loss of control from a single
Event by dispersing Event density, expanding buffer space, and building Nearest Equilibria in advance.
This is also why some reforms succeed and others fail.
Not because they reform more or less, but because they handle “Event density” and “equilibrium succession” well or
poorly.

If a reform simultaneously loosens the old order while failing to build a new equilibrium in time, it is not gradual—it
is throwing the system into the air.
And where it falls is usually not the ideal new order, but the hardest, nearest, first-available old-style arrangement.

IX. Events Do Not Exist in Isolation—They Always Rewrite “What Becomes Easier and What Becomes Harder Afterward”
At this point, the true significance of Events can be compressed even further.

The most important thing about an Event is not how big it was at that moment.
It is what cost structures it changes for various paths going forward.

After an election defeat, a certain reform path may still be “theoretically possible,” but its political cost has
suddenly risen to near-prohibitive.
After a currency crisis, market participants may still “theoretically trust the central bank,” but the cost of restoring
that trust is no longer what it was.
After a conflict escalates, both sides may still “theoretically reconcile,” but the cost of repairing social psychology
and identity boundaries has surged.
After an alliance betrayal, cooperation is certainly not absolutely impossible, but the default premises of the previous
cooperation have already vanished.

So the final core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 8.8: What an Event truly changes is not just the outcome, but the entry cost of various paths going forward.
This sentence is important because it turns “path closure” from an abstract phrase into a more understandable real
structure.
It is not that the door has been mysteriously shut; it is that the door may still be there, but the cost of pushing it
open has become completely different.

And precisely because of this, history appears irreversible.
Not because the world cannot operate in reverse,
but because after an Event, reverse operation no longer sits within the original cost structure.

X. The Landing Point of This Chapter: From Here, Change Is Understood as “History Organized by Events” Rather Than “Flow
Naturally Unfolded by Trends”
With this chapter complete, the framework of Volume 2 tightens another layer.

The previous chapter said that Difference does not simply vanish—it is conserved, converts, and slides toward Nearest
Equilibria.
This chapter completes the other half: these conversions and rearrangements do not always occur in smooth flow—they
frequently must pass through Events.
Events close doors, change costs, cut open old structures, amplify certain conversions, and shatter certain equilibria.

So, from this chapter onward, this book’s understanding of change becomes very clear:

Slow processes accumulate conditions
Difference transfers and compresses within structure
The system maintains itself through Nearest Equilibria
When thresholds, lock-ins, and obedience chains push pressure to a certain point
Some Event cuts open the structure
Afterward, the cost structure of paths has already changed
This is why genuine social change always appears to “happen suddenly,” and yet afterward one can always trace a long
string of causes that already existed.
Because the causes were always there, but what actually cut open the old structure was still the Event.

The next chapter will push forward along this line:

If Difference is conserved and converts, and change cuts through structure via Events, then do the narratives, theories,
and beliefs about society themselves participate in this process?
This leads to one of the sharpest parts of Volume 2: the recursion of narrative and reality.

Chapter 9: The Recursion of Narrative and Reality
(The Theory of Difference, Volume 2, Chapter 9, Official Revised Draft)

I. The most distinctive thing about the social world is not that people can speak, but that what is spoken can turn back
and change the world
The preceding chapters have tightened one line increasingly close.

Chapter 6 distinguished three basic forms of Difference in society: Power, Wealth, Identity.
Chapter 7 showed that these Differences cannot be zeroed out without cost—they are conserved, convert, and slide along
low-resistance channels toward new equilibria.
Chapter 8 further pointed out that change does not unfold smoothly; many changes that truly rewrite structure are cut
open by Events.

At this point, the framework can already explain many things.
But it still lacks the most critical step.
Without this step, the entire theory will remain at the level of “how Difference moves” and will not truly enter the
core of the social world.

This step is: Narrative.

Because the social world has a very deep distinction from the natural world.
The natural world certainly also has structure, constraints, change, and interaction.
But it does not change its own behavior because it has been described.
An apple will not fall differently because you wrote a book about gravity.
Electrons will not alter their orbits because humans reinterpreted them.

The social world is different.

Subjects in society listen, remember, retell, believe, imitate, refuse, and change behavior around a single sentence.
So statements, explanations, slogans, theories, news, labels, historical memories, and future imaginings about society
are not merely “descriptions.”
They flow back into reality itself, changing how subjects perceive Difference, organize boundaries, judge risk, accept
obedience, and decide on action.

So Volume 2 must, at this point, establish this:

Proposition 9.1: In the social world, narrative is not a footnote outside reality, but part of reality’s own movement.
Once this sentence holds, many things that previously looked like “ideological appendages” immediately gain weight.
Because they are no longer merely “explanations added afterward,” but forces that directly affect how Difference becomes
visible, clusters, is compressed, and cascades.

II. What Is Narrative: Not Storytelling, but Organizing Reality’s Emphases, Boundaries, and Causality
The word “narrative” is used too loosely today.
Often, it has almost become shorthand for “things other people say that I don’t like.”
To prevent it from continuing to float, it must first be made more concrete.

Definition 9.1: Narrative is the organized expression by subjects of Difference, boundaries, causality, and direction in
reality.
This definition contains four key words.

First: Difference.
Narrative does not create its objects from nothing. It first seizes certain Differences from reality: who differs from
whom, where things are unequal, who is harmed, who benefits, who belongs here, who is excluded.

Second: Boundaries.
Narrative does not spread everything out evenly. It assigns emphasis.
It tells you: here is inside, there is outside; here is normal, there is exception; here is worth attention, there can
be ignored.

Third: Causality.
Narrative does not merely say “what happened”—it also arranges “why it happened.”
Who caused it, where the problem lies, who is responsible, who is the victim, what should be handled first, what can
wait.

Fourth: Direction.
Narrative rarely stops at the present.
It always subtly tells you where the situation should go, what counts as restoring normalcy, what counts as better, what
counts as regression, what counts as a way out.

So narrative is not casual talk.
It is one of the organizing modes of reality.
It determines what a society sees, ignores, fears, and expects.

So the first core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 9.2: Narrative is not an auxiliary explanation of reality, but the organizing mode for Difference,
boundaries, causality, and direction within reality.
III. Why Narrative Has Force: Because Subjects Do Not Face Reality Directly, but Face Reality Through Explanation
If subjects faced reality directly and without mediation, narrative’s influence would naturally be limited.
The problem is that subjects almost never live this way.

No person faces the world bare-handed, without language, without categories, without historical memory.
They always already stand within some language, within some label, within some classification taught to them by others.
They know what counts as success, what counts as failure, who is one of us, who is dangerous, what counts as shameful,
what counts as being offended, what counts as order, what counts as abnormal—all of these are not merely the result of
“seeing,” but the result of interpretation.

So the force of narrative emerges.

It does not directly change rocks and trees.
It changes:
how subjects perceive Difference,
how they rank Difference,
which Differences they decide are worth acting on,
which boundaries they judge must be defended,
how they understand what a particular Event actually means.

In other words, narrative changes not all of reality, but the entrance through which subjects enter reality.

This is why the same set of objective changes can lead to completely different social consequences under different
narratives.

Wage stagnation may be narrated as “a temporary fluctuation in the market cycle”;
it may also be narrated as “capital’s long-term extraction of labor”;
it may also be narrated as “foreigners taking away locals’ opportunities”;
or it may be narrated as “the state no longer protecting its true people.”

Why is the consequence completely different from the same economic fact?
Because subjects do not first see the fact and then casually add a layer of interpretation;
they enter the fact through a narrative framework.

So the second key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 9.3: Narrative has force not because it can fabricate reality out of nothing, but because subjects always
enter reality through some interpretive framework.
IV. What Is Recursion: Narrative Does Not Only Explain Reality—Reality Also Turns Back and Rewrites Narrative
At this point, only half has been accomplished.

If narrative merely influences reality in one direction, it is still just “a very important variable.”
What makes the social world more troublesome is that the relationship between narrative and reality is not
unidirectional—they bite into each other back and forth.

This is the central concept of this chapter: recursion.

Definition 9.2: Recursion is the process by which narrative influences reality, and reality in turn changes narrative,
forming a circular feedback loop.
Let us look at the first half.
Once a certain narrative gains dominance, subjects act accordingly.
They reorganize their boundaries, re-understand their interests, re-arrange their risks, re-draw who counts as ally and
who as enemy.
These actions change the actual distribution of Difference.
Reality then begins to grow new shapes in accordance with this narrative.

Now the second half.
Once reality changes, narrative cannot remain unchanged.
If reality continuously supports the old narrative, the old narrative becomes harder, stronger, more natural.
If reality continuously contradicts the old narrative, the old narrative gets patched, redirected, replaced, or
completely collapses.
Meanwhile, new narratives emerge opportunistically, reorganizing Difference.

So recursion is not an elegant word—it is a very concrete thing:

How you speak about reality affects how reality moves;
How reality moves turns back to affect how people speak.
So the third core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 9.4: In the social world, narrative and reality do not interact unidirectionally—they are in a recursive
relationship.
This sentence will run through the latter half of the book.
From market bubbles and institutional legitimacy to identity politics, international relations, and theory itself,
everything works this way.

V. The Three Most Common Results of Recursion: Reinforcement, Backlash, and Drift
When recursion is mentioned, people tend to find it very abstract.
Version 1.6 here grounds it in three most common results.

The First: Reinforcement
This is the most intuitive kind.
Narrative first influences behavior, behavior then produces reality that more closely conforms to the narrative, and so
the narrative is “confirmed” by reality, becoming even stronger.

For example, in markets: “housing prices only go up.”
Once everyone believes it, buying increases, prices rise, and after prices rise, more people believe the statement is
true—so narrative and reality reinforce each other.

Or a country that constantly says “we are being encircled and contained”:
if this narrative guides it to adopt more aggressive behavior, and the adversary becomes more defensive as a result, the
real-world opposition strengthens, and the original narrative appears even more like the truth.

The Second: Backlash
Some narratives are powerful in the short term, but the more successful they become, the more they create conditions
that will eventually overturn them.

For example, a certain order narrative keeps saying “this system has already solved the main problems”:
if widely accepted, the society’s self-correction capacity declines, institutional rigidity increases, and new
Differences more easily accumulate.
In the end, reality begins to contradict this narrative, and the more successful the narrative once was, the more
violent its later collapse.

Or an investment method that more and more people believe in and use:
its very success destroys the original Differences that supported it.
The method therefore begins to fail itself after being widely trusted.

The Third: Drift
There is also a kind of recursion that does not go to extremes—not becoming truer and truer, nor collapsing
immediately—but slowly deforms through reality’s feedback.

The original narrative, to preserve itself, constantly adds patches, shifts emphases, adjusts tones, changes enemies,
and rewrites causes.
It neither completely dies nor completely wins, but drifts under reality’s pull.
This is especially common in institutional narratives, national identity narratives, and market consensus.

So the fourth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 9.5: The three most common results of narrative recursion are reinforcement, backlash, and drift.
With these three basic results, subsequent analysis becomes much easier.
You will no longer merely ask “is this narrative correct?” but will begin asking:
Is it hardening? Is it overextending itself? Has it already begun to drift?

VI. Narrative Is Not Neutral: It Always Amplifies Some Differences and Suppresses Others
If narrative is the organizing mode for Difference, boundaries, causality, and direction, then it certainly cannot be
neutral.
Because once you are organizing, you are ranking.

What you bring to the foreground will be seen.
What you push to the background will be treated as unimportant.
What you define as the core contradiction will attract action and resources.
What you define as secondary noise will more easily continue accumulating underground.

So one of the most important functions of narrative is not to create Difference, but to assign priorities to
Differences.

Some narratives amplify Wealth Difference.
Some narratives amplify Identity Difference.
Some narratives use national security to cover internal Difference.
Some narratives use growth to cover distribution.
Some narratives use freedom to cover risk.
Some narratives use unity to suppress boundary problems.

This means that narrative not only participates in reality but changes the visibility structure of Difference.
And once visibility changes, subjects’ attention, anger, cooperation, obedience, and defense also change.

So the fifth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 9.6: The force of narrative lies not only in explaining Difference, but in deciding which Differences are
amplified and which are suppressed.
This also explains why many societies long-term misjudge their deepest problems.
They do not lack problems;
it is that the dominant narrative has consistently pushed certain Differences backstage while letting others monopolize
the foreground.

VII. Theory Itself Is Also Narrative: It Does Not Stand Outside the World to Observe It
Here, the sharpest and most uncomfortable statement of this chapter must be made.

If narrative and reality are in a recursive relationship,
if subjects change behavior according to narrative,
then theories about society certainly cannot stand outside the world either.

Theory is not a pure mirror.
Theory enters classrooms, policies, organizations, news, markets, and daily judgments.
It changes how people name problems, judge risks, organize friend and foe, and imagine possibilities.
So once a theory is disseminated, it is not merely describing reality—it becomes a force within reality.

So the sixth judgment of this chapter must be firmly established:

Proposition 9.7: Theory itself is also narrative; it does not stand outside the world to observe it—it flows back into
the world.
This is important for the entire book.
Because the Theory of Difference itself cannot be exempt.
If this book is adopted, used, cited, and internalized, it will also change how people observe society.
It will make people more sensitive to Difference conversion, more alert to the reflux of repressed Difference, more
attentive to Nearest Equilibria, and less inclined to believe “the problem has been solved.”
Once these changes occur, the Theory of Difference is no longer merely a book—it becomes a variable within social
reality.

This is not a flaw—it is honesty.
A theory that does not acknowledge its own reflux into its object is, first and foremost, misjudging itself.

VIII. Why Narrative Is Sometimes Faster Than Institutions: Because Institutions Must Execute, but Narrative Changes the
Entrance First
One more layer must be added here, or later explanations will not hold together.

Many people will say: what truly matters is still institutions; narrative is just talk.
This view is half right and half wrong.

Institutions certainly matter.
But institutions are often slow; execution has costs, revision requires procedures, implementation requires
organizational chains.
Narrative is different.
What narrative changes first is not the rules themselves, but the way people enter the rules.

It first changes:

How people understand a rule
Whether people are willing to comply
How people view a loss
Whether people still believe in a certain center
Whether people think a certain arrangement is “the way it should be”
Whether people re-categorize a group as dangerous, ally, parasite, or representative
So narrative is sometimes faster than institutions not because it is stronger than institutions, but because it acts on
the entrance first.
Before institutions have time to change, narrative has already shifted the angle from which subjects face the world.
By the time institutions react, much of reality has already begun growing in a new direction.

This is why:

Before policy changes, the market has already reacted;
Before law moves, group boundaries have already re-hardened;
Before an international treaty is torn up, alliance trust has already collapsed;
While organizational rules remain, internal compliance has already changed.
So the seventh key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 9.8: Narrative frequently appears to take effect before institutions not because it replaces institutions,
but because it first changes the entrance through which subjects enter reality and rules.
IX. Recursion Does Not Mean Narrative Can Say Anything: Reality Will Still Bite Back
At this point, another misreading must also be blocked.

Some people, hearing “narrative is important,” slide toward another extreme:
as if reality does not matter, and as long as you speak well enough, anything can be made true.

Not so.

Recursion is not magic.
Narrative certainly can organize reality, but reality also bites back at narrative.
You can suppress problems in the short term through strong narrative,
but if Differences in reality do not support it over the long term, it will eventually crack.
You can narrate a certain market mania as a permanent bull market,
but as long as cash flow, risk tolerance, liquidity, and trust begin to fail to support it, the narrative will backlash.
You can keep saying “we have already achieved equality,”
but if subjects’ daily experience of boundary awareness and sense of gap grows stronger, the official narrative will
eventually lose its penetrative force.

Therefore, the eighth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 9.9: Narrative certainly can shape reality, but it cannot remain stable over the long term while detached
from the Difference structures in reality.
This sentence is critical.
It prevents the Theory of Difference from retreating to the naive realism of “reality determines everything,” and from
slipping into the lightweight constructivism of “it is all about how you talk.”
It insists that narrative and reality interact, but neither can permanently consume the other.
There is always tension between them, and recursion occurs precisely within this tension.

X. The Landing Point of This Chapter: From Here, the Social World Is No Longer Just Difference in Motion, but Difference
and Explanation in Motion Together
At this point, Volume 2 has truly become sharp.

The preceding chapters showed us that Difference is conserved, converts, and cuts through structure via Events.
This chapter adds the most social-scientific layer:
The social world is not Difference moving alone—it is Difference and explanation of Difference moving together.

Subjects do not act within a purely objective reality.
Subjects always perceive, rank, fear, expect, and organize Difference within some narrative.
Therefore, narrative itself participates in the visibility, clustering, compression, conversion, cascade, and
equilibrium formation of Difference.
This means that from this chapter onward, this book no longer merely analyzes “what is happening within structure,” but
also analyzes “what language, what boundaries, what causal imagination subjects use to enter these structures.”

Once this step is filled in, the subsequent chapters on identity conflict, institutional legitimacy, financial bubbles,
national narrative, alliance formation, hegemonic decline, and theory itself all gain a genuine engine.

The next chapter will push this engine toward one of the most sensitive and difficult domains:

If subjects maintain boundaries through narrative, and Identity Difference is most easily understood as the boundary of
existence itself, then why is identity so stubborn, so easily closed off, so difficult to resolve through interest
negotiation?
This leads to one of the heaviest chapters in Volume 2: Identity Self-Reference and the Roots of Conflict.

Chapter 10: Identity Self-Reference and the Roots of Conflict
(The Theory of Difference, Volume 2, Chapter 10, Official Revised Draft)

I. Why some conflicts, after being negotiated for years and with conditions much improved, become harder rather than
easier to negotiate
At this chapter, Volume 2 enters the part that is most prone to losing control and most in need of restraint.

Several foundations have already been laid.

The three most basic forms of social Difference are Power, Wealth, and Identity.
These Differences do not simply vanish—they are conserved, convert, precipitate, and reflux.
Social change is not a purely smooth process; it frequently cuts through structure via Events.
Narrative is not an instruction manual external to reality—it flows back into reality, changing how subjects understand
boundaries, interests, and danger.

If these judgments hold, then a question that has long plagued social science must be raised anew:

Why do some conflicts, despite years of negotiation, multiple layers of institutional patches, and economic conditions
not necessarily worse than before, become harder rather than easier to negotiate?
Such conflicts are not rare.

In some places, resource distribution has improved, yet conflict has not disappeared.
Some communities’ institutional arrangements have become more sophisticated, yet fractures have not narrowed.
Some boundary disputes technically have room for compromise, yet both sides’ tone has hardened.
Some groups’ material gap has not continued to widen, yet they increasingly see each other as threats.

If explained purely in terms of interest distribution, these phenomena are often difficult to penetrate.
Because interest theory naturally asks: is the distribution still insufficient? Is compensation still lacking? Is rule
design still too coarse? Have people not yet seen the benefits of cooperation?

These questions are certainly not meaningless.
But they tend to miss a deeper layer:
Some conflicts appear to be fighting over interests, but deep down they are fighting over “who is who,” over boundaries,
over who has the right to define reality.

Once conflict rises to this level, it is no longer merely about “whether resources are distributed to one’s
satisfaction.”
It becomes a more thorny question:
If I accept your definition, am I still myself?

This is the fundamental difference between identity conflict and ordinary interest conflict.

II. Why Identity Is Special: Because It Is Not Just About “What I Have,” but About “What I Count As”
Chapter 6 already stated that Identity Difference is most stubborn not because it is most illusory, but because it is
most easily understood by subjects as the boundary of existence itself.
This chapter must fully unfold this point.

Wealth Difference certainly hurts.
It makes people feel pushed down, deprived, constrained.
Power Difference also hurts.
It makes people feel dominated, commanded, excluded from decision-making.
But the pain brought by Identity Difference is not quite the same.

Because Identity Difference does not merely make a subject feel “I lack something”—
it makes it feel: I have been placed in a position that is incomplete, illegitimate, subordinate, suspect, or not
belonging here.

This is why identity problems always harden quickly.

“I get less” can still be negotiated in terms of getting more.
“I have no power” can still be discussed in terms of whether some power can be shared.
But “whether I count as a full member”—once this is touched, subjects often stop seeing themselves as ordinary
negotiators and begin seeing themselves as beings that must protect their boundaries.

So this chapter must first redefine identity more precisely.

Definition 10.1: Identity is not a casual label a subject gives itself, but a subject’s sustained expression of its own
boundaries, belonging position, and legitimate existence.
The most important word in this definition is not “label,” but “boundaries,” “belonging position,” and “legitimate
existence.”

Who I am is not merely a question of self-feeling.
In the social world, it is always entangled with the following:

Whether I count as fully belonging here
Who has the right to decide whether I am a person of this place
Whether my boundaries are defined by me or defined for me by others
Whether my existence is taken for granted or conditionally tolerated
Whether I can be treated as a legitimate subject without first having to prove myself
This is why identity cannot be treated as a “soft cultural add-on.”
It is a deep form of Difference organization.
It does not only determine emotion—it also determines the direction of action, the limits of concession, and the
escalation path of conflict.

III. What Is Identity Self-Reference: Identity Is Hardest to Negotiate Because It Constantly Says “I Am Myself”
“Self-reference” has been mentioned repeatedly in earlier chapters. Here it must be made sufficiently clear.

Definition 10.2: Identity Self-Reference is the process by which a subject maintains its own boundaries through the
continuous act of designating “who counts as us, who does not, and why we are different from them.”
This definition may seem convoluted, but it is actually very everyday.

A nation says “we have our own language, history, and sacrifices.”
A state says “we have our own sovereignty, borders, and way of life.”
A religious community says “we have our own truth, rituals, and boundaries of purity.”
A class says “our kind of people” and “their kind of people” are not the same thing.
An organization says “our way of doing things here” is different from “what they do out there.”
A person also says: “I am this kind of person, not that kind.”

The common thread among these statements is not that they are all intense, but that they all do the same thing:
maintaining the subject through the repetition of Difference.

Why is it called self-reference?
Because identity is not pointing at an external object—it is constantly pointing back at itself:
Who are we.
Who are we not.
Why can we still count as us.
Why can we not be conflated.
Why can we not be renamed by others.

So identity is stubborn not only because it involves boundaries, but because it is the process of boundaries constantly
pointing back to themselves.

You can trade wealth,
you can negotiate rules,
but you cannot easily make a subject say on the question of boundaries:
“Well then, I will no longer define myself in my own way.”

This is the deepest force of identity self-reference.

IV. Why Identity Conflict Closes Off More Easily Than Interest Conflict: Because It Fights Over the Framework, Not Just
the Shares Within It
With the above definitions, the difference between identity conflict and ordinary interest conflict begins to clarify.

Interest conflict usually occurs within the same framework.
Both sides implicitly accept a basic order, merely fighting over distribution, proportions, positions, and resources
within that order.
You get more, I get less;
you go first, I go after;
how rules change, how compensation is given, who retreats, who yields.

Such conflicts can certainly be fierce.
But they still have negotiation space because both sides are broadly on the same map.

Identity conflict is not like this.

Once identity conflict goes deep, what both sides fight over is usually not “shares within the framework,” but:

Who has the right to define this framework
Whose framework this really is
Who counts as normal, who counts as exception
What is the legitimate name of this land, this state, this community, this order
So both sides are not fighting for position on the same map—
they are fighting over “which map counts as the map.”

This leads to a very typical phenomenon:
concessions on interests are instead interpreted as surrender on identity.

For example, offering compensation may not be seen as sincerity;
it may instead be seen as “you want to use resources to buy away my boundaries.”
Offering institutional arrangements may not be seen as a solution;
it may instead be seen as “you have implicitly assumed you can define me, and now you are giving me a small space to fit
in.”
Yielding a little may not be understood by the other side as rational;
it may be understood by one’s own side as “have you already given up defining us in our own way?”

So the first core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 10.1: Interest conflict usually occurs within the same framework; identity conflict often fights over the
framework itself.
This sentence is very important.
It can almost serve as a master key for many seemingly “unnegotiable” conflicts later on.
Not because people are unreasonable,
but because being reasonable itself requires a shared framework.
And the deepest difficulty of identity conflict is precisely that the shared framework has already fractured.

V. Why Identity Problems, Once Ignited, Rapidly Slide from “Pluralistic Coexistence” to “You or Me”
Many people, in times of peace, like to use gentle words when discussing identity.
Words like pluralism, difference, cultural coexistence, mutual respect, inclusiveness.
These words are certainly important and represent real aspirations in many contexts.
But they share a premise: identity boundaries exist, but have not yet been widely interpreted as survival boundaries.

Once this premise fails, the situation changes rapidly.

Why?

Because when subjects begin to understand a certain conflict as a “boundary life-or-death” problem, their logic
immediately hardens.
In this state, many things that could previously have been handled as interest problems are re-elevated.

Population movement ceases to be mere population movement and becomes “our space is being invaded.”
Language policy ceases to be mere educational arrangement and becomes “our way of existence is being replaced.”
An institutional change ceases to be a procedural problem and becomes “who will have the right to define what counts as
normal.”
A piece of land ceases to be a resource and governance problem and becomes “if this is not ours, then whose are we.”

So subjects enter a very typical self-maintenance state:
they would rather bear high costs than accept boundary rewriting.
From the outside, this often appears “irrational” or “why not take a step back”—but from the subject’s interior, it is
perfectly logical, because what is at stake in the retreat is not a share but a boundary. Taking a step back is not
losing a little—it is becoming something else.

So the second key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 10.2: Once identity problems are understood by subjects as survival boundary problems, conflict rapidly
slides from distribution logic to existence logic.
The difference between existence logic and distribution logic lies here.
Distribution logic can still negotiate quantity.
Existence logic first asks: am I still me.

This is also why identity conflict always carries a stronger closure.
Not because it is inherently more “real” than interests,
but because it more easily triggers subjects’ boundary defense mechanisms.

VI. Why Identity Gets Harder the More It Is Spoken: Because Narrative Constantly Colors, Thickens, and Historifies the
Boundary
At this point, identity self-reference has only told half the story.
To truly harden, it also needs narrative recursion.

Subjects do not exist on boundaries alone—they must also constantly narrate why their boundary matters, why it is
legitimate, why it is under threat, and why it cannot loosen.

So narrative begins its work.

It does four things.

First, It Naturalizes the Boundary
That is, it makes a historically formed boundary appear as though it were naturally so, self-evident, and unchangeable.
The language is ours.
The land was always ours.
Tradition has always been this way.
This is not a choice—it is “how things simply are.”

Second, It Moralizes the Boundary
It does not merely say “this is ours” but also “this is legitimate.”
Defending this boundary is not only reasonable in terms of interests—it is a moral imperative.
So yielding ceases to be a transaction and is re-labeled as dishonor, betrayal, forgetting one’s roots.

Third, It Historifies the Boundary
Identity narrative rarely speaks only of the present.
It always pulls in history.
Who was wounded in the past, who was oppressed, who was driven from where, who shed blood, who was humiliated, who once
lost their name.
Once these are drawn in, the boundary ceases to be merely a present-day line—it becomes a line bearing the weight of
memory.

Fourth, It Futurizes the Boundary
Identity narrative always also says:
if we cannot hold today, there will be nothing tomorrow.
If we retreat this time, there will be no place for us in the future.
If we do not act now, the next generation will no longer be us.

This way, a single boundary simultaneously possesses naturalness, morality, historical weight, and future anxiety.
This greatly increases its rigidity.

So the third key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 10.3: Identity hardens the more it is spoken not merely because Difference exists, but because narrative
constantly adds naturalness, morality, historicity, and futurity to the boundary.
Once these four layers are stacked together, the identity boundary is no longer merely a social line—it becomes
something more like an existential shell that must be defended.

VII. Why Identity Conflict Produces the Paradox of “The More You Talk, the Worse It Gets”
Now we can return to the question at the beginning of this chapter.

Why do some conflicts, despite years of negotiation and increasingly sophisticated institutional design, become harder
rather than easier?

Because much negotiation proceeds along interest logic,
while the place where the conflict has truly hardened has already moved to the identity self-reference layer.

So a paradox emerges:

The more detailed the negotiation,
the more it forces both sides to articulate their boundaries.
The process of articulating boundaries is supposed to precisely solve problems;
but it simultaneously reinforces the boundary itself.

A problem that could once be left vague is now written down line by line in the negotiation.
Who is a legitimate representative, who is not.
What counts as a community, what does not.
Which parts of history are recognized, which are not.
Which names enter the text, which are excluded.
This can certainly improve institutional clarity, but it also simultaneously increases identity clarity.

And once identity is clarified,
many boundaries that could once beglossed over by daily life get re-nailed.

This is why, after some peace agreements are signed, violence decreases,
but the sense of boundary between groups does not decrease in tandem.
Because the agreement addresses the operational layer of conflict,
while identity self-reference continues producing itself in the depths.

So the fourth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 10.4: The paradox of “the more you talk, the worse it gets” in identity conflict arises because
interest-based negotiation, while solving operational problems, may also continuously reinforce the boundary itself.
This does not mean one should not negotiate.
It means: if one negotiates only at the operational level without addressing how identity boundaries are re-named and
loosened, then the more detailed the negotiation, the structure may not become looser—it may even become harder.

VIII. Why Identity Conflict Is So Hard to “Completely Resolve”: Because It Is Not a One-Time Problem but a Continuously
Self-Sustaining Structure
Many people, when understanding conflict, habitually ask: how do we solve it?

This question is reasonable in many contexts.
But for identity conflict, it is often asked too quickly.
Because identity conflict is not a one-time problem—it is more like a structure that continuously self-maintains,
self-feeds, and self-escalates.

Why?

Because identity is not a block of stone—it is something continuously produced by naming, exclusion, repetition, and
narrative.
As long as these mechanisms keep operating, identity boundaries will not automatically loosen.

This means:

Ceasefire does not mean boundaries have loosened;
joint governance does not mean mutual recognition is complete;
economic improvement does not mean boundary anxiety has disappeared;
a younger generation that did not experience the original conflict does not mean they will not inherit boundary memory.

Because the deepest fuel for identity conflict is usually not the Event itself, but the boundary awareness that, after
the Event, is woven into daily life and collective memory.

So the fifth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 10.5: Identity conflict is hard to completely resolve not because it cannot be managed, but because it
belongs to a structure that continuously self-maintains.
“Resolve” is often too grand a word here.
More accurate words are usually:

Cooling down
Constraining
Layered handling
Buffering
Isolating cascades
Rewriting narrative interfaces
Letting boundaries shift from hard boundaries to breathable boundaries
That is to say, the truly mature governance goal is not necessarily to “eliminate identity conflict” in one stroke, but
more like:
not letting it continuously escalate into a total conflict that consumes everything.

IX. When Identity Conflict Is Most Dangerous: When It Overlaps with Power and Wealth Difference
Identity Difference is already hard enough on its own.
But its most dangerous moment is not when it exists alone—when it begins to coincide with Power and Wealth Difference.

If a group is not only marginalized in terms of identity,
but simultaneously poorer in wealth
and weaker in power,
then what it faces is no longer mere boundary discomfort, but a triple-layered Difference.

Conversely, if a group occupies a central position in terms of identity
while also being more advantaged in wealth and power,
it will more easily interpret its own advantages as “naturally legitimate”
and interpret the other’s resistance as “irrational” or “a threat to order.”

So identity conflict ceases to be merely narrative against narrative—it, together with institutions, resources, and
obedience chains, forms a higher-intensity locking structure.

This is why some societies’ identity problems are especially untouchable.
Not because their culture is more “stubborn,”
but because Identity Difference is highly overlapped with Wealth Difference and Power Difference.
Touching the boundary is the same as touching distribution, and touching distribution immediately activates rules and
obedience.

So the sixth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 10.6: Identity conflict is most dangerous when Identity Difference is highly overlapped with Wealth
Difference and Power Difference.
This is also why some conflicts that appear to be cultural problems
are especially prone to escalation.
Because they are not single-boundary conflicts—they are three kinds of Difference simultaneously heating up at the same
breakpoint.

Subjects must maintain boundaries

Once boundary conflict is elevated, it ceases to be merely a distribution problem

Chapter 11: The Double-Edge and Fixation of Institutions
(The Theory of Difference, Volume 2, Chapter 11, Official Revised Draft)

I. The most puzzling thing about institutions is not whether they fail, but why they always begin to harden after their
greatest success
At this chapter, Volume 2 begins to confront a problem that nearly every reformer encounters.

People usually criticize institutions when they are already in trouble.
Institutions are inefficient, rigid, protect vested interests, detached from reality, become obstacles to change.
This criticism is certainly valid, but it often sees only the second half of an institution’s life.

The truly harder question is: how did the institution get there?

In other words, why does an arrangement originally formed to solve a problem later become part of the problem itself?
Why does a structure that originally reduced uncertainty, organized Difference, and maintained order later freeze
Difference, lock paths, and push risk deeper?

If this happened only occasionally, one could call it institutional design failure.
But in reality, it repeats almost endlessly.

In the early days of reform, institutions function like tools.
Institutions organize scattered Differences, bring conflict within manageable range, lower uncertainty, and let subjects
know where boundaries are, how rules work, and how cooperation continues.
But once they succeed, new problems begin to emerge.
The more stable institutions become, the more they sediment interests.
The more effective they are, the more habits form.
The more natural they seem, the more people forget they are merely a historically contingent arrangement.
In the end, institutions begin not merely managing Difference but freezing it;
not merely constraining conflict but creating new low-mobility structures;
not merely protecting boundaries but turning boundaries into untouchable things.

So this chapter does not plan to simply say “institutions have problems.”
It aims to say something harder to hear but more real:

The problems of institutions do not begin on the day they fail; their problems are often already planted at the moment
of their greatest success.
This is the double-edge of institutions.

II. What Are Institutions: Not Rules on Paper, but Repetitive Structures Formed After Difference Has Been Stably
Arranged
To understand why institutions harden, “institution” must first be more precisely defined.

Often, people understand institutions as formal rules, legal texts, organizational charters, flowcharts, management
regulations.
These are certainly part of institutions, but not the deepest layer.

Definition 11.1: An institution is a structuralized rule that emerges from the stable arrangement, sustained execution,
and subsequent constraining of subjects’ behavior through the long-term repetition of Difference.
This definition contains four key words.

First: Difference is arranged.
Institutions never emerge in a vacuum.
They emerge because certain Differences must be placed in some order:
who commands whom, who is responsible for what, who goes first, who goes after, who gets what, who has what
qualification to enter, who does not.

Second: long-term repetition.
A one-time decision is not an institution.
The key to an institution is not merely that “it was prescribed,” but that “things will be handled according to this
prescription repeatedly.”

Third: stable execution.
Without execution, there is no institution.
The life of an institution is not in the text—it is in the execution chain, habits, expectations, and punishment
mechanisms.

Fourth: constraining subjects in return.
Institutions are not a simple extension of subjects’ will.
Once an institution forms, it reshapes subjects in return—determining who can think what, do what, enter how, and exit
how.

So institutions are not rule plaques hanging on walls.
They are more like Difference arrangements that, after being repeated enough times to stabilize, begin reshaping reality
in reverse.

Once this definition holds, the double-edge of institutions begins to show.
Because anything that can stably arrange Difference over the long term simultaneously possesses two sides:
it can stabilize the situation,
and it can also freeze the situation.

III. The First Face of Institutions: They Press Difference into Order So the World Does Not Have to Renegotiate Every
Moment
First, let us speak of the benefits of institutions, and speak of them with sufficient weight.
Because without acknowledging this face first, later criticism of institutions will lack force.

Institutions do not emerge because humans love rules.
They emerge because without institutions, many Differences simply cannot be handled over the long term.

Imagine a world entirely without institutions.
Power Differences must be re-tested every time.
Wealth attribution must be re-fought every time.
Boundaries must be re-drawn every time.
Cooperative relationships must be re-negotiated every time.
Responsibility, punishment, reward, trust, obedience, qualification—none of it is stable.
Such a world would certainly not be “free”—it would be more like a continuous provisional state of high friction, high
cost, and low predictability.

The first function of institutions is to reduce this high friction.
They let subjects know:

What can be done, what cannot
Who goes first, who goes after
What belongs to whom
How to handle problems when they arise
What channel conflict takes
Who bears risk
Where boundaries are
This is no small matter.
It is almost the prerequisite for large-scale social cooperation to be possible.

So the first core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 11.1: The primary function of institutions is not to create justice, but to press Difference into repeatable
order, reducing the cost of continuous renegotiation.
This sentence is very important.
Because it places institutions from the start not merely as moral objects, but first as organizational objects.
Many institutions may be imperfect, even unjust, but their longevity often comes precisely from their effectiveness at
“suppressing continuous renegotiation.”

This is also why institutions often have astonishing inertia.
They exist not through persuasion, but through saving vast numbers of subjects the cost of continuously re-negotiating
the world.

IV. The Second Face of Institutions: After Stabilizing Difference, They Also Freeze Difference in Place
Now that the benefits of institutions have been stated, the real problem emerges.

If institutions derive their power from long-term repetition and stable arrangement,
they cannot stop at merely “keeping things orderly.”
They must also do something else: write the current Difference distribution into the future.

Who is on top, who is on the bottom;
what counts as a normal path, what counts as exception;
who has the right to enter, who must first prove themselves;
what resources can flow freely, what resources are locked behind doors;
what identity is naturally recognized, what identity must constantly explain itself.

Once these are stabilized by institutions, institutions cease merely “managing Difference” and begin “fixing
Difference.”

So the second core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 11.2: While reducing disorder, institutions also write the current Difference configuration into the future
starting line.
This is the true double-edge of institutions.

They lower conflict costs on one side,
while raising the cost of later changing Difference distribution on the other.
They make the world more predictable on one side,
while making it harder to redirect on the other.
They make cooperation sustainable on one side,
while making old advantages more easily accumulate into new inertia on the other.

So the problem of institutions is not a simple “good” or “bad.”
Their problem is:
the more successfully they stabilize a certain configuration, the more likely they make that configuration later
immovable.

This is why many institutions start as bridges and end as dams.
At first, they make flow smoother;
over time, they begin blocking water.

V. Why Institutions Fixate: Because They Simultaneously Lock Interests, Cognition, and Paths
At this point, “institutions harden” is still just a feeling.
To turn it into something analyzable, we must further explain: how exactly do institutions fixate.

Version 1.6 here tightens the mechanisms of institutional fixation into three types.

First, Interest Lock-in
Once an institution stably operates, some people will inevitably form positional advantages within it.
This advantage is not merely about money—it is also about qualification, familiarity, channels, timing, organizational
resources, and default legitimacy.

So the more stable the institution, the more it slowly grows a group of people who depend on it.
These people may not all be “bad people,” but their real positions are already bound to the institution.
They need not shout daily “I want to defend the institution”—
they only need to continue living, profiting, advancing, distributing, and judging in the institutional way, and the
institution is already being continuously reproduced through them.

This is interest lock-in.

Second, Cognitive Lock-in
Institutions do not merely arrange resources—they arrange intuitions.
Over time, people cease seeing institutions as historically formed and begin seeing them as “normal.”
“This is how it should obviously be done.”
“Isn’t this common sense?”
“The rules have always been written this way.”
“Other approaches are simply unimaginable.”

Once an institution sinks into common sense, it gains a second protective shell.
Because at this point, opposing the institution is no longer merely opposing one arrangement—it feels like opposing “the
normal world itself.”

This is cognitive lock-in.
It is often deeper than interest lock-in, and harder to break.

Third, Path Lock-in
Institutions also accumulate vast sunk costs.
Organizational structures are built around them,
talent is trained according to them,
processes are designed to fit them,
metrics are computed based on them,
narratives are told through them,
subjects are differentiated by them.

So even when everyone knows there is a problem,
actually changing it reveals: it is not one rule that needs changing, but many connected things.
The old institution therefore continuously generates startup costs for new paths.

So the third key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 11.3: Institutional fixation occurs primarily through three locking mechanisms—interest lock-in, cognitive
lock-in, and path lock-in.
Once these three lock-ins overlap, institutions become increasingly like a structure that “you know has problems, but
find increasingly difficult to move.”

VI. Why Institutions Always Plant Rigidity at Their Most Successful Moment: Because Success Thickens the Lock-in Layers
Many people think institutions begin to fail only when they fail.
In reality, the most dangerous moment for many institutions is precisely when they are most successful.

Why?

Because the more successful the institution,
the more stable the beneficiaries,
the more naturalized the cognition,
the deeper the path investment.
That is to say, all three layers of lock-in thicken together.

A newly established institution, though crude, has not yet grown into the daily bones.
An institution that has successfully operated for decades certainly looks more mature on the surface,
but this also means:

More and more people depend on it for livelihood
More and more people treat it as common sense
More and more organizational links are built around it
So the more effectively the institution solved its early problems,
the more easily it later turns itself into “a problem that cannot be easily touched.”

So the fourth judgment of this chapter must be stated bluntly:

Proposition 11.4: Institutional rigidity often arises not from failure, but from prior excessive success.
This sentence suddenly clarifies many historical and real-world problems.

Why do examination systems often become harder to reform the more mature they are?
Because the more mature they become, the more people organize their lives around them.
Why do welfare systems become harder to adjust the more stable they are?
Because they do not only distribute resources—they shape expectations and life paths.
Why do certain approval systems in state governance become harder to touch the more complete they are?
Because they are no longer merely rules—they are a combination of organizational power, interest flows, and daily common
sense.

The double-edge of institutions truly shows here.
They helped you stabilize the world,
and they also made that world increasingly hard to stabilize differently.

VII. Why Institutions Always Lean Toward the Nearest Equilibrium: Because Their Primary Task Is Not the Best, but First
to Stabilize the Situation
If the previous chapter mainly used “Nearest Equilibrium” for Difference conversion, this chapter must explicitly embed
it in institutional analysis.

Institutions are not designed in a vacuum.
They are always needed in some state of imbalance, chaos, conflict, or high friction.
So the original mission of institutions is usually not to establish the optimal order, but:

First, stabilize the situation.

This brings a very important consequence:
institutions naturally lean toward Nearest Equilibria.

That is to say, they more easily adopt arrangements that:

Still have continuity with the old order
Can most easily secure compliance from existing subjects
Require the least rebuilding of execution chains from scratch
Can most easily let key Differences temporarily settle
So institutions are often not the historically best solution, but the historically first usable solution.
They are like a plank hastily laid to prevent falling through.
The problem is that once the plank has been in place long enough, it may be mistaken for the floor itself.

So the fifth core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 11.5: Institutions naturally lean toward Nearest Equilibria because they first serve “stabilize first”
rather than “optimize first.”
This is also why many institutional reforms end up looking like variants of the old order rather than the beginning of a
brand-new world.
Not because human imagination is insufficient,
but because institutions, at their initial birth, first had to answer “how not to fall apart” rather than “how to be
optimal.”

This judgment will return again and again in organizations, states, market regulation, and international order.

VIII. Why Institutions Often Suppress Problems While Also Suppressing the Channels Through Which Problems Can Be Seen in
Time
Once an institution successfully stabilizes the situation, there is a very thorny side effect:
it often does not merely manage problems—it also manages the visibility of problems.

This is the most dangerous kind of double-edge effect.

Because the normal operation of an institution produces an appearance:
everyone is following procedures,
conflict has handling mechanisms,
risk has statistical frameworks,
boundaries have clear specifications,
and so people easily feel: the situation is under control.

But often, what the institution has suppressed is not only disorder—
it is also the outlets through which Difference becomes visible.
It makes problems no longer appear in their original form.
And once problems cannot appear in their original form, they more easily transfer into covert space.

So the stronger an institution is, the more likely it creates a dangerous illusion:
problems are not visible, so problems have decreased.
In reality, problems have merely changed channels.

So the sixth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 11.6: Institutions do not only manage Difference—they also manage the visibility of Difference; this enables
them to stabilize situations while also delaying timely recognition of problems.
This is also why many institutional crises appear especially calm before they erupt.
Because what they lose first is not operational capacity,
but the ability to expose real Differences in a timely manner.

IX. Institutional Failure Does Not Mean Institutions Disappear—It More Often Means Informal Order Grows Behind the
Formal One
Many people have an overly neat imagination about institutional crisis:
institutions fail, and so chaos ensues.

Reality is usually not that clean.

More commonly:
formal institutions weaken, but affairs do not stop running.
It is just that the rules formerly written on the surface begin yielding to other arrangements that are unwritten but
still coordinate the situation.

These arrangements may be relationship networks, gray channels, local understandings, unwritten rules, internal
negotiation, small-circle governance, personal authority, or provisional patches.
They are not necessarily good and are often opaque,
but they emerge not by accident—because the system still needs a structure that “can be stabilized nearest at hand.”

So the seventh judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 11.7: The most common consequence of institutional crisis is not the absence of institutions, but the rapid
emergence of informal equilibria behind the formal institution.
This is critical.
Because it prevents us from understanding institutional problems as a binary switch between “having rules” and “having
no rules.”
The real situation is more like:
one set of rules weakens,
and another set of unwritten rules immediately takes over.
The old order is gone,
but the Nearest Equilibrium will not be left vacant.

This also explains why many reforms, upon breaking old institutions without timely building new equilibria, see the
situation first taken over not by ideal institutions but by old relationships, old circles, old organizational
resources, and old obedience chains.
This is not an accidental byproduct of “reform failure”—
it is the structural result of institutional double-edge and Nearest Equilibrium operating together.

X. The Landing Point of This Chapter: From Here, Institutions Are No Longer Understood as “Rule Collections,” but as
Difference-Stabilizing and Difference-Freezing Devices
At this point in this chapter, the overall logic of Volume 2 has tightened further.

Chapter 6 established three basic Difference forms.
Chapter 7 showed Difference is conserved, converts, and slides toward Nearest Equilibria.
Chapter 8 showed major change cuts through structure via Events.
Chapter 9 showed narrative recursively participates in organizing reality.
Chapter 10 showed identity boundaries self-maintain and lead to conflict closure.
At this chapter, institutions are finally placed in their true position:

Institutions are not neutral rule collections—they are devices that society uses to stably arrange Difference.
Precisely because they can stabilize Difference, they create order.
And precisely because they can stabilize Difference, they also freeze Difference.

This is the double-edge of institutions.

From this chapter onward, whenever institutional problems are encountered, this book will no longer merely ask:

Are the rules written correctly?
Is the design detailed enough?
Was execution adequate?
It will further pursue:

Which Differences has it stabilized?
Which Differences has it frozen?
Which Differences has it made less mobile?
Is it turning old equilibria into future shackles?
When it suppresses problems, does it simultaneously suppress the visible channels for problems?
When it fails, from where will the Nearest Equilibrium emerge to take over?
This is where institutional analysis becomes truly hard.

The next chapter will push forward from here:

If institutions stabilize Difference, freeze Difference, and suppress visibility, then how will those Differences that
have been long suppressed by institutions eventually return?
This leads to the next chapter of Volume 2: The Return of Repressed Difference.

Chapter Echo
Remember the six most important sentences from this chapter.

The first: The primary function of institutions is not to create justice, but to press Difference into repeatable order,
reducing the cost of continuous renegotiation.
The second: While reducing disorder, institutions also write the current Difference configuration into the future
starting line.
The third: Institutional fixation occurs primarily through three locking mechanisms—interest lock-in, cognitive lock-in,
and path lock-in.
The fourth: Institutional rigidity often arises not from failure, but from prior excessive success.
The fifth: Institutions naturally lean toward Nearest Equilibria because they first serve “stabilize first” rather than

The sixth: The most common consequence of institutional crisis is not the absence of institutions, but the rapid

These six sentences will continually echo in the later sections on investment studies and international politics.

Chapter 12: The Return of Repressed Difference
(The Theory of Difference, Volume 2, Chapter 12, Official Revised Draft)

I. The most dangerous moment is usually not when conflict is most visible, but when it appears to have vanished
If the logic of the preceding chapters has been layered up to this point, then this chapter is almost inevitable.

Chapter 7 said Difference cannot be zeroed out without cost—it is conserved, converts, and flows along low-resistance
channels.
Chapter 8 said structural change is frequently cut open by Events.
Chapter 9 said narrative not only explains reality but recursively organizes it.
Chapter 10 said identity boundaries self-maintain and conflict closes off rapidly on the question of “who are we.”
Chapter 11 further pointed out that institutions can both stabilize and freeze Difference, and even suppress the
channels through which problems are seen in time.

If all of this holds, then an almost inevitable consequence emerges:

Those Differences that have been suppressed, flattened, and pushed out of sight will not obediently die.
They will continue growing elsewhere.
They will deform in places less subject to supervision.
They will accumulate in silence, ferment at the margins, and coagulate in the seams that institutions cannot see.
And when conditions are right, they often do not return gently—they return in more urgent, more extreme, and
harder-to-manage forms.

This is what this chapter addresses: the return of repressed Difference.

Note that the word here is “repressed,” not “handled.”
The two are not the same.

Handled means Difference has been redistributed, reorganized, and given some sustainable outlet.
Repressed means the overt expression of Difference has been sealed off, but Difference itself remains.
The former may reduce risk;
the latter often merely pushes risk backward, inward, and into darkness.

Precisely for this reason, this chapter must first nail down one sentence:

Proposition 12.1: A society’s most dangerous moment is usually not when conflict is most visible, but when conflict
appears to have vanished while in fact merely having lost its public outlets.
Many institutional crises, market collapses, identity explosions, and organizational breakdowns do not occur during
“open arguing.”
They precisely often occur when:

The surface is at its tidiest
Discourse is at its most unified
Metrics are at their most attractive
Obedience appears most natural
Problems most resemble having been solved
Because that often means real Difference has been pushed into covert space rather than genuinely handled.

II. What Repression Means: Not Less Difference, but Difference No Longer Allowed to Appear in Its Original Form
“Repression” must also be clearly defined, or it will easily become too emotional a term.

Definition 12.1: Repression refers to the forced compression of Difference’s original forms of expression,
organizational channels, or positions of manifestation, without Difference itself thereby disappearing.
The most critical part of this definition is “original forms of expression being compressed.”

That is to say, what is repressed is not Difference as a structural fact itself, but:

The ways it could formerly be spoken
The paths it could formerly be organized through
The positions in which it could formerly be seen
The names under which it could formerly be recognized
For example, a society that suppresses public strikes does not mean labor-capital Difference is gone—it merely means
labor-capital Difference can no longer appear in the form of “strike.”
A state that suppresses public identity expression does not mean identity boundaries are gone—it merely means they can
no longer appear through the same discourse, the same flags, the same organizational forms.
A financial system that pushes risk off the on-books ledger does not mean the risk is gone—it merely means it can no
longer sit there in its original accounting form.
An organization that suppresses internal public dissent does not mean different views are gone—they merely can no longer
appear in meetings and procedures.

So the first key definition of this chapter tells us not “how bad repression is” but “what repression actually does.”
It does not eliminate Difference—it
pushes Difference off the track of visibility, speakability, organizability, and countability.

Once this step is seen clearly, many later phenomena suddenly become coherent.

III. Once Difference Is Repressed, the First Thing It Loses Is Not Force but Legitimate Outlets
Why is repressed Difference dangerous?
Not because it suddenly becomes stronger,
but because it first loses its formerly gentler, more transparent, more negotiable outlets.

This is key to understanding the return mechanism.

If a Difference can still be publicly expressed, can still enter institutional procedures, can still be negotiated, can
still be seen in daily life, it certainly may bring conflict, but this conflict at least remains on a plane that society
can handle.

The problem is that once these outlets are sealed off, Difference does not thereby give up existing.
It merely begins seeking new outlets.
And new outlets tend to be more extreme, harder, and harder to mediate than old ones.

So the first step in the return of repressed Difference is not “it grew bigger,” but:

It first lost the capacity for gentle expression.
Public protest is gone, replaced by underground organization.
Normal dissent is gone, replaced by extreme expression.
Formal financial channels are gone, replaced by gray financing.
Institutional competition is gone, replaced by private factionalism.
Negotiable boundary problems are gone, replaced by unnegotiable questions in memory.

So the second judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 12.2: Once Difference is repressed, the first thing it loses is usually not force, but its original
legitimate outlets.
This sentence, though seemingly simple, is actually critical.
It explains why returning Difference often appears more extreme, more closed, and harder to handle than the original.
Not because repression makes people “fundamentally worse,”
but because the lower-intensity expression channels were first blocked.

IV. Why Return Is Often More Violent Than the Original Difference: Because It Accumulated in Narrower Channels
A river flowing in its wide bed
is not the same force as a river that, after being dammed, bursts through a narrow gap.
The same is true for Difference in society.

When Difference flows openly in its original position,
it is continuously dissolved, dispersed, and eased by daily negotiation, institutional procedures, social conventions,
public feedback, and local compromise.
This keeps it in a state of “present but not yet explosive.”

But once it is pressed into narrower, darker, less transparent, less feedback-rich channels, its mode of accumulation
changes.

Because in these channels:

It is less subject to reality-testing
Less dissolved by institutions
Less interrupted by diverse voices
More easily wrapped in singular narratives
More easily entangled with humiliation, resentment, rumor, and symbolic boundaries
So Difference that returns has often lost the “still negotiable, still adjustable” form it once had.
It carries the bias and density accumulated in narrow channels.

So the third key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 12.3: Repressed Difference often returns in more violent form not because it became stronger out of nothing,
but because it accumulated higher density in narrower, less feedback-rich channels.
This explains many real-world cases of “why it became harder later.”

It is not that later people were worse than earlier people.
It is not that the problem itself grew malice.
It is that Difference that could once breathe was forced into positions where it could not breathe.
When it came back out, its breathing had of course changed.

V. Return Is Not Random—It Usually Occurs Along Three Paths: Underground, Extreme, and Surrogate
To prevent this chapter from falling into vague generalizations about “it always comes back,” Version 1.6 here
explicitly identifies the three most common paths of repressed Difference return.

The First Path: Going Underground
This is the most direct path.
Open space does not allow it, so it moves underground.
Underground markets, underground credit, underground religious networks, underground political organizations,
underground culture, underground identity communities—all follow this path.

The characteristic of going underground is not that it is inherently more dangerous, but that once underground, it is
harder for normal social procedures to monitor and correct.
Everything continues, but retreats to positions where institutions cannot see clearly.

The Second Path: Extremeization
Some Differences, even without going fully underground, lose their middle expression layers, so when they return, only
two extremes remain: silence and explosion.
Gentle expression has no space,
nuanced distinction has no space,
gray-area narrative has no space,
and what can survive is only the sharpest, hardest, most boundary-conscious language.

This is why some problems that were not necessarily extreme grow into extreme forms after repression.
Not because they inherently belong to extremes, but because the middle ground was first cleared away.

The Third Path: Surrogacy
This path is easily overlooked but actually very widespread.
Difference does not return under its original name—it borrows another shell.

Wealth Difference returns disguised as cultural taste.
Identity Difference returns disguised as public-safety narrative.
Power Difference returns disguised as professionalism.
Institutional discontent returns disguised as moral purism.
Market risk returns disguised as technology-innovation packaging.

You think you are facing a new problem,
but often it is merely the old Difference finding a more viable surrogate.

So the fourth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 12.4: Repressed Difference most commonly returns through three paths: underground, extreme, and surrogate.
These three paths are very important
because they cover many of the hardest-to-read phenomena in the later chapters on institutions, investment,
international politics, and identity.

VI. Why Repression Often Creates “False Governance Success”
This is the most ironic but also most realistic part of this chapter.

Many repressions appear effective in the short term not because they actually solved the problem, but because they are
very skilled at producing an appearance:

Public conflict has decreased
Metrics look better
Surface order has been restored
Dissent no longer sits at the table
Risk no longer appears in the original reports
Boundary disputes no longer occupy the foreground
So governors, institutional designers, market participants, and external observers all face a strong temptation:
Has the problem already been solved?

This temptation is very strong because the real world rewards short-term stability.
Everyone likes the feeling of “things are stable for now.”
But the trouble lies precisely here—repression is most easily mistaken for resolution.

So the fifth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 12.5: The most dangerous aspect of repression is not merely that it delays problems, but that it creates the
illusion that “problems have been solved.”
Once this illusion forms, consequences worsen.
Because it not only delays genuine treatment—it
also weakens the system’s alertness to returning risk.
Problems are pushed to more hidden positions,
while the will to address them is carried away by the narrative of “we have already stabilized.”

This is why many crises, before truly erupting,
always have a stage where “everyone looks relieved.”

VII. Repressed Return and Nearest Equilibrium: Why Systems Often Fall into Harder Structures Upon Return
This chapter would not be complete without integrating “Nearest Equilibrium.”

After Difference is repressed and then returns, it certainly does not automatically return to its original position.
The original position has often ceased to exist, or is no longer the same kind of position.
So where will it land?

The answer remains: it will first fall on the nearest, most easily accommodating equilibrium.

The problem is that once repressed Difference returns,
the system has often lost many of its softer middle layers.
The formerly negotiable channels are gone,
the formerly buffering spaces have narrowed,
the formerly accommodating positions forambiguous boundaries have been cut off.
This means that when it lands again, it is more likely to fall not into a more open structure, but into a harder,
shorter, more direct equilibrium.

For example:

Public political expression is long suppressed; upon return, it most easily lands first on underground organization and
extreme identity mobilization, not rational deliberation.
The financial system long squeezes risk off the on-books ledger; upon return, it most easily lands first on liquidity
stampede, not smooth repricing.
An organization long suppresses internal dissent; upon return, it most easily lands first on factionalism and distrust,
not gentle correction.
A state long suppresses boundary disputes; upon return, it most easily lands first on stronger identity closure, not
more nuanced coexistence arrangements.

So the sixth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 12.6: When repressed Difference returns, the system does not automatically recover to a healthier state—it
more commonly slides first to a harder, nearer equilibrium that can more rapidly stabilize the situation.
This sentence is extremely important.
Because it shows that return is not self-repair—
return is merely return.
Where it first lands after returning depends on what accommodating structures remain.
And long-term repression precisely tends to hollow out the soft accommodating structures first.

VIII. Why Some Repression Does Not Immediately Cause Trouble: Because the System Can Still Rely on Old Inertia to Hold
At this point, one more layer must be added, or the impression will be “repression always explodes.”

In reality, many repressions do not immediately lead to violent return.
Some systems can even operate smoothly with massive repression for many years.
Why?

Because old institutions, old identities, old obedience chains, and old organizational inertia are still holding things
together.
They provide some provisional stability,
allowing repressed Difference to be temporarily contained.

But there is an important qualification here:
this kind of stability does not mean the repression succeeded; often, it merely means old inertia is still paying the
bill.

Once this old inertia begins to loosen:

The older generation exits
Old organizations decline
Old narratives lose penetrative force
Old interest coalitions dissolve
External shocks increase
New technology opens new covert channels
The previously contained Differences will more rapidly become visible.

So the seventh judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 12.7: Some repressions do not immediately cause trouble not because the problem is gone, but because old
inertia is still paying the stability cost on behalf of the system.
This judgment is especially important for institutional analysis and international politics.
Many orders can still hold not because they solved their problems, but because someone is still dragging the old
problems forward on their behalf.
Once they can no longer carry the weight, the return will suddenly appear to come “out of nowhere.”

It does not come from nowhere.
The bill has simply come due.

IX. The Landing Point of This Chapter: From Here, Repression Is No Longer Understood as “Solving Problems,” but as
“Rearranging the Order of Problems’ Appearance”
At this point in this chapter, a key thread running through Volume 2 has finally been fully clarified.

Difference cannot be zeroed out without cost.
Institutions can suppress Difference and also suppress the visible channels for Difference.
Narrative can push problems offstage and also rearrange how problems are understood.
Identity boundaries, once losing gentle outlets, return more extremely.
Events cut through old structures, and repressed Differences continue growing at the moment they are least visible.
Upon return, they usually do not return to their original position—they first slide toward the nearest, often harder
equilibrium.

So, from this chapter onward, repression can no longer be understood as “solving problems.”
It is closer to:

Rearranging the order of problems’ appearance.
The problem is not gone.
It is simply not allowed to appear this way now.
So it changes time, place, face, shell, and temperature, and returns anew.

This will make the later sections on investment studies and international politics clearer.

Chapter Thirteen: Price, Valuation, and the Revelation of Difference
I. The most fascinating thing about markets is not that they price things, but that they constantly make people mistake
the price for reality itself
Markets have long attracted people not merely because they can make money. Their deeper allure is that they look like a
place that keeps telling “the truth.”

When a price rises, it seems to mean value has increased. When a price falls, it seems to mean judgment has been
corrected. When an asset is bought by many, it’s as if many people are voting at once. Thus, the price is easily
mistaken for reality itself—as though it were naturally closer to the truth, harder than narrative, colder than
positions, less likely to lie than a human mouth.

But it is precisely here that markets most readily produce illusions.

Because a market is not a simple mirror reflecting reality. It doesn’t just say: reality is out there, and the market
merely copies it. The price within a market is essentially a temporary revelation of Differences among Subjects. It
compresses the judgments, information, fears, desires, time horizons, risk tolerances, and narrative expectations of
different people into a single number. This number matters, of course—it matters enormously. But importance does not
equal transparency. A price can look very clean, yet contain extremely murky contents.

So this chapter begins by establishing a judgment that seems simple but actually determines the direction of the entire
volume:

Proposition 13.1: Price is not reality itself, but the temporary revelation of Difference within the market.
This sentence will run through the entire volume. Because once price is understood as a revelation of Difference rather
than an objective truth in itself, many questions about markets, bubbles, crashes, valuation, risk, and investment
behavior can be reorganized from a different angle.

II. Why price is not “the thing’s own voice,” but the result of compressed Differences between Subjects
Let’s make the most basic layer clear first.

A price exists not because the asset can speak for itself. It can be transacted because there is a Difference between
buyer and seller.

If both parties valued it identically—and had no differences in time preference, risk tolerance, liquidity needs,
information, or expectations—then many trades simply wouldn’t occur. Trades happen precisely because:

Someone thinks it’s worth more
Someone thinks it’s worth less
Someone needs cash more urgently
Someone is more willing to tolerate volatility
Someone believes the future will change
Someone just wants to stabilize the present
Someone knows more
Someone is simply following their emotions
So price is never “value standing up and speaking a sentence on its own.” Price is more like: at a given moment, after
competition, compromise, misalignment, and trading among a large number of market participants, a number temporarily
pressed out.

This is why the same asset can produce prices that seem outrageous yet actually transact—at different times, in
different market structures, under different narrative atmospheres. Because what price reflects has never been just the
underlying object itself, but also the structure of Differences operating around it.

Thus, the first key definition of this chapter can be written as:

Definition 13.1: Price is the temporary result of Subject Differences within the market being compressed, matched, and
revealed at a specific point in time.
Note that “temporary” here is very important. Price is not a final verdict. It is merely a compression result at one
point in time. At the next moment, once the Difference structure supporting this compression changes, the price will
immediately change as well.

III. Valuation is not “seeing the true value,” but arranging a speakable framework for Difference
In many investment discussions, “valuation” is treated as something especially objective. As if the market may be noisy,
but there is always a more stable “true value” calculated by models, waiting for the price to slowly converge toward it.

This is certainly not entirely wrong. For many targets with cash flows, assets, and clear business structures, valuation
can indeed provide a judgment framework more stable than emotion. The problem is that valuation has never been as simple
as seeing the true value. It is actually doing something else:

It is arranging a “temporarily speakable” order for the Differences in the market.
What you include in cash flows, what discount rate you use, how you judge the risk premium, how you view industry
cycles, governance quality, technological prospects, policy environments, and Subject psychological expectations—none of
these are pure facts. They themselves already stand within a narrative framework.

So valuation is not standing outside Difference to see a true thing. Valuation is more like: attempting to use a
relatively stable interpretive framework to find a temporarily inhabitable position for the continuously fluctuating
Differences in the market.

Thus, the second judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 13.2: Valuation is not a direct perception of true value, but a relatively stable organizational mode for
Difference.
Once this sentence holds, many debates take on a different shape.

The market being high doesn’t necessarily mean it’s simply “wrong”—it could also mean narrative Differences are
currently overwhelming cash flow Differences. The market being low doesn’t automatically mean “cheap”—it could also mean
certain Subjects are repricing with higher fear premiums, liquidity preferences, and survival logic.

So valuation matters, but it cannot be deified into a God’s eye outside the market. It remains an organizational form
within reality—just one that is slower, more stable, and better able to temporarily shield against emotional winds than
the immediate price.

IV. Why prices so often look “excessive”: because what the market compresses is never just information, but also time,
liquidity, and boundary fear
If price were merely information revelation, then many extreme market fluctuations would be hard to explain. In reality,
a great many prices clearly do more than calmly update for information. They surge too far, fall too far, squeeze too
far, and stampede too far, all in one go.

Why?

Because what gets pressed into price during market transactions is not only information Difference, but also three types
of Difference that are chronically underestimated.

First, time Difference
Some people can wait; others cannot. This is unremarkable during calm periods, but at moments of stress it can instantly
become the core of the market.

A person who is bullish long-term, if they must recover cash today, will be forced to sell at the worst possible moment.
A person who knows the price is unreasonable, if their short-term leverage can’t hold up, will also have to go with the
market’s direction. Thus, price reflects not only “is it worth it,” but also “who can’t afford to wait right now.”

Second, liquidity Difference
Everyone in the market likes having buyers when they want to sell and sellers when they want to buy. But once liquidity
starts contracting, asset prices no longer reflect only value judgments—they also reflect “can this thing turn into
money right now.”

Many price crashes don’t happen because the long-term value of the underlying suddenly goes to zero, but because the
market at some moment suddenly values “who can take the other side right now” over “what is it actually worth
long-term.”

Third, boundary fear
This is a point the Theory of Difference especially emphasizes. Subjects enter the market not only with return
calculations, but also with boundary fear. Fear of being pushed out, fear of losing their safety cushion, fear of
falling from center to periphery, fear of others running first, fear of being left holding the bag. These things are not
merely psychological fluctuations—they are connected to the Subject’s survival boundary and positional boundary.

When boundary fear enters the market, price undergoes a very typical structural change: it stops primarily asking “is it
worth it?” and starts primarily asking “let’s just survive first.”

So the third key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 13.3: Prices so often overshoot not merely because of information updating, but because time Difference,
liquidity Difference, and boundary fear are all compressed into price together.
This sentence is important because it returns market prices from being “information numbers” to being “social structural
results with very high Difference density.”

V. Why short-term trading is approximately zero-sum: because Difference is redistributed among Subjects, not created out
of nothing
Here we must enter one of the most easily misread, yet most necessary to clarify, points in investment studies.

Short-term market trading certainly looks exciting. Every day there are rises, falls, turnover, transactions, floating
profits, floating losses—as if something new is constantly being created. But if we look at the trading layer alone, we
must acknowledge a very hard fact:

After costs are deducted, short-term trading is approximately zero-sum.
The reason is not complicated. One party sells at a certain price at a given moment; another party buys at that moment.
If the price moves later and one side earns more, it usually means the other side earns less, or could have earned more
but didn’t. What the market does here is not create new value, but redistribute Difference among Subjects.

Who is faster, who is slower. Who has more information, who is more ignorant. Who can hold on longer, who breaks first.
Who is better at exploiting narrative, who is more easily swept away by narrative. All these Differences are eventually
settled through price fluctuations.

So the fourth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 13.4: At the short-term trading level, the market is primarily redistributing Difference, not creating net
new value within trading itself.
This sentence must be stated carefully, because it directly connects to the relationship between investment studies and
the Theory of Difference.

The market is certainly not without value. It has an enormous resource allocation function. It allows those willing to
bear certain risks to take them on, allows those who want to exit to exit, allows those with more time, more tolerance,
and better judgment to absorb volatility. But all this occurs at the level of “redistributing Difference”—not at the
level of “trading actions themselves automatically generating new wealth.”

VI. Where does long-term positive-sum come from: not from trading itself, but from real growth outside the market
Once the short-term approximately zero-sum point is stated, many people immediately ask: then why can long-term
investment be positive-sum? Why do indices go up? Why does enterprise value increase over the long term?

Here we must completely separate two layers.

Definition 13.2: The market’s short-term trading layer primarily handles Difference redistribution; the market’s
long-term positive-sum results primarily come from real value growth outside the market.
That is to say, what truly makes long-term investment positive-sum is not “everyone trading with each other” in itself,
but:

Enterprises genuinely creating new products
Technology genuinely improving productivity
Organizations genuinely expanding scale
Society genuinely accumulating more capabilities
People genuinely being able to cooperate on a larger scale and create new distributable output
These things come from creation in reality, not from pressing the trade button.

The market’s role is to transmit, distribute, discount, and reallocate these external gains through the price system. It
functions like a mechanism that converts real-world value changes into tradable signals. Without external growth,
relying solely on the market buying and selling with itself, long-term positive-sum cannot be established out of thin
air.

So the fifth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 13.5: The market’s long-term positive-sum is not a product of trading itself, but the result of real growth
outside the market being redistributed through the price system.
Once this sentence holds, many confusions in investment studies can be cut apart. You can more clearly distinguish:

When you are participating in the sharing of value creation results
When you are merely playing a Difference redistribution game with other Subjects
When the market is reflecting external growth
When the market is merely self-amplifying internal sentiment and leverage
VII. The relationship between price and narrative: price is not the opposite of narrative—it is often what narrative

As stated earlier, narrative flows back into reality. In markets, this backflow becomes extraordinarily direct.

The problem is that in markets, the number itself is often what the story looks like after being transacted.

An asset rises not only because new information appears, but also because narratives like “it represents the future,”

So the sixth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 13.6: In markets, price is often not the opposite of narrative, but the visible form after narrative has
been collectively executed by Subjects.
This is why price cannot be simply understood as a “truth display.” Sometimes it is more like a “narrative intensity

Of course, real Differences will eventually come back to bite narrative. If narrative long-term departs from cash flows,
productivity, credit structures, and institutional constraints, price will eventually run into trouble. But for extended
periods, price and narrative can completely feed each other, until the system enters a state of higher-density
fragility.

Chapter Fourteen: Reflexivity, Method Failure, and Black Swans
I. The strangest thing about markets is not that they err, but that they change themselves because people believe in
certain methods
The previous chapter repositioned price from a seemingly neutral number back to the intersection of Difference,
narrative, liquidity, and Subject boundaries. If that chapter holds, then the market is no longer a screen that simply
reflects reality, but a dynamic Structure that simultaneously compresses real-world Differences, Subject expectations,
and narrative intensity into price.

But once this is acknowledged, a more troublesome question immediately arises:

If price is not a pure mirror but a revelation of Difference and narrative, then might the cognition and methods within
the market in turn change the market itself?
The answer is: yes. And this is precisely where the market is most restless and most likely to cause everyone to
misjudge.

This means that many of the hardest problems in the investment world cannot be explained merely by “who got the
fundamentals right.” Because the market is not a dead object waiting to be seen through. It is an object that looks back
at you. How you understand it, how you trade it, what method you use to capture it—all of these will in turn change how
it operates.

This is the central problem this chapter addresses. It includes three tightly intertwined things:

Reflexivity: how cognition and reality rewrite each other
Method failure: why a strategy often starts to dull after being widely adopted
Black Swans: why those “low-probability but devastating” events always occur at the moment they least should
These three may appear to belong to different topics, but they all point to the same structural fact:

The market is not a passive object, but a system that can be collectively altered by participants, and which therefore
continuously deviates from stable observation conditions.
II. What is reflexivity: not “people make mistakes,” but cognition flows back into the object itself
The term “reflexivity” is easily reduced to psychology. As if it merely says: market participants always have biases,
fantasies, and emotions, so prices are irrational.

This is certainly not wrong, but it is not enough. If reflexivity were just “people make mistakes,” it would not go
deeper than behavioral finance. What is truly powerful about it is:

Definition 14.1: Reflexivity is the process by which a Subject’s understanding, judgment, and actions regarding the
market flow back into the market object itself, altering the market’s subsequent operating conditions.
The key here is not “understanding has errors,” but “understanding flows back in.”

One person being bullish doesn’t matter. Ten thousand people being bullish simultaneously and trading, financing,
leveraging, and reallocating assets accordingly—that changes market structure. One fund manager believing a certain
strategy is effective doesn’t matter. A large amount of capital believing and executing it changes the price path,
flattening the Difference structure the strategy originally relied on. One country believing in a certain market
narrative doesn’t matter. But when regulation, liquidity arrangements, policy communication, and institutional behavior
all unfold around this narrative, the market object itself has changed.

So the first core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 14.1: Reflexivity is not cognitive bias itself, but cognition flowing back into the object through action,
altering the object’s Structure.
This sentence is important because it transforms the market from “an object that is cognized” to “an object that can be
rewritten by cognition.” And once the object itself can be rewritten, the method of observing it cannot be as stable as
observing a rock.

III. Why markets are especially prone to reflexivity: because here, action can almost instantly rewrite others’ reality
Many domains in social life have recursion. Institutions, identity, state narratives, and organizational governance all
involve cognition flowing back into reality. But markets have something special: their feedback is extremely fast, and
almost every step can be monetized and settled.

A person buying is not just expressing a view—it also moves the price. Once the price moves, it affects other people’s
books, risk metrics, collateral capacity, stop-loss lines, and emotional judgments. These people then act again, pushing
the price further. That is, cognition in markets doesn’t first speak and then slowly influence reality—it often begins
changing reality the moment the button is pressed.

This makes the market one of the most reflexive, most sensitive, and most unstable social structures. Because here:

Narrative can directly become positions
Expectations can directly become leverage
Consensus can directly become price
Price in turn rewrites others’ situations
Thus, a very typical loop emerges:

View → Action → Price → Constraint change → New view → New action

This is the reflexivity chain in markets. It is shorter, faster, and sharper than many other social processes.

So the second judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 14.2: Markets are especially prone to reflexivity not because participants are more irrational, but because
cognition in markets can be almost instantaneously settled, rapidly rewriting others’ reality.
This also explains why markets appear more dramatic than many other social domains. They are not more insane—the
feedback chain is simply shorter.

IV. The two most common directions of reflexivity: self-reinforcement and self-negation
Since reflexivity exists, we can’t just say “it flows back”—we must also clarify its most common directions of movement.

Version 1.6 tightens this into two basic directions here.

First: self-reinforcement
Once a certain view is accepted by enough people, it produces through action a reality that more strongly supports it.
The more reality supports it, the more it is believed. Thus, cognition and object form a loop that feeds continuously in
the same direction.

Bubbles in markets almost always grow this way. “This industry represents the future.” “This type of asset is
increasingly scarce.” “This time is different.” “Risk is controllable.” These statements, if they were merely
statements, would be unremarkable. What is truly dangerous is when they begin entering financing, valuation,
positioning, and institutional allocation logic—then they produce price realities that support themselves.

So narrative is not simply lying—it is, in the short term, genuinely pushing the world in the direction of its own
claims. This is what makes bubbles most alluring: they are not pure lies, but lies that temporarily prove themselves
true.

Second: self-negation
The other direction is more subtle and more easily overlooked. That is: the more successful a certain cognition becomes,
the more it eliminates the Differences on which it originally depended, ultimately rendering itself ineffective.

An arbitrage strategy may work if only a few people use it; once everyone starts using it, the misalignment it relies on
gets compressed. A consensus that “stablecoin pegging is very safe,” if widely accepted, attracts increasingly fragile
capital and leverage structures, eventually turning the peg itself into a high-pressure node. A belief that “the central
bank will backstop,” if thoroughly believed, encourages more aggressive risk-taking, ultimately putting the backstop’s
own credibility under stress.

So the third key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 14.3: The two most common directions of reflexivity are cognition and reality mutually reinforcing each
other, or cognition gradually hollowing out its own effective premises through its own success.
These two directions will appear repeatedly. They can explain the vast majority of market booms, popular methods, risk
desensitization, and the strange calm before crashes.

V. Why methods fail: not because the market went bad, but because the method ate the Differences it depended on
This is one of the most frustrating and most illuminating facts in investment studies.

Any method that genuinely works faces the same fate: it ultimately changes the market it confronts.

This is not a curse—it is Structure.

A method works typically because it has captured some Difference that has not yet been fully exploited. For example:

A mispricing
A cognitive lag
A liquidity mismatch
An information asymmetry
A behavioral bias
A cyclically undervalued risk premium
As long as only a few people identify and exploit it, this Difference can still exist. But once the method is widely
disseminated and more and more Subjects use it to trade, allocate, hedge, and model, these pre-existing Differences are
rapidly compressed.

So the method did not suddenly and mysteriously fail. It simply ate, step by step, the space it originally lived on.

So the fourth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 14.4: The fundamental reason methods fail is not that the market suddenly became lawless, but that after
being widely adopted, the method gradually compresses the Differences that made it effective.
This sentence is extremely critical. Because it directly changes how you view investment methods, trading systems, and
academic anomaly factors.

You no longer understand “a method failing” as market caprice. You first ask:

What Difference did it originally exploit?
Have too many people already targeted these Differences?
Is the method still making money from the original Difference, or from a new crowded premium?
Is its failure local, temporary, or structural?
Once you start asking these questions, you stop treating methods as real estate. You treat them as stage-specific tools
within a Difference compression process.

VI. Why Black Swans always come “when they shouldn’t”: because the system is most fragile precisely when Differences
appear flattest
The term “Black Swan” has been used so often that it frequently becomes a vague exclamation. The truly interesting
question is not “will the world produce surprises,” but:

Why do the most destructive events almost always occur when the system is most confident, most stable, most certain that
risk has been managed?
The Theory of Difference’s answer here is very clear:

Because the system is most fragile precisely when key Differences have been temporarily flattened, suppressed, and made
to look unimportant.

Risk Differences appear small. Leverage Differences appear manageable. Liquidity Differences seem arranged. Credit
Differences seem smoothed by ratings, models, and insurance packaging. Subjects’ views of the future increasingly
resemble each other. Boundary fear is increasingly suppressed by “this time is different.” Methods become more unified,
narratives more singular, the market more smooth.

These look like order. But structurally they often mean something else: the system has lost its necessary Difference
buffer.

Once Differences are pressed to the point where “everyone is on the same side, same map, same expectation,” any event
that truly breaks this plane will catch the system unable to re-stratify in time. Pressures that were distributed across
multiple positions simultaneously squeeze toward the same exit. At this point, the Black Swan is no longer just an
“accident”—it is the systematic exposure of fragility after high-density Difference compression.

So the fifth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 14.5: Black Swans always come “when they shouldn’t” because the system is most fragile precisely when key
Differences have been temporarily flattened and appear to no longer matter.
This is also why stable periods should not always be understood as safe periods. Sometimes stability is just a pressure
cooker that appears silent. The real sound may have been pressed into invisible places.

VII. Black Swans are not pure accident—they always strike a node already saturated with tension
At this point, one more layer must be added, or we slide back into “nothing is predictable.”

Black Swans certainly carry contingency. Which specific day, which specific point, which specific trigger detonates
them—this is often genuinely difficult to pin down in advance. But this does not mean they are entirely without
Structure.

The most important property of a Black Swan is not “nobody could have imagined it,” but that it always strikes a node
already saturated with tension and with very poor absorption capacity.

A credit anchor
A category of high-leverage positions
A critical financial institution
An identity boundary
A political symbol
An over-trusted institutional arrangement
A liquidity chain mistaken for unbreakable
That is, a Black Swan does not fall randomly into a structurally stable, Difference-distributed, channel-clear system.
It always strikes, in a system that is already very fragile but simply hasn’t shown it yet, the point that least should
have been struck.

So the sixth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 14.6: Black Swans certainly carry contingent triggers, but their destructiveness comes from the system
having already accumulated high-density Difference at certain key nodes.
This sentence connects with the event theory from earlier. Chapter Eight stated that an event’s capacity to amplify
depends on whether it hits a critical junction point, whether it occurs at a moment of high tension, and whether the
system still has a Nearest Equilibrium that can absorb the shock. A Black Swan is merely the extreme expression of this
in the market world.

VIII. Why people always say afterward “there were actually signs”: because the signs were always there—the dominant

After every major crisis, there is a familiar atmosphere: large numbers of people look back through the materials and
say those signals actually existed all along. Leverage was already high. Valuation was already strange. Structure was
already twisted. Consensus was already too full. Risk had already been squeezed to the surface.

This is certainly true. The question is: why are these signs so often dismissed beforehand?

The answer is not that everyone is stupid. It is that the dominant narrative determines which signals count as signals.

When a narrative is dominant, things that support it are seen as “evidence,” while things that undermine it are seen as
“noise,” “temporary disturbance,” “local problems,” or “old thinking.”

So the signs were always there. They simply were not allowed, as “truly important signs,” to enter the system’s
judgment.

This is why market analysis cannot focus solely on data. Whether data is seen as risk depends heavily on whether it fits
the visibility structure permitted by the dominant narrative.

So the seventh key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 14.7: The reason pre-crisis signs are always seen afterward and overlooked beforehand is not that they
didn’t exist, but that the dominant narrative suppressed their importance beforehand.
This sentence actually brings the narrative theory from Chapter Nine completely into investment studies. There is no

Chapter Fifteen: State, Boundary, and the Difference Structure of International Conflict
I. The most confusing thing about international politics is not that states conflict, but why conflict always erupts at
the moment it least should
From this chapter on, Volume Three shifts from markets to international politics.

This leap looks large. One side is about prices, leverage, valuation, liquidity; the other is about sovereignty, war,
alliances, boundaries, nations, and international order. They speak different languages, belong to different research
traditions, and share almost no overlap in daily concerns.

But if the framework from the preceding chapters holds, this leap is actually not large. Because whether in markets or
international politics, both are running the same underlying mechanisms:

Differences cluster into Structures
Structures are cut open by Events
Narratives flow back into reality
Subjects prioritize boundary maintenance
Conservation ensures that suppressed Differences return elsewhere
Nearest Equilibria determine where the system first lands after a fracture
So the move from markets to international politics is not a change of domain—it is the same Difference dynamics revealed
at a larger scale.

And the most confusing question in international politics is highly isomorphic with the Black Swan question in markets:

Why do some international conflicts appear to erupt suddenly, yet afterward a long chain of pre-existing causes can
always be found?
The answer is still the same: the signs were always there, but the dominant narrative suppressed them. Differences were
always accumulating, but were covered over by surface order and temporary equilibria. Until some event cut through the
old Structure, and the system suddenly revealed what it had been accumulating all along.

So what this chapter needs to do is not retell international relations theory, but reorganize the hardest-to-explain
problems in international politics through the framework of the Theory of Difference.

II. What is a state: not a piece of territory, but a Subject Structure in which Differences are organized at massive
scale
When many people discuss the state, they first think of territory, population, government, military, and law. These are
certainly all components of the state. But from the perspective of the Theory of Difference, these are merely the
external forms of the state, not its deepest Structure.

The state’s deepest Structure is its capacity to organize Differences on an extremely large scale.

It organizes internal power Differences into a chain of obedience; organizes wealth Differences into a system of
taxation, property rights, and distribution; organizes identity Differences into a boundary of “who counts as us and who
doesn’t”; organizes narrative Differences into a Structure of what is permitted to be said and what is not; organizes
geographic Differences into a system of border management, resource control, and security defense.

So the state is not first a piece of land, then a group of people, then a government. It is: a Subject Structure that
stably organizes Differences at massive scale and can continuously maintain internal and external boundaries.

Definition 15.1: A State is a Subject Structure that stably organizes power, wealth, identity, and narrative Differences
at massive scale within a specific geographic range and can continuously maintain internal and external boundaries.
Once this definition holds, many questions about the state become clearer.

Why do some states appear to “have the name of a state without the substance”? Because they have not truly organized
their internal Differences stably—the obedience chain is broken, boundaries cannot be maintained. Why is the gap between
strong and weak states not merely military power, but deeper structural Difference? Because the real gap lies in who can
maintain more dimensions of Difference, over a wider range, at lower cost. Why do conflicts between states often go
beyond resource competition to boundary competition? Because the state as a Subject exists fundamentally to maintain
boundaries. Once boundaries are challenged, what it faces is not merely a loss of interests, but a threat to
Subjectivity itself.

III. Why states protect boundaries: because boundaries are not just lines, but the existential conditions of the state
as a Subject
Chapter Ten already explained that the Subject’s most fundamental action is not pursuing maximum benefit, but first
ensuring it can still exist as itself. States are no exception. In fact, because the state is the largest and most
complex Subject, its boundary maintenance logic is often stronger, more stubborn, and harder to replace with pure
interest calculation.

The state’s boundaries are not merely geographic lines. They include:

Territorial boundary: where counts as mine
Sovereignty boundary: who has the right to set rules here
Identity boundary: who counts as my people
Narrative boundary: what counts as a legitimate account of us
Institutional boundary: what kinds of external intervention are unacceptable
Once these boundaries are touched, the state’s response typically exceeds the range of pure interest calculation.
Because boundaries are not accessories—they are the existential skeleton of the state as a Subject.

So the first core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 15.1: The state’s maintenance of boundaries is not merely interest calculation, but a necessary expression
of Subject existential logic.
This explains many state actions that appear “irrational” to outsiders. A state may take economic losses but still
refuse to concede a certain piece of territory; a state may be at a military disadvantage but still refuse to accept a
certain sovereignty arrangement; a state may suffer trade damage but still refuse to accept a certain narrative
framework being imposed.

These behaviors may appear “irrational” from a pure interest perspective, but from the perspective of boundary
maintenance, they are entirely logical. Because once what is conceded is a boundary rather than a share, what the
Subject feels is not “a little less,” but “am I still me?”

IV. What is international order: not peace itself, but the Structure after Differences have been temporarily stably
arranged
The term “international order” is too easily spoken of as an ideal state. As if order is good, chaos is bad, and the
international community should pursue order and maintain peace.

This is certainly not wrong, but it is not deep enough.

From the perspective of the Theory of Difference, international order is first a structural fact, not a moral goal.

It means: in a given period, the power Differences, wealth Differences, identity Differences, and narrative Differences
between states are temporarily arranged into a relatively stable configuration. This configuration is not without
Difference—rather, Differences are organized into some sustainable chain of obedience, rule system, and shared
narrative.

So international order is not “Difference has disappeared,” but:

Definition 15.2: International order is the structural state in which the major Differences between states are
temporarily arranged into some sustainable configuration and recognized by a sufficient number of Subjects.
There are several key words in this definition.

“Temporarily”: there is no permanent order. Any international order is a Difference arrangement under specific
historical conditions. “Sufficient Subject recognition”: order is not maintained by coercion alone, but by enough
Subjects considering this arrangement more acceptable than alternatives. “Sustainable configuration”: not all
arrangements can last—only those that are continuously re-executed, internalized by Subjects, and institutionalized
count as true order.

Thus, the second judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 15.2: International order is not a state where Difference has disappeared, but structural stability after
Differences have been temporarily arranged into a sustainable configuration.
Once this sentence holds, many analyses of “order collapse” become clearer. Order does not collapse because “bad guys
appeared” or “rationality departed”—it collapses because the conditions supporting the original Difference arrangement
have changed: certain Differences begin exceeding the original configuration’s capacity to bear them, certain Subjects
begin feeling the existing arrangement’s costs outweigh its benefits, a certain narrative begins losing its penetrating
power, and a certain key node can no longer continue to hold.

V. Why alliances form: not because of shared values, but because of facing a common counter-Difference
This point was already seeded in Volume Two; here it must be fully unfolded in the context of international politics.

Many analyses of alliances like to emphasize “shared values,” “common interests,” and “shared institutions.” These all
have some merit, but the Theory of Difference proposes a more fundamental judgment here:

The most fundamental clustering force of an alliance is usually not shared value, but shared counter-Difference.
That is, what truly binds a group of states together is usually not “we all like the same thing,” but “we all face the
same threat, the same pressure, the same type of external Difference.”

During World War II, Britain, the United States, and the Soviet Union had vastly different values, political systems,
and ideologies. Yet they formed the strongest wartime alliance in history. Why? Because Nazi Germany, as a shared
counter-Difference, temporarily pushed their internal Differences to secondary positions.

During the Cold War, the United States maintained alliance-like relationships with many developing countries whose
political systems were completely different—some even military dictatorships. Why? Because the Soviet counter-Difference
was real.

Once the shared counter-Difference disappears, the internal Differences that were suppressed within the alliance
resurface. This is why after the Cold War, NATO, the EU, and various multilateral mechanisms all began experiencing new
internal fissures. Not because they suddenly went bad, but because the shared counter-Difference that had held them
together weakened, and internal Differences began reclaiming the foreground.

So the third core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 15.3: The true clustering force of an alliance usually comes from a shared counter-Difference, not from
shared values.
This sentence has very direct analytical value. When you see an alliance, don’t first ask “are their values
consistent”—first ask: what Difference do they face together? How real, how urgent, how difficult to face alone is this
Difference?

If this Difference is large enough and real enough, the alliance will be strong, even with many internal disagreements.
If this Difference begins to blur and disperse, the alliance will start to loosen, even if it continues to hold meetings
on the surface.

VI. Why international conflicts are often so hard to resolve through negotiation: because many conflicts have already
risen from the interest layer to the boundary layer
Chapter Ten already clarified this problem at the level of domestic identity conflict. The logic at the international
level is the same, but the scale is larger and the consequences harder to control.

Many conflicts between states appear on the surface to be disputes over resources, trade, territory, or security
interests. At this level, there is theoretically room for negotiation: you get a bit more, I get a bit less, some
compensation, some concession.

But once many conflicts persist, they rise from the interest layer to the boundary layer. Once they reach the boundary
layer, the original negotiation logic begins to fail.

Because at the boundary layer the question is no longer “how do we divide,” but:

Who has the right to set rules on this issue
Whose mode of existence is legitimate
Whose historical narrative is recognized
Whose sovereignty boundary cannot be rewritten
Once these questions enter, concession is no longer just “taking less”—it is perceived as “admitting the other side has
the right to rewrite me.”

So the fourth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 15.4: International conflicts are often so hard to resolve through negotiation not merely because interests
are difficult to reconcile, but because conflicts have often risen from the interest layer to the boundary layer.
This also explains why many international negotiations are technically sophisticated yet still fail to achieve lasting
reconciliation. Because negotiation handles interest terms, while what both sides truly cannot accept is the implicit
logic of a certain boundary rewriting.

VII. Why international order always plants imbalance at its most stable moment: because stability itself allows
Differences to continue accumulating
This is an extremely common yet rarely confronted pattern in international politics.

The more stable an international order, the more easily it makes people forget it is merely a temporary arrangement.
Subjects begin treating this arrangement as a natural state, its rules as universal rules, its narrative as objective
description, its boundaries as natural boundaries.

But at the same time, the Differences within this order have not stopped moving. Some states are accumulating power,
some are losing advantage, some narratives are losing persuasiveness, and some boundary arrangements are generating
friction with new real-world Differences.

Thus, the more stable the surface of an international order, the greater the structural tension accumulated within it.
Because stability allows these tensions to go unaddressed for longer.

This is the institutional double-edgedness at the international level. It stabilizes the order while simultaneously
pressing new Differences into the old configuration.

So the fifth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 15.5: The more stable an international order, the more easily the Differences continuously accumulating
within it are covered over by the dominant narrative, thereby delaying the moment when imbalance is addressed.
This sentence clarifies many historical turning points.

The “unipolar moment” after the Cold War appeared to be the most stable period of international order. But it was
precisely during that period that massive new Differences were accumulating: the rise of emerging economies,
globalization’s domestic distributional impact, the reactivation of identity politics, the quiet erosion of
institutional legitimacy, and the deep dissatisfaction of peripheral states with the existing order narrative. These did
not appear only after 2008—they were already growing during the most stable times. The dominant narrative simply kept
them outside the foreground.

VIII. Why changes in international order are often more sudden than expected: because the Nearest Equilibrium makes the

Long periods of apparent stability
Internal Differences continuously accumulating
An event cutting through the old Structure
The system rapidly sliding toward a new Nearest Equilibrium
Why does this happen?

Because the Subjects within international order—states—have extremely strong impulses to maintain boundaries and
preserve the status quo. No state willingly surrenders its advantageous position in the existing configuration. No

can still function.

So the system continues pressing Differences below the surface order through old inertia, old narratives, old obedience
chains, and old interest arrangements. Until some node where old inertia can no longer pay the cost.

At that point, change comes quickly. And where it first lands is usually not the ideal new order, but the nearest
arrangement that can first stabilize the situation.

So the sixth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 15.6: Changes in international order are often more sudden than expected because the system maintains

concentrated, discrete fashion.

IX. What Nearest Equilibrium means in international politics: once the old order loosens, where does the system first

The Theory of Difference’s answer is not an ideal blueprint, but: first look at where the Nearest Equilibrium is. Where

Chapter Sixteen: Order Cycles and the Non-Finality of History
I. The illusion history most readily produces is not progress, but the end state
Every era that has just won a great victory carries an almost instinctive impulse: to interpret its own victory as
history having finally given its answer.

When one system wins, people say it is the end of institutional competition. When one order expands, people say it is
the final form of universal order. When one narrative becomes the dominant narrative, people say history has completed
its most important learning process. Even after a war, a crisis, or a technological revolution, people are always
willing to believe: this time, the old cycle has finally been broken.

This impulse is not hard to understand. Because any stable order, if it lasts long enough, will shift in Subjects’
experience from “temporary arrangement” to “natural reality.” A generation that grows up within only one order easily
takes it for the way the world naturally is. This is especially true for the victors. Because victory does not merely
expand resources—it also expands interpretive power. Whoever wins has more opportunity to narrate their stage-specific
advantage as a universal truth.

But the Theory of Difference must, from the very beginning, restrain this impulse.

Proposition 16.1: History will not end, because Difference will not end; as long as Difference continues to be
generated, transferred, sedimented, and returned, any order can only be stage-specific.
This sentence is not an emotional judgment—it is the direct inference of everything the book has established so far,
applied at the scale of history.

If Difference is a precondition of existence, if Subjects always maintain boundaries, if Conservation prevents
Difference from being zeroed out at no cost, if institutions can only suppress, distribute, and delay Difference but
never permanently eliminate it, then what is called “the end of history” is structurally impossible.

What can end is only one round of manifest confrontation. What cannot end is Difference’s continual revelation after
changing shells, positions, and pathways.

So this chapter is not saying “history always repeats.” It says something more precise:

History does not repeat the same content—it is Difference repeatedly organizing, compressing, erupting, and reorganizing
within different Structures.
II. Why every order believes it is closer to the end state: because once an order stabilizes, it mistakes its
stage-specific arrangement for universal nature
Once an order is established, it does not only organize resources—it organizes perception. It tells people:

What counts as normal
What counts as exception
What counts as mature
What counts as backward
What futures are imaginable
What regressions are unacceptable
That is, an order is never merely an external set of rules—it is also a device for interpreting reality that enters
Subjects’ perceptual Structure.

When this interpretive device runs smoothly, people find it increasingly difficult to see it as “a particular
arrangement.” It looks increasingly like “reality itself.”

This is why dominant orders in history almost always develop some sense of finality. The Romans felt their order
approached the completion of civilization. Early modern Europe felt the sovereign state system was the universal form of
mature politics. The liberal international order after the Cold War was also for a time understood by many as the high
point of institutional evolution. It is not that these orders achieved nothing—it is that once they succeed, they tend
to rewrite their own success as history’s final grammar.

So the second judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 16.2: Every stable order tends to mistake its stage-specific success for history’s universal end state.
This is not a problem unique to any one civilization, any one system, or any one ideology. It is a structural tendency
of order itself. Because the more stable an order, the more it can naturalize itself. The more it naturalizes itself,
the harder it is to see what Differences it suppresses. And the less it sees these Differences, the more it believes the
truly big problems have been solved.

III. Why history does not advance linearly: because Difference does not accumulate along a single axis, but moves
simultaneously along multiple axes
Ideas like “the end of history” always arise partly because people easily understand history as single-axis movement. As
if the world always has a single yardstick—productivity, rationalization, marketization, legalization, secularization,
or technological complexity—and as long as this one axis keeps advancing, history keeps approaching a more completed
state.

The Theory of Difference holds fundamental reservations about this single-axis view of history. Because in reality,
Difference never moves along a single axis.

Productivity increases, but that does not mean identity conflicts decrease. Institutions become more complex, but that
does not mean Subject anxiety declines. Global connections tighten, but that does not mean boundary consciousness
weakens. Markets develop further, but that does not mean political conflict is automatically neutralized.

Quite the opposite: advancement along one axis often creates new high-density Differences on another axis. Economic
integration may compress costs but re-create center–periphery Differences; technological progress may raise efficiency
but amplify distributional inequality and identity anxiety; institutional rationalization may reduce one type of chaos
but intensify another type of loss of control and de-subjectivization.

So the third core judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 16.3: History does not advance linearly because Difference always moves simultaneously along multiple axes,
and progress on one axis often creates new tension on another.
This sentence is critical. It means there is no general shortcut in history of the form “once we develop to this point,
other problems will naturally be solved.” What counts as “solving” is usually just transferring the problem from one
form of Difference to another.

This is not pessimism. It is the minimum honesty required by complex social structures.

IV. Order cycles do not mean spinning in place—they mean the system always oscillates between stability, accumulation,
fracture, and reorganization
Saying “history does not end” is often misheard as “history is just a cycle.” But if “cycle” is not clarified, it also
creates misunderstanding. It easily evokes mechanical repetition: dynastic succession, hegemonic rotation,
war–peace–war, round and round like a pendulum swinging back to its starting point.

What the Theory of Difference describes is not this kind of simple cycle. It describes a more structural oscillation:

Differences are organized into some order
The order runs stably and suppresses certain Differences
Suppressed Differences transfer, sediment, change form
The old order becomes increasingly unable to handle new Differences
An event cuts through the Structure
The system slides toward a new Nearest Equilibrium
The new order reorganizes Differences
A new round of stability begins
This is not a return to the starting point. Because after each round of reorganization, the distribution of Differences,
the positions of Subjects, the forms of institutions, and the contents of narratives have all changed. Nor is it
infinite linear ascent. Because each round of stability accumulates material for the next round of fracture.

So the fourth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 16.4: Order cycles are not mechanical repetition of content, but structural oscillation between stability,
accumulation, fracture, and reorganization.
This definition is important because it frees “cycle” from fatalism. Cycle is not destiny—it is the historical form of
motion produced by Difference Conservation, boundary maintenance, institutional double-edgedness, and event cutting
acting together.

V. Why victory always breeds the next crisis: because victory most readily desensitizes Subjects to residual Differences
This point has already been discussed in markets, institutions, and international order. Now it must be gathered at the
scale of history.

The most important consequence of a victory—political, institutional, economic, military, ideological—is often not
merely that it eliminated the old opponent, but that it changed how the victor perceives the world.

Victors become more confident in their own methods. More confident in their own narrative. More willing to interpret
dissent as local noise. More likely to treat Differences that originally needed serious attention as secondary issues,
transitional problems, local costs, or even “the natural reaction of the backward who can’t adapt to the new order.”

So the real danger of victory lies not first in arrogance, but in its systematic desensitization of the Subject to
residual, peripheral, and emergent Differences.

When this sensitivity declines, the order appears smoother. But precisely because of this, many genuinely destructive
tensions remain out of sight for longer.

So the fifth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 16.5: Victory always breeds the next crisis not merely because it creates enemies, but because it
desensitizes the victor to residual Differences.
This sentence is more precise than “prosperity leads to decline.” It is not that the universe dislikes success—it is
that success changes the system’s perceptual Structure. And once the perceptual Structure dulls, Differences grow more
easily in unseen places.

VI. Why failure does not automatically bring learning: because failure can equally push Subjects back toward narrower
boundary defense
If victory does not mean the end state, we must also prevent another misunderstanding: failure does not automatically
mean progress.

Many historical narratives like to believe “failure educates people.” As if after a crisis, a war, a crash, an
institutional breakdown, society will automatically become wiser, more mature, more reflective.

Reality is far from this optimistic. Failure can certainly bring learning, but it can equally bring narrower boundary
defense, stronger scapegoating mechanisms, and more urgent identity hardening.

Because when a Subject feels its boundary damaged in failure, its first response may not be open learning—it may be to
stop losses first, retract first, redraw the line between “us” and “them” first. So failure may bring review, or it may
bring more closed self-protection.

States after failure may not become more open—they may become more nationalistic. Markets after crashes may not become
more rational—they may become more short-sighted. Institutions after being damaged may not become more inclusive—they
may become more punitive. Individuals after setbacks may not become more mature—they may become more stubborn.

So the sixth judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 16.6: Failure does not automatically bring learning, because Subjects whose boundaries have been damaged
often first move toward narrower defense, not deeper reflection.
This point is important. It means history does not automatically improve through “suffering.” Learning requires
structural conditions, narrative space, institutional containers, and sufficient buffers. Without these, failure is
likely to merely push the system toward another harder Nearest Equilibrium.

VII. What is “progress,” really: not the disappearance of Difference, but a temporary increase in the system’s capacity
to handle Difference
At this point, we must distinguish “the non-finality of history” from “whether progress exists.” Otherwise we slide into

Volume IV: Method, Limits, and Openness
Chapter 17: Method Is Not Standing Outside the World, but Learning to Judge from Within Difference
People most easily fall into the belief that a truly reliable method should be like standing on high ground looking at a
map — first retreating outside reality, then using a neat enough toolkit to explain it. This wish is understandable.
Standing outside means clarity, and clarity often tricks people into thinking they already have control.

But the Theory of Difference has been saying from the very beginning that the Subject is not a spectator standing
outside the world. The Subject is itself part of the Difference-structure. When you observe the world, you do not merely
see Difference — you enter Difference carrying your own position, boundaries, interests, experience, and fears. The
judgments you make are not just descriptions of an object; they are simultaneously a projection, a cutting, and a wager
by you as a Subject within the structure.

So the first premise of method is not pure neutrality, but the admission that no judgment is entirely positionless.

Proposition 17.1: Method does not extract the Subject from the world; it allows the Subject, on the premise of
acknowledging its own positionality, to form judgments that are as testable, correctable, and consequence-bearing as
possible.
This definition is critical. It pulls “method” back from a posture of external overview into the interior of reality. It
no longer promises a God’s-eye view. What it demands is something stricter: you must know where you stand, why you see
things this way, how wrong you could be if you are wrong, and whether you have the capacity to bear the consequences of
that error.

A true method does not make people lose their position. It makes people more awake to the position they already hold.

I. Why the Harder You Chase Absolute Objectivity, the More Easily You Slip into Your Own Blind Spots
The moment people discuss method, many rush toward “absolute objectivity.” This wish has its legitimacy — behind it lies
a vigilance against arbitrariness, bias, and self-deception. But the problem is that once someone interprets “absolute
objectivity” as “I no longer have a position,” they become less able to discover that their position is still at work.

If a person admits they have a standpoint, there is still a chance for self-correction. If a person firmly believes they
have fully transcended standpoint, their standpoint will usually operate in a more hidden, more stubborn way.

Institutional designers think they are merely discussing efficiency, when in reality they may have written a certain
class position into “common sense.” Investors think they are merely reading data, when in reality they may have packaged
emotion and desire as “rational judgment.” Intellectuals think they are merely offering universal principles, when in
reality they may have mistaken a particular life experience for the human experience itself.

So the danger in methodology is not just subjectivity — it is the subjectivity that refuses to acknowledge itself as
subjective.

Proposition 17.2: Denying positionality does not eliminate bias; it only deprives bias of the chance to expose itself.
This sentence carries serious weight. It requires method to first possess a kind of honesty: not to begin by saying “I
have no bias,” but to begin by saying “I may be especially prone to bias in these particular places.”

This does not weaken judgment. On the contrary, it gives judgment less disguise and more resilience.

II. Method’s First Task Is Not Omniscience, but Learning to Cut the Object Apart
Facing a complex world, people most commonly fail not because they are entirely ignorant, but because they mix together
things from different layers. Treating short-term fluctuations as long-term trends. Treating narrative heat as
structural change. Treating institutional consequences as institutional intent. Treating individual motives as
collective outcomes. Treating the impact of a single event as proof that an entire era has been rewritten.

The root of such problems lies not in insufficient information, but in the object not having been cut apart.

So the second task of method is not to first collect unlimited material, but to first ask: what layer of object am I
actually facing right now? Am I speaking at the ontological layer, the cognitive layer, the institutional layer, the
distributional layer, the narrative layer, or the event layer? If layers are not separated, any material will only pile
up into greater confusion.

Proposition 17.3: The first technique of method is not completeness, but cutting apart; not fitting everything into an
explanation, but first judging which layer Difference is primarily revealing itself at.
This is what the entire Theory of Difference has been doing. It is not meant to make the world sound more mystical, but
to prevent different layers from impersonating each other. Much intellectual confusion stems not from insufficient
concepts, but from unclear layers.

Only with cutting apart can comparison become possible. Only with comparison can judgment emerge. Only with judgment can
correction be discussed.

III. So-Called Explanatory Power Is Often Just “Explaining What Already Happened”; True Method Must Face the Incomplete

of the time, what is called explanatory power is really just after-the-fact narration. Once things have happened,
rearranging the material can always produce a seemingly coherent story. What is truly difficult is not telling a tidy
story after the fact, but identifying — while things are still unfinished — which Differences are accumulating, which
equilibria are loosening, and which narratives are overextending.

Put differently, method must not only explain completed states; it must also handle incomplete states.

incomplete, even when partially wrong, it can still help the Subject avoid the kind of large errors that destroy its own

Chapter 18: Possibility-Space Analysis: Not Predicting the One Future, but Identifying Which Futures Have Structural

The first three volumes discussed how Difference generates, transfers, precipitates, and flows back; how narrative

Therefore, Volume IV must here explicitly present a core method. This method is not meant to provide oracles for people,
nor to create the illusion of “I already know the future.” What it truly aims to do is reopen, within complex reality,
those possibility-Differences that have been obscured by surface facts, and judge which directions have structural
carrying conditions and which, though imaginable, are for now merely wishes in language.

This is the method this chapter proposes:

Definition 18.1: Possibility-space analysis does not predict which outcome must occur; within the given
Difference-structure, Subject capacities, institutional constraints, resource distributions, and narrative boundaries,
it analyzes which outcomes are genuinely possible and which are imaginable but not structurally supportable.
This definition must be read slowly. Because what it changes is not merely analytical technique, but one of the most
common ways people misjudge reality.

People are most prone to misreading possibility in two kinds of moments. The first is when order is stable. At such
times, people mistake the already-realized reality for the only possible reality, as if everything before them naturally
had to be this way. The second is when order loosens. At such times, people mistake the outcome they most desire for the
one most likely to arrive.

The first misjudgment comes from inertia. The second comes from wish. Possibility-space analysis is precisely the method
for resisting both simultaneously. It neither submits to the status quo nor surrenders to desire. What it demands is not
a side-taking judgment, but a structural judgment.

I. Why People Always Mistake Reality for the Only Possibility: Because Stability Naturalizes Itself
Any stable order, if it lasts long enough, will gradually deprive those living within it of the ability to perceive
“other possibilities.” When institutions are stable for a long time, people assume institutions were always meant to be
this way. When markets rise continuously, people assume rising is the basic state. When an international configuration
is maintained for a long time, people assume that is the world order itself. When a dominant narrative holds sway for a
long time, people assume it is common sense.

This is not because people are stupid, but because stability itself changes the structure of perception. When some
arrangement continuously organizes reality, it ceases to be merely an arrangement and gradually masquerades as the
natural environment. And once the environment is naturalized, people no longer ask “is there another possibility?” —
they only adjust details within the existing order.

This is the first illusion that possibility-space analysis must break.

Proposition 18.1: Reality is often mistaken for the only possibility not because it truly is the only one, but because
stable structures disguise themselves as natural states.
This sentence is important. It means the first step of method is not immediately predicting change, but first re-seeing
the fact that reality is merely one realized branch. The order before you is not the way the world inherently is — it is
just one arrangement among many that has temporarily become real.

This shifts analysis from “accepting the given reality” toward “reopening the structural field of vision.”

II. But Possibility Is Not Fantasy: Not Every Future That Can Be Spoken Is a True Possibility
Once we say “reality is not the only possibility,” people easily slide to the other extreme: as if since it is not the
only one, every conceived alternative is equally possible. This too is wrong.

Because “imaginable” does not equal “possible.” Language can outrun reality, and desire is always freer than structure.
A result that is conceptually coherent is easily mistaken for a real possibility. But the real world is not pushed
forward by concepts — it is pushed forward by carrying conditions.

This is the second distinction this chapter must make explicit:

Proposition 18.2: A genuine possibility depends not on whether it can be conceived, but on whether it has structural
carrying conditions.
Structural carrying conditions refer to the requirements for a result to become reality: there must be some chain of
forces to support it. Subjects must be willing to act. Resources must be mobilizable. Institutions must either support
it or at least not completely block it. Narrative must provide some legitimacy. And there must be some continuity on the
path from here to there.

If none of these conditions exist, then even the most moving plan is merely an image in thought, not a possible branch
in reality.

So possibility-space analysis is never a romantic method. It does not aim to inspire. Its job is to distinguish: what
can enter the competition for reality, and what merely remains a projection of wish at the level of language.

III. The First Step of Possibility-Space Analysis: Separating “What Has Already Happened” from “What Could Still Happen”
Reality has a strong sense of pressure. Once something has already happened, people instinctively treat it as heavier
and more real than things that have not happened. This has its reasonableness — the realized result has indeed organized
resources, emotions, institutions, and expectations. But if analysis is entirely bound by realized facts, it loses the
ability to perceive unexpanded branches.

So the first thing method must do is separate two questions in cognition:

First, what has already happened? Second, under the current structure, what else could happen?

These two questions cannot be mixed. The former is reality recognition; the latter is space recognition. Many analyses
fail not because they do not know reality, but because they only know reality.

Proposition 18.3: The starting point of possibility-space analysis is not supplementing reality with imagination, but
clearly separating “what has been realized” from “branches not yet closed.”
This step seems simple but is actually extremely difficult. Because reality constantly induces people to believe: once
this path has been walked, other paths do not exist. But the Theory of Difference requires the reverse question: why was
this path walked? What carrying conditions does it depend on? Are those conditions stable, or are they loosening? If
they loosen, which previously inconspicuous branches will suddenly become more viable?

Once pursued this way, the possibility space truly opens.

IV. The Possibility Space Is Not Uniform: Some Branches Are Near, Some Far, Some Nearly Sealed
Here we must go further. When we say “open the possibility space,” we do not mean all possibilities sit equally, waiting
for random realization. The possibility space in reality is never uniformly distributed. It has dense and sparse areas,
near and far zones, open and closed sections.

Some outcomes are easy to bring about not because they are wiser, but because they are closer to the current structure
and the resistance to slide toward them is lower. Some outcomes remain on paper for long periods not because they are
worthless, but because the rupture between them and existing structure is too great and there are not enough
transitional bridges. Still others appear to still be discussed, but the Subject capacities, institutional containers,
resource bases, and narrative legitimacy they require have all been severely depleted — these branches are structurally
close to being closed.

So possibility-space analysis is not about making lists; it is about judging the distance between branches and the
strength of their carrying conditions.

Proposition 18.4: The possibility space is not a flat wish list, but a non-uniform structure shaped by distance,
resistance, carrying conditions, and boundary constraints.
Once this is grasped, method immediately shifts from “vague openness” to “openness with a center of gravity.” You stop
asking “anything is theoretically possible” and begin asking: which possibility is closest to reality? Which possibility
must pass through several institutional gates? Which possibility is distant but gaining new carrying conditions? Which
possibility looks lively on the surface but has already lost its real-world entry point?

This is genuine structural judgment.

V. Possibility Must Be Layered: Logical, Narrative, Institutional, Structural, Near-Term, Long-Term
To prevent the word “possibility” from being used too loosely, this chapter must stratify it. Because possibilities in
different senses are fundamentally not the same thing. A result that is logically possible does not equal one that is
institutionally possible. A narrative possibility does not equal a near-term structural possibility. Without
stratification, analysis will keep slipping between layers.

Therefore, this chapter offers its most important layered framework.

Definition 18.2: Possibility-space analysis must distinguish at least six types of possibility.
The first type: logical possibility. As long as a result is not self-contradictory in concept, it is logically possible.
This is the widest layer. Many intellectual discussions remain here and therefore seem especially free. But logical
possibility is usually very far from real possibility.

The second type: narrative possibility. If a result can already be told, imagined, and organized into a transmissible
story, it has narrative possibility. Narrative possibility means it has already entered the boundary of public
perception and can begin to mobilize emotion, identity, and expectation. But being speakable does not equal being
doable.

The third type: institutional possibility. A result has institutional possibility only if it has a legitimate channel
within the existing institutional framework, or at least a translatable institutional interface. Otherwise, no matter
how much social sentiment exists, it may be stuck before the institutional threshold.

The fourth type: structural possibility. This is the most critical layer. It requires a result not only to be
institutionally accessible, but to have realistic support in terms of resource distribution, Subject capacities,
interest alignment, and path continuity. Whether a result has structural possibility determines whether it is “a
genuinely competing future branch.”

The fifth type: near-term possibility. This is a result that could be reached within a relatively short time frame under
current constraints. It is usually highly correlated with Minimal Variation and Nearest Equilibrium. Many imperfect but
first-to-occur realities belong to this type.

The sixth type: long-term possibility. Some results currently lack sufficient support but are not forever impossible. If
certain structural conditions gradually change, they may enter the competitive space in the future. This type of
possibility must be preserved but must not be mistaken for something imminent.

This stratification is important because it greatly increases analytical precision. When people argue about “whether
something is possible,” their disagreement often stems not from genuine divergence but from having never clarified which
type of possibility they are discussing.

Proposition 18.5: All serious possibility analysis must first declare which layer of possibility it is discussing;
otherwise conclusions easily slip between different levels.
This sentence may seem technical, but it is actually the core of methodological clarity.

VI. Possibility Analysis Is Not a Static Map, but Observing Which Conditions Are Loosening, Stacking, and Turning
One more step forward. The possibility space is not fixed once drawn. Because structural conditions in reality are
themselves changing. Resources move, narratives expand, institutions loosen or tighten, Subjects learn, fear, unite, or
split. Therefore, the true method is not listing all possibilities once, but continuously observing: which conditions
are strengthening, which are weakening, which previously disconnected factors are beginning to stack on each other.

This means possibility analysis is not static mapmaking — it is dynamic tracking.

Proposition 18.6: The key to possibility-space analysis lies not only in listing branches, but in tracking which
carrying conditions are accumulating, thereby allowing previously marginal branches to gradually enter real-world
competition.
This is especially important in social change. Many historical turning points do not manifest, before they erupt, as
“the result has already appeared.” They manifest only as several conditions beginning to change simultaneously:
scattered grievances being strung together by narrative, marginal groups finding organizational interfaces, rigid
institutions developing cracks, Subjects who dared not act seeing each other. Each of these changes alone is
insufficient, but once they stack, a previously distant branch suddenly draws close.

So what method must watch is not surface excitement, but the speed at which carrying conditions converge.

VII. Minimal Variation–Nearest Equilibrium: The Landing Principle Within Possibility Space
Now this chapter must connect with the most important methodological cornerstone of the entire book. After the
possibility space is opened, analysis cannot stop. Because “what possibilities exist” does not equal “where the system
is most likely to first head.” Possibility-space analysis answers the question of branch sets; Minimal Variation–Nearest
Equilibrium answers the question of real-world landing points.

When a system loosens, it does not automatically move toward the value-wise best branch, nor toward the linguistically
most moving branch. It usually first slides toward the direction of less resistance, lower cost, and the ability to
quickly form local stability. That is to say, among all still-open possibilities, the first to become real is usually
not the “best” one, but the one that is “nearest and can hold steady.”

Proposition 18.7: Possibility-space analysis answers “which futures are still open,” while the Minimal Variation–Nearest
Equilibrium principle answers “which open branch the system is most likely to first land on.”
These two must not be conflated. If you only discuss equilibrium landing points, the method appears too conservative, as
if reality can only ever move toward the nearest answer. If you only discuss possibility space, the method appears too
loose, as if all unclosed branches are equally worth betting on. Only by combining the two does judgment become both
open and centered.

This is also why the methodology of The Theory of Difference is not simple trend judgment, but a layered judgment: first
open the space, then judge distance, then estimate resistance, and finally identify the landing point.

VIII. The Most Important Use of This Method Is Not Magical Prediction, but Preventing Two Most Common Cognitive
Disasters
The most valuable place of possibility-space analysis is not that it can always precisely predict the future. The future
will never be fully captured by any method. Its true value lies in helping Subjects avoid two of the most common and
most dangerous cognitive disasters.

The first disaster is status-quo fatalism: treating the current existing order, distribution, and power relations as
unchangeable fate. This thinking makes people underestimate structural loosening, underestimate the accumulation of
marginal conditions, and underestimate moments when new branches suddenly open.

The second disaster is wishful necessitarianism: automatically believing that because some outcome is more just, more
beautiful, or more aligned with value, it will therefore happen. This thinking makes people miswrite moral preference as
real-world probability, smuggling the ought into the possible.

Proposition 18.8: The greatest real-world value of possibility-space analysis lies not in replacing the future, but in
simultaneously preventing status-quo fatalism and wishful necessitarianism.
This sentence is well suited for Volume IV. Because it reconnects methodology with all the real-world analysis from the
first three volumes. In markets, many people die from treating a rally as eternal, or treating their hoped-for price as
inevitable. In politics, many people die from treating the old order as destiny, or treating an ideal plan as imminent.
In international relations, many people die from treating the current configuration as final, or treating value
narrative as structural reality.

The place where method can truly save people is never by giving them eyes that foresee the future, but by helping them
avoid the kind of large errors that derail their entire judgment structure.

IX. The Landing Point of This Chapter: Possibility-Space Analysis Is the Method That Puts Reality Back into “Not Yet
Completed”
At this point, the chapter has said enough. Possibility-space analysis, in the end, is the refusal to treat the world as
a finished text. It requires us to acknowledge: reality certainly has its thickness, but reality is not the only
possibility; the future certainly cannot be imagined at will, but neither has the future been closed.

Therefore, this method differs from fatalism and also from fantasy. It neither bows before the given reality nor
sacrifices itself to abstract desire. It simply requires people to continuously return to a harder but more reliable
position: to see structural support, to see condition changes, to see which branches are drawing close, to see where the
system is most likely to first stabilize.

This is not an easy method. Because it does not let people comfortably believe any simple answer. But it is a method
closer to reality. And what Volume IV needs is precisely this composed, clear-eyed analytical force.

Chapter 19: Recursive Narrative Analysis: How Narrative Organizes Reality, and How Reality Rewrites Narrative in Return
Chapter 18 established one method: facing reality, we cannot only stare at what has already happened, but must reopen
branches not yet closed, identifying which futures genuinely have structural support and which are merely wishes in
language. But this is still not enough. Because possibilities in reality do not sit quietly waiting to be selected. Many
possibilities enter the competition not only because resource conditions, institutional conditions, Subject capacities,
and path distances have changed, but because someone has told these changes as a story that can be believed,
transmitted, and executed.

That is to say, the possibility space is not a purely objective map. It is always being illuminated, amplified,
obscured, distorted, and rearranged by narrative. Certain originally marginal branches suddenly gain the capacity for
action through narrative organization; certain apparently powerful paths rapidly lose their support through narrative
failure. So Volume IV must introduce a second core method: not asking “what is reality,” nor merely asking “what
possibilities remain,” but further pursuing:

How does narrative enter reality, organize Subject action, change Difference distribution, and get strengthened,
backlash-ed, or drifted in real-world feedback?

This is the method this chapter proposes.

Definition 19.1: Recursive narrative analysis does not treat narrative as an ancillary description of reality, but as a
participatory force capable of organizing expectations, coordinating action, shaping boundaries, and in turn rewriting
the structure of reality.
This definition must be carefully grasped. Because it is not the same thing as the everyday understanding that
“narrative matters.” We are not saying that language affects emotion, nor that propaganda sways judgment, nor reducing
narrative to surface rhetoric. What we are truly saying is: in the social world, narrative enters Subject expectations;
expectations change modes of action; action changes resource flows, institutional pressures, group boundaries, and price
structures; and these changes in turn validate, weaken, or distort the original narrative. That is what makes it
recursive.

So narrative is not a layer of smoke outside reality. Narrative is itself part of the mechanism through which reality is
generated.

I. Why Possibility-Space Analysis Alone Is Not Enough: Because Possibilities Do Not Happen by Being Seen — They Must Be
Organized into Action
Chapter 18 already showed that reality is not the only possibility, and the future is not any arbitrary possibility. But
the last step is still missing: even if a branch genuinely has structural support, it will not automatically realize
itself. Structural conditions only provide “happenability” — they do not automatically supply the “engine of happening.”

This engine, in many cases, is narrative. Because Subjects do not directly react to structural diagrams. Subjects always
understand their position, the risks they face, the opportunities they hold, and who they should stand with or against
through some world-picture that has been told to them.

A possibility that has not been organized by narrative often remains mere potential within structure. And potential that
does not enter consensus, belief, expectation, and coordinated action will long remain in a state of “could happen but
has not happened.”

So the first judgment of this chapter must be:

Proposition 19.1: The reason possibility can transform from structural potential into a real-world branch is usually not
that it was seen, but that it was organized by narrative into executable common expectations.
This sentence is critically important. It truly connects Chapter 18 and Chapter 19. Possibility-space analysis answers
which futures have structural carrying conditions. Recursive narrative analysis further answers how these structural
possibilities are illuminated, amplified, and converted into the real-world momentum of coordinated Subject action.

If Chapter 18 provides the “sense of the map,” then Chapter 19 provides the “sense of the engine.”

II. The First Force of Narrative: Not Explaining the World, but Assigning Positions Within It
People usually think narrative’s main function is “explaining things.” But in social reality, the earlier and deeper
layer of narrative’s force is not explaining events themselves — it is arranging positions for Subjects.

Any powerful narrative simultaneously answers these questions:

Who are we?
Who are they?
What have we lost?
What should we hold on to?
Why are we where we are today?
In what direction should change occur?
Who is responsible for our current困境?
That is to say, narrative does not merely add annotations to reality — it provides coordinates for the actors within
reality. And once coordinates are determined, the Subject’s feelings, alliances, anger, endurance, willingness to
sacrifice, and action thresholds are all rewritten.

Therefore, the first thing recursive narrative analysis must look at is not whether a narrative “is correct,” but how it
arranges positions, redraws boundaries, and defines friends and enemies.

Proposition 19.2: The primary force of narrative lies not in how many facts it explains, but in how it assigns positions
to Subjects, defines boundaries, and organizes friend-enemy relations.
This is where The Theory of Difference differs sharply from ordinary discourse analysis. We do not understand narrative
as the surface of text — we see it as a boundary-arrangement device. Once boundaries are redrawn by narrative,
Difference clusters along the new cut.

III. Narrative Has Real-World Force Because It Can Compress Dispersed Feelings into Coordinated Action
In many historical moments, dissatisfaction, anxiety, loss, humiliation, and a sense of opportunity in reality are
dispersed. Everyone vaguely senses some problem, but these feelings have not yet formed a structure that can be
transmitted, confirmed, and coordinated. At this point, even if Difference is already deep, the system may not
immediately change, because dispersed feelings have not yet been compressed into common action.

Narrative’s role is precisely to compress this dispersed state. It tells a scattered set of experiences as a single
causal line; it names Subjects who did not recognize each other as the same “us”; it transforms local discomfort into
shared fate; it converts vague expectations into a direction that can be followed.

So narrative is not just expressing emotion — it is organizing action-consistency.

Proposition 19.3: The real-world force of narrative comes from its ability to compress dispersed feelings, local losses,
and scattered expectations into coordinateable common expectations.
This is also why many structural conditions clearly exist yet fail to reveal themselves, while some changes suddenly
accelerate once they are spoken aloud. Not because narrative creates reality out of nothing, but because it makes
previously scattered real-world factors begin to see each other, confirm each other, and follow each other.

So recursive narrative analysis must pay special attention to whether a given narrative has the capacity to turn
fragmented feelings into coordinated action. If not, it may be mere emotional noise; if so, it has already entered the
process of reality-generation.

IV. Narrative Changes Reality Not Through Truth or Falsity, but Through Its Capacity to Stably Organize Expectations
Here the chapter must become more forceful. Many people like to discuss narrative from a true/false angle, as if a
sufficiently true narrative will automatically be effective, and an untrue one is doomed to fail. Reality is far more
complex.

Narrative certainly cannot fully detach from reality, or it will eventually lose support. But in the actual running of
reality, the direct force of narrative comes not first from truth-judgment, but from whether it can stably organize
expectations. If a narrative can make enough Subjects act on it, it is already changing the object. Financial markets
are especially clear: narratives about inflation, growth, technological revolution, or policy turns — as long as enough
people believe and act — will rewrite prices, allocations, risk preferences, and capital flows. Politics and
international relations are the same. As long as some narrative stably enough defines threat, interest, glory, and
shame, it begins rearranging alliances and changing institutional thresholds and tolerance for action.

So the fourth key judgment of this chapter is:

Proposition 19.4: The force of narrative depends not mainly on whether it is entirely true, but on whether it can, over
a period of time, stably organize Subject expectations and coordinate real-world action.
This is not relativism, nor does it say truth and falsehood do not matter. It says that in the social world, once
narrative enters the action system, it can no longer be treated as pure description. It becomes part of reality, and
part of reality must be measured by the consequences it generates.

V. The Key to Recursion: Once Narrative Enters Reality, Reality Begins Rewriting Narrative in Return
If narrative only influences reality in one direction, it is not yet recursive. What truly makes recursion work is:
narrative drives action, action changes reality, and once reality feeds back, the original narrative cannot stay the
same. It will be strengthened, patched, distorted, swallowed, or entirely backlash-ed by reality.

This is the unfolding of the fifth axiom at the methodological level. Narrative is not an arrow shot at reality — it is
a circuit that loops back to narrative itself. The story you tell, once executed, produces new facts in the object; and
these facts determine whether the story becomes more credible, more radical, more fragmented, or more hollow.

Therefore, what recursive narrative analysis must capture is not static narrative content, but the feedback loop that
forms after narrative enters reality.

Proposition 19.5: The core of recursive narrative analysis is not analyzing what narrative says, but analyzing what kind
of feedback loop narrative forms after entering reality.
This requires the analyst to stop treating narrative as text, and instead treat it as a process: how it enters Subjects,
how it changes action, how it produces real-world consequences, and how those consequences reshape the original
narrative.

Once seen this way, many things that appeared to be merely “propaganda,” “public opinion,” or “ideology” will reveal
their structural force.

VI. Recursive Feedback Usually Has Three Outcomes: Reinforcement, Backlash, and Drift
To make the method truly operational, this chapter must clearly specify the basic result types of recursive narrative.
Otherwise “recursion” remains abstract description.

Definition 19.2: After narrative enters reality, its feedback usually moves toward three basic outcomes.
The first: reinforcement. Narrative guides action, and the real-world consequences of action validate the original
narrative, making it stronger, Subject belief more stable, and more people continue acting on it. This forms a
self-reinforcing loop. Rising expectations in markets, victory narratives in politics, and deterrence narratives in
international relations may all experience this phase.

The second: backlash. Narrative guides action, but the consequences produced by action conflict with what the narrative
promised. The original narrative begins losing credibility, even triggering stronger reverse emotions. Language
originally used for mobilization may ultimately become evidence for dismantling legitimacy.

The third: drift. Narrative is neither fully validated nor completely negated, but gradually deforms in real-world
feedback. It keeps its original name, but its content, object, emotional center, and actual function have changed. Many
institutional slogans, market consensuses, and identity narratives do not end with dramatic collapse — they end by
drifting into something unrecognizable.

Proposition 19.6: Recursive narrative analysis must track at least three feedback outcomes: reinforcement, backlash, and
drift.
This three-part division is important. It helps analysts avoid two crude judgments in complex reality: treating all
success as stable success, and treating all failure as sudden collapse. What is actually most common is the third kind:
the name has not changed, the story is still being told, but the narrative has long since ceased to be the original
narrative.

VII. A Theory of Innovative Possibility: Truly New Futures Usually Begin Not from Central Narratives, but from Marginal
Branches Being Renamed
Here we can fill in a point that Chapter 18 left unexpanded. Possibility-space analysis already distinguished which
futures have structural support. But a subtler question remains: how do innovative possibilities emerge?

Many people understand innovation as “something entirely new that suddenly appears.” But from the perspective of the
Theory of Difference, innovation with genuine real-world penetration rarely appears out of nowhere. Its more common mode
is: certain Differences that were previously marginal, fragmented, and low-visibility are renamed, reconnected, and
given new direction by a new narrative, thereby gaining the qualification to enter central competition.

That is to say, innovative possibility is not merely “new” — it is “something that was not previously treated as a main
branch gaining new structural support and a narrative engine.”

Proposition 19.7: The possibility of innovation comes not mainly from inventing a future that has never existed, but
from renaming, reconnecting, and giving real-world support to branches that were previously marginalized, fragmented,
and low-visibility.
This judgment is important. It frees “innovation” from romantic coloring. Innovation is not a mystical leap — it is a
structural transition after marginal Differences are reorganized. A new market, a new institution, a new political
alliance, a new technological paradigm gaining social acceptance — all follow this pattern.

So if you want to analyze genuinely innovative possibility, you cannot only watch the center. You must look at: which
marginal Differences are accumulating, which fragmented experiences are seeking unified naming, which previously
disconnected Subjects are being convened by the same story, which old narratives have lost the power to organize
reality, and which new narratives are creating entry points for marginal branches.

This is where innovative possibility truly grows.

Proposition 19.8: Recursive narrative analysis not only analyzes how existing order is maintained, but also analyzes how
new consensus, new alliances, and new real-world channels are first opened by narrative.

IX. The Practical Steps of Recursive Narrative Analysis: Not Dismantling Texts, but Tracking the

To make this chapter more grounded, the method needs to be simplified into a workable analytical path. It cannot be

Definition 19.3: Recursive narrative analysis includes at least four observational stages.

Second, identify expectation organization. See whether this narrative has actually entered Subject cognition, and
whether it has changed their judgments about risk, return, justice, threat, and time horizons.

Third, identify action consequences. See whether these expectation-changes further translate into capital flows,
institutional pressures, alliance reorganization, boundary reinforcement, price changes, or behavioral coordination.

Fourth, identify feedback outcomes. See whether real-world consequences have reinforced, backlash-ed, or drifted the
original narrative, and judge how the next round of narrative will evolve on the basis of the old feedback.

“narrative–expectation–action–feedback.”

Chapter 20: The Power of Theory Lies Not in Managing Reality, but in Preserving Intelligibility for Reality
By this point in Volume IV, method is no longer mere posture. Chapter 17 placed the judge back within Difference,
showing that method is not an external overview but the Subject forming testable, correctable, consequence-bearing
judgments while acknowledging its own position, boundaries, and risks. Chapter 18 proposed possibility-space analysis,
showing that reality is not the only possibility, the future is not any arbitrary possibility, and the key lies in

organized by narrative into common expectations and continuously reinforced, backlash-ed, or drifted in feedback.

At this point, Volume IV’s methodological skeleton has appeared. But precisely at this moment, one more thing must be
done: setting limits for theory.

Because any theory with genuine explanatory power encounters a nearly inescapable temptation. Once it has successfully
explained part of reality, a larger ambition naturally grows: since it works here, it probably works there too; since it
can already unify many objects, why not unify more; since it has become an effective framework, why not take in the
entire world?

This step looks like theoretical expansion, but in many cases it is precisely where theory begins losing its sense of
proportion. Because once a theory mistakes itself for “the total spokesperson for reality” rather than “a tool for
understanding reality,” it gradually loses sensitivity toward remainder, exception, heterogeneity, incomplete states,
and newborn Difference. The stronger it gets, the more easily it dulls at the most critical points.

So this chapter is not meant to weaken theory — it is precisely meant to preserve theory’s true power.

Proposition 20.1: The power of theory lies not in managing reality, but in continuously preserving intelligibility for
reality amid complexity and change.
“Managing reality” and “preserving intelligibility” differ not in tone but in the fundamental mode of theoretical
existence.

Managing reality means theory wants to become the final explainer. It wants to absorb the object entirely into its own
language, making remainder smaller and smaller, exceptions more and more suspect, and new things understandable only in
old concepts. Such theory may initially feel overwhelmingly powerful, because it provides a complete, stable, nearly
one-time explanation.

Preserving intelligibility is entirely different. It admits that reality will always outrun theory by one step, that
there is always something in the world not yet absorbed, that once an object responds to theory, the theory’s original
conditions of application are rewritten. Therefore, the true task of theory is not declaring “I have finished explaining
the world,” but ensuring that as the world keeps changing, people do not entirely lose the ability to enter reality, cut
it apart, rename it, and correct their judgments.

This sounds more restrained, but it is actually stronger. A theory that builds its life on the promise of “ultimate
completeness” will eventually be dragged down by reality. A theory that builds its life on the capacity to “continuously
provide entry points for understanding” may in fact be more durable and more penetrating.

I. Why Theory Always Inflates: Because Successful Explanation Naturally Invites Greater Ambition
Theory is not without desire. Once a theory succeeds on several objects in succession, it is hard for Subjects not to
develop higher trust in it. This trust has its necessity — without theoretical confidence, thought cannot advance; if
every generalization immediately retreats through self-doubt, theory can only remain stuck in material.

The problem is that successful explanation naturally invites a larger explanatory ambition. People slide from “it
explained this” to “it must also explain that”; from “it works under these conditions” to “it works under all
conditions”; from “it provides the main skeleton” to “it has no exterior.”

The inflation of theory usually does not begin from error. It begins from success. And precisely for this reason, it is
more dangerous than ordinary error — it wraps itself in the coat of correct experience, making people think they are
merely extending along the natural line, when in fact they have already crossed the original boundary.

So the first clear reminder of this chapter is:

Proposition 20.2: The most common runaway of theory is not insufficient explanation at the start, but refusing to stop
after explanatory success.
“Stop” here does not mean stopping thought — it means stopping the impulse to rewrite all objects in advance as internal
variables of one’s own framework. Theory must know what its strengths are, and it must also know where it can at most
give direction, cut-in, and structural feel, rather than a final verdict.

This is the difference between mature theory and young theory. Young theory most wants to conquer its objects; mature
theory first learns the sense of boundary.

II. What Theory Truly Fears Is Not Counterexamples, but Silencing Counterexamples in Advance
Many theories that look extremely solid are not so because they can truly withstand continuous real-world testing, but
because they have built a strong linguistic defense mechanism. Anything that does not fit the framework is pre-emptively
rewritten as accidental, local, noise, temporary deviation, not-yet-fully-revealed, or a special case where “the essence
has not yet fully unfolded.” Counterexamples exist, but they cannot enter the theory’s formal field of vision. The
theory appears forever correct on the surface.

This approach is extremely tempting because it maximizes theoretical authority. But it is actually transforming theory
from an understanding tool into a self-protection device. Once it reaches this point, theory is no longer used to
contact reality — it is used to defend against reality.

The Theory of Difference cannot be this way. Because if Difference is the genuine precondition of existence, if reality
always has an unabsorbed remainder, if objects change themselves through narrative and reflexivity, then any theory that
wants to maintain life must allow things that do not conform to its own language to enter.

So the second judgment of this chapter must be stated forcefully enough:

Proposition 20.3: The true robustness of theory lies not in eliminating counterexamples, but in allowing counterexamples
to force itself to re-cut objects, adjust layers, and correct boundaries.
This sentence is critically important. It means counterexamples are not merely attacks on theory — they are entry points
for theory to renew itself. A theory that cannot handle counterexamples may not collapse immediately, but it will
gradually lose the channel through which fresh reality enters. In the end, what it preserves may be its own linguistic
completeness, not its penetration of the world.

III. Why Abstraction Is Still Necessary: Without It, Reality Fragments into Incomparable Pieces
At this point, one common misunderstanding must also be prevented. Emphasizing that theory must set limits for itself
does not equal advocating the abandonment of theory and retreat into an empirical pile. Because once abstraction is
fully abandoned, reality does not thereby become more real — it only becomes more fragmented, harder to compare, and
harder to organize judgment around.

Abstraction is necessary not because theorists prefer high-altitude language, but because complex reality cannot stably
enter understanding without abstract mediation. The reason we can put different market cycles, different institutional
arrangements, different international conflicts, and different historical ruptures together for comparison is not simple
accumulation of detail — it depends on an abstract capacity that can extract relations, identify structures, and
organize layers.

So this chapter is absolutely not anti-abstraction. What it opposes is something else: mistaking abstraction for a
substitute for reality, and mistaking the tidiness of concepts for the completion of objects.

Proposition 20.4: The value of abstraction lies not in replacing reality, but in reorganizing reality from countless
isolated fragments into structures that can be compared, judged, and corrected.
This sentence is also a self-explanation for the entire Theory of Difference. The reason it discusses axioms,
inferences, typologies, and methods is not to flatten the world into a conceptual spreadsheet, but to keep multi-level,
multi-scale, multi-object reality visible on a single analytical plane. Without this layer, the whole book would
scatter; with only this layer and no sense of boundary, the whole book would harden. What Volume IV must do at this
moment is hold together “unifying force” and “openness” simultaneously.

IV. Theoretical Boundary Sense Comes from the Ability to Distinguish Skeleton from Filling
A marker of whether a theory is mature is whether it knows what it must hold on to as skeleton and what is merely
filling that can be adjusted, replaced, added, or removed.

Skeleton is what, once removed, means the theory is no longer itself. Filling is the analytical expression, example
arrangement, and stage-specific inference used to make the skeleton visible in particular periods, particular objects,
and particular domains. Skeleton determines the theory’s identity; filling determines the theory’s applicable form. The
former cannot be lightly discarded; the latter should be allowed to change.

If a theory cannot distinguish the two, it usually falls into two opposite but equally dangerous states. One is too
brittle: at any local correction, it mistakes the entire theory for needing to be overthrown. The other is too hard: it
sacralizes stage-specific judgments, historical cases, and even certain habits of expression, as if any revision is
betrayal.

This is also what Volume IV must now clarify for the entire book. The skeleton of The Theory of Difference is the
ontological status of Difference, Conservation, discrete change, narrative recursion, Minimal Variation–Nearest
Equilibrium, and the Subject maintaining itself around boundaries. If these are removed, the book’s root is severed. But
certain cases, certain era-specific expressions, and certain inferences about specific objects are closer to filling.
They certainly matter, but they should be updated as reality changes.

Proposition 20.5: The stability of theory comes not from everything being unchangeable, but from its ability to
distinguish what must be held and what was always meant to change.
This judgment has great significance. It rescues theory from the either/or of “entirely rigid or entirely fluid.” A
truly strong theory is not an iron block — it is a living structure with bone and flesh, stable skeleton and updatable
texture.

V. Why Theory Cannot Promise a Final Answer: Because Objects Change Themselves in Responding to Theory
The first three volumes already discussed reflexivity and narrative recursion. Now this must be brought back into theory
itself. Theory does not speak to a static object. Especially in the social world, objects read theory, hear theory, cite
theory, use theory, and resist theory. Once this happens, theory no longer merely explains the world — it participates
in making a new one.

What does this mean? It means the more successful a theory becomes, the more likely it is to change the very object it
originally explained. And once the object is changed, the theory’s original conditions of application are no longer the
same. Any social theory that attempts to give a “final answer” encounters a fundamental difficulty: the object it must
explain shifts because of the theory’s own entry.

Financial markets are the most typical. Once a strategy is widely known, it changes price behavior and may quickly
become ineffective. The institutional domain is the same. A governance principle that becomes a public slogan causes
Subjects to rearrange around it, changing the original institutional environment. International relations are the same.
An order theory that becomes a consensus for action is no longer a neutral instruction manual — it participates in

VII. The Final Convergence: This Book Does Not Aim to Finish the World on Its Behalf, but to Stop People from Finishing
the World Too Easily
At this point, the most fitting way to end is perhaps not to offer new grand sentences, but to return to the book’s
actual function.

The Theory of Difference was written in this form not to replace all other languages with a larger one, nor to capture
every real-world object within a single standard explanation. What it truly aims to do, in a world where people too
easily chase endpoints, too easily treat phase-specific arrangements as natural, too easily mistake narrative for
reality, and too easily speak of reality as already completed, is to force out a harder but more honest way of
understanding.

This book does not aim to finish the world on its behalf. It aims to stop people from finishing the world too easily.

It reminds us:

Difference is not noise but the condition of existence
Order is not an endpoint but an organizational form
Stability is not completion but phase-specific suppression
Price is not reality but revelation
The state is not an abstract machine but a boundary-maintenance body
History is not a final chapter but reorganization within cycles
Method is not an external overview but internal judgment
Theory is not managing reality but preserving intelligibility
Openness is not weakness but the correct posture before an incomplete world
At this point, the book has essentially said what it needs to say. But the world has not. This is precisely why the book
cannot end in the voice of finality. It can only land in a clearer way:

Proposition 22.8: The best ending for theory is not declaring that the world has been explained, but letting judgment
continue while the world remains unfinished.
Chapter Echo
Remember the eight most important sentences from this chapter.

First: The world is not an object waiting to be unified, but a Difference-structure that constantly demands we re-judge.
Second: The first step of this book is not adding a concept to the world, but turning the starting point of
understanding from sameness back to Difference.
Third: Social order is not the endpoint of Difference, but the temporary organizational form of Difference under
specific conditions.
Fourth: Only a theory that can maintain explanatory power on high-pressure objects like markets, states, and history
deserves to be called a structural theory.
Fifth: The maturity of theory is not expanding itself into everything, but maintaining skeleton while acknowledging that
the world is always larger than one’s own expression.
Sixth: The Theory of Difference opposes not unity, but the mistake of treating phase-specific unity as finally completed
unity.
Seventh: The responsibility of people in a Difference-world is not searching for an endpoint where judgment is never
again needed, but building better judgment, organization, and correction capacity within continuously generated
Difference.
Eighth: The best ending for theory is not declaring that the world has been explained, but letting judgment continue
while the world remains unfinished.
At this point, Volume IV is complete. The full structure of The Theory of Difference V1.6 is complete. Starting from the
ontological status of Difference, through social axioms, markets and states, history and method, it finally returns to
the limits and openness of theory itself, forming an overall structure that closes without dying and converges without
rigidity.
