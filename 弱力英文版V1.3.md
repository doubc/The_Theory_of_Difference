# Introduction

## The Three Anomalous Features of Weak Interactions

Among the four fundamental interactions, the weak force occupies a unique
position in the Standard Model by virtue of three structural features that
have no counterpart in the other interactions.

**Chirality.​** The weak force couples exclusively to left-handed fermions.
The $SU(2)_L$ gauge symmetry acts only on left-handed doublets; right-handed
fermions are singlets under $SU(2)_L$. This maximal parity violation was
discovered experimentally by Wu et al. (1957) and remains, within the
Standard Model, an input assumption rather than a derived consequence.

**V–A coupling structure.​** The weak current takes the form
$J^\mu \propto \bar{\psi}\gamma^\mu(1 - \gamma^5)\psi$, combining vector
($V$) and axial-vector ($A$) contributions with equal magnitude and opposite
sign. The ratio $|g_V| = |g_A|$ is not predicted by the Standard Model; it
is selected by the choice of the left-handed projection operator
$(1-\gamma^5)/2$.

**Massive gauge bosons.​** The $W^\pm$ and $Z^0$ bosons acquire mass through
the Higgs mechanism, which introduces spontaneous symmetry breaking as an
additional structural layer.

All three features are inputs to the Standard Model, not outputs. The
framework does not explain *why* the weak interaction is chiral, *why* V and
A couple with equal strength, or *why* parity is violated maximally rather
than partially. These are accepted as empirical facts encoded in the gauge
group choice $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$.

## The Question This Paper Addresses

This paper asks a more fundamental question:

> Can the chiral gauge algebra $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ and the V–A coupling
> structure be *derived* from a set of formal axioms that contain no prior
> physical content — no fermions, no gauge bosons, no spacetime — rather
> than being *postulated* as the gauge group of weak interactions?

We answer this question affirmatively within the discrete difference-space
framework introduced in the companion paper on gravitational potential
derivation [CITATION: Paper 1]. The derivation proceeds entirely by
constraint: we identify which algebraic structures are *unavoidable* given
the axioms, rather than which structures happen to match observation.

## Method: Constraint Derivation

The WorldBase framework adopts what we term *constraint derivation* as its
methodological principle. Rather than inducting laws from empirical
observations (additive logic), it begins from all logically possible
structures and systematically eliminates those incompatible with a set of
formal axioms (subtractive logic). The surviving structure is what the axioms
*necessitate*.

This methodology has precedents in the foundations of physics. Einstein's
special relativity derives the Lorentz transformation from two axioms
(relativity principle and constant speed of light) without any empirical
fitting. Noether's theorem derives conservation laws from continuous
symmetries. The present framework extends this tradition to the derivation of
gauge algebra structure from axioms of difference relations.

The companion paper [CITATION: Paper 1] established the gravitational
potential $\Phi(r) \propto -1/r$ and the effective spatial dimension
$D_\text{eff} = 3$ from five axioms. The present paper uses the same axiomatic
framework to derive the algebraic structure of weak interactions, using three
of the ten axioms: A4, A6, and A9.

A foundational commitment distinguishes the WorldBase framework from
lattice approaches in computational physics. In Wilson's lattice gauge
theory, the discrete lattice is an approximation tool introduced for
computational tractability, to be removed in the continuum limit
$\epsilon \to 0$; the continuous theory is taken as ontologically
fundamental. The present framework inverts this priority: the discrete
state space $\{0,1\}^N$ is the ontological foundation, and continuous
field theories are *emergent descriptions* valid at scales $L \gg
\epsilon$, where $\epsilon = LN^{-1/3}$ is the fundamental lattice
spacing in the scaling limit $N \to \infty$.

This distinction has a precise mathematical consequence. The relevant
notion of convergence is not pointwise convergence (requiring the
discrete equation to agree with the continuous equation at every point
as $\epsilon \to 0$) but *statistical emergence* (requiring the
macroscopic average of the discrete equation to satisfy the continuous
equation at scales $L \gg \epsilon$). This is the same relationship
that connects molecular dynamics to the Navier-Stokes equations via
the Boltzmann equation and Chapman-Enskog expansion, or discrete spins
to $\phi^4$ field theory via the renormalization group. In all such
cases, the continuous equation is not a limit of the discrete equation
in the pointwise sense; it is an emergent description of the collective
behavior of many discrete degrees of freedom. The companion paper
[CITATION] established the first rigorous instance of this emergence
within the WorldBase framework: the discrete Hamming potential field
converges, in the $L^2_\mathrm{loc}$ sense under the scaling limit
$N \to \infty$, to the continuous Newtonian gravitational potential.

## Main Results

This paper establishes three theorems:

**Theorem 1** *(Parity violation from irreversibility)*. Under axioms A4 and
A6, the transfer operator $T$ is non-Hermitian ($T \neq T^\dagger$), and the
discrete parity operator $\mathcal{P}$ satisfies
$$\mathcal{P}\, T\, \mathcal{P}^{-1} = T^\dagger \neq T.$$
Parity symmetry is broken as an algebraic consequence of the DAG constraint,
not as an empirical input.

**Theorem 2** *($\mathfrak{su}(2)$ closure)*. The polar decomposition of $T$
generates three Hermitian operators $\{H_1, H_2, H_3\}$ satisfying
$$[H_i,\, H_j] = i\varepsilon_{ijk}\, H_k,$$
establishing $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ as the minimal non-Abelian algebra emergent
from non-Hermitian local transfer under A4 and A6.

**Theorem 3** *(V–A parameter locking)*. Under axiom A9, the Hermitian and
anti-Hermitian components of $T$ cannot be assigned independent coupling
constants. The V–A ratio is locked:
$$|g_V| = |g_A|,$$
as a joint algebraic consequence of A4, A6, and A9.

## Scope and Boundary

All three theorems in this paper are exact results within the discrete
difference-space $\{0,1\}^N$. No continuous limit is required for
their validity.

The relationship between these discrete results and continuous
$SU(2)_L$ gauge field theory is that of *emergence*, not
approximation. Continuous gauge theories are expected to arise as
macroscopic descriptions of the discrete algebraic structure in the
limit $N \to \infty$, in the same sense that the Navier-Stokes
equations arise as macroscopic descriptions of molecular dynamics.
The following are therefore not deficiencies of the present framework
but open problems in the construction of emergent bridges:

- Continuous $SU(2)_L$ gauge field theory (emergent bridge from
  discrete $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra to continuous gauge connection);
- $W^\pm$ and $Z^0$ boson masses (expected to arise from the A8
  symmetry-preference potential barrier at the middle-section
  boundary);
- Left-handed fermion spinor representations in continuous spacetime;
- Electroweak unification.

The mathematical tools available for constructing these emergent
bridges include homogenization theory [Bensoussan-Lions-Papanicolaou
1978], graph limit theory (graphons) [Lovász 2012], and the
renormalization group [Wilson 1975]. The discrete exterior calculus
framework already employed in the companion paper's treatment of
electromagnetic structure provides a direct connection to established
convergence theorems for discrete differential operators.

# Axiomatic Framework

## State Space

The WorldBase framework is built on a minimal discrete state space. Let

$$\mathcal{X} = \{0,1\}^N$$

be the $N$-dimensional binary hypercube, containing $2^N$ states. For any
state $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_N) \in \mathcal{X}$, define:

- **Hamming weight**: $w(x) = \sum_{i=1}^N x_i$
- **Hamming distance**: $d_H(x, y) = |\{i : x_i \neq y_i\}|$
- **Ground state**: $\mathbf{0} = (0, 0, \ldots, 0)$, the unique state with $w = 0$

**Ontological interpretation.​** Each bit $x_i$ does not represent the value
of a physical quantity. It represents whether a difference exists in dimension
$i$. The ground state $\mathbf{0}$ is the unique reference state of zero
difference. All other states are difference patterns relative to $\mathbf{0}$.
Particles, fields, and geometry are not fundamental objects in this framework
— they are stable emergent patterns of difference relations at appropriate
scales.

## The Ten Axioms

The WorldBase framework is defined by ten axioms on $\mathcal{X}$. The full
axiomatic system is presented in the companion paper [CITATION: Paper 1];
here we provide the complete list with brief descriptions, followed by
detailed treatment of the three axioms directly used in this paper.

| Axiom  | Name                      | Core Constraint                                                                               |
|:------:|:--------------------------|:----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|   A1   | Primordial Difference     | Unique ground state; Hamming weight monotonically non-decreasing along evolution paths        |
|  A1'   | Lateral Emergence         | Transverse emergence space carries continuous rotational symmetry; orthogonal to A1 direction |
|   A2   | Binary Concretization     | State space is binary-valued                                                                  |
|   A3   | Finite Discreteness       | $                                                                                             |\mathcal{X}| = 2^N < \infty$ |
| **A4** | **Minimal Variation**     | **Each evolution step changes exactly one dimension: $d_H(x,y) = 1$​**                        |
|   A5   | Difference Conservation   | Total difference quantity conserved in closed systems                                         |
| **A6** | **Irreversibility**       | **Evolution graph is a directed acyclic graph (DAG)​**                                        |
|   A7   | Cyclic Closure            | Stable states must participate in directed cycles of length $\geq N$                          |
|   A8   | Symmetry Preference       | System prefers states with $w = N/2$                                                          |
| **A9** | **Internal Completeness** | **No degrees of freedom beyond those mandated by the axioms: $F = F_\text{axiom}$​**          |

The three axioms central to this paper are:

**A4 (Minimal Variation)​** constrains the *locality* of evolution. Each
transition moves exactly one activated bit from one position to another, or
flips exactly one bit. This is the discrete analog of the principle of
minimal action and enforces that information propagates only through
neighboring states.

**A6 (Irreversibility)​** is the axiom with the deepest consequences for weak
interaction structure. The requirement that the evolution graph be a DAG
means: if a transition $x \to y$ is allowed, then $y \to x$ is forbidden.
This directedness, as we show in Section 3, is precisely what forces transfer
operators to be non-Hermitian — and non-Hermiticity is the algebraic root of
all chiral structure derived in this paper.

**A9 (Internal Completeness)​** acts as a closure principle. It prohibits the
system from possessing any degree of freedom not introduced by the axioms
themselves. In the companion paper, A9 provides the upper bound in the
squeeze argument that fixes $D_{A1'} = 2$ and hence $D_\text{eff} = 3$. In
this paper, A9 prevents the Hermitian and anti-Hermitian components of a
single transfer operator from being treated as two independent physical
degrees of freedom, thereby locking the V–A ratio.

## The Middle Section

**Definition 2.1** *(Middle section)*. For even $N$, the middle section is

$$M_N = \left\{x \in \{0,1\}^N : w(x) = \frac{N}{2}\right\}, \qquad |M_N| = \binom{N}{N/2}.$$

The middle section is the layer of maximally symmetric difference
distributions. By A8, the system assigns preferential weight to $M_N$. The
strong and weak interaction structures both originate in $M_N$: the strong
force from the full algebra of first-order transpositions on $M_N$ (yielding
$\mathfrak{su}(3)$, as derived in [CITATION: Paper 1]), and the weak force
from the directional sub-algebra induced by A6, as derived in this paper.

## First-Order Transposition Operators

**Definition 2.2** *(First-order transposition operator)*. For $i \neq j$,
define $E_{ij} : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{X}$ by

$$E_{ij}|x\rangle = \begin{cases} |x'\rangle & \text{if } x_i = 1 \text{ and } x_j = 0 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $x'$ is obtained from $x$ by setting $x_i \mapsto 0$ and $x_j \mapsto 1$.

**Remark 2.3** *(Dual role of $E_{ij}$)*. The operator $E_{ij}$
simultaneously flips two bits ($x_i: 1 \to 0$ and $x_j: 0 \to 1$) and
therefore is *not* a single A4 evolution step, which requires $d_H = 1$.
Rather, $E_{ij}$ is a two-step composite: $|x\rangle \xrightarrow{\text{flip
}i} |x'\rangle \xrightarrow{\text{flip }j} |x''\rangle$, where the
intermediate state $|x'\rangle$ has $w(x') = w(x) - 1$ and lies outside
$M_N$. Within the algebraic study of middle-section structure, however,
$E_{ij}$ serves as an *algebraic generator* of weight-preserving local
rearrangement symmetry — not an A4 evolution edge, but a representation of
the internal symmetry structure of $M_N$.

**Notation.​** We write $E_{ij}^\dagger = E_{ji}$, verified directly:
$E_{ji}|x\rangle \neq 0$ iff $x_j = 1$ and $x_i = 0$, which is precisely
the condition under which $\langle x | E_{ij}^\dagger | y \rangle =
\overline{\langle y | E_{ij} | x \rangle} \neq 0$.

# Non-Hermiticity, Parity Violation, and Nilpotency

## The Discrete Parity Operator

In continuous space, parity is the coordinate inversion $\mathbf{x} \to
-\mathbf{x}$. On the discrete hypercube $\{0,1\}^N$, we require an
involutive map that preserves Hamming weight (since physical processes
preserve the total amount of difference).

**Definition 3.1** *(Discrete parity operator)*. Let $N$ be even. Pair the
$N$ coordinates as $(x_1, x_2), (x_3, x_4), \ldots, (x_{N-1}, x_N)$. Define

$$\mathcal{P}: (x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4, \ldots, x_{N-1}, x_N) \longmapsto (x_2, x_1, x_4, x_3, \ldots, x_N, x_{N-1}).$$

That is, $\mathcal{P}$ exchanges bits within each pair.

**Proposition 3.2** *(Properties of $\mathcal{P}$)*. The operator $\mathcal{P}$
satisfies:

1. **Involution**: $\mathcal{P}^2 = \mathrm{id}$.
2. **Hamming weight conservation**: $w(\mathcal{P}(x)) = w(x)$, since the
   sum $x_{2k-1} + x_{2k}$ is invariant under exchange within each pair.
3. **Hamming distance conservation**: $d_H(\mathcal{P}(x), \mathcal{P}(y)) =
   d_H(x, y)$, since $\mathcal{P}$ is a coordinate permutation.
4. **Action on transfer operators**: $\mathcal{P}$ induces a coordinate
   permutation $\sigma$ on index positions. For any transfer operator
   $E_{ij}$,
   $$\mathcal{P}\, E_{ij}\, \mathcal{P}^{-1} = E_{\sigma(i)\,\sigma(j)}.$$

*Proof of (4).* For any state $|x\rangle$:
$$(\mathcal{P}\, E_{ij}\, \mathcal{P}^{-1})|x\rangle = \mathcal{P}\bigl(E_{ij}|\mathcal{P}^{-1}(x)\rangle\bigr).$$
The operator $E_{ij}$ moves an activated bit from position $i$ to position
$j$ in $\mathcal{P}^{-1}(x)$. Under $\mathcal{P}$, position $i$ maps to
$\sigma(i)$ and position $j$ maps to $\sigma(j)$. The net effect is therefore
to move an activated bit from position $\sigma(i)$ to position $\sigma(j)$ in
$x$, which is precisely the action of $E_{\sigma(i)\,\sigma(j)}$. $\square$

**Remark 3.3** *(Independence from pairing scheme)*. The parity-breaking
conclusion of Theorem 3.4 below does not depend on the specific pairing
scheme chosen in Definition 3.1. For any involutive weight-preserving
permutation $\mathcal{P}$ and any A6-allowed directed transfer operator $T$,
the non-Hermiticity of $T$ (established independently by A6) guarantees
$\mathcal{P}\,T\,\mathcal{P}^{-1} \neq T$.

## Theorem 1: Parity Violation from DAG Irreversibility

**Theorem 3.4** *(Parity violation from irreversibility)*. Under axioms A4
and A6, the discrete parity symmetry is broken:

$$\mathcal{P}\, T\, \mathcal{P}^{-1} = T^\dagger \neq T.$$

*Proof.* Let $T = E_{ij}$ be an A6-allowed directed transfer operator.

**Step 1** *(A6 forces non-Hermiticity)*. The DAG condition requires that if
$E_{ij}$ is an allowed transition (moving an activated bit from $i$ to $j$),
then $E_{ji}$ is forbidden. Therefore:
$$\langle y | E_{ij} | x \rangle \neq 0 \implies \langle x | E_{ij} | y \rangle = 0.$$
This means $E_{ij} \neq E_{ij}^\dagger = E_{ji}$, i.e., $T \neq T^\dagger$.

**Step 2** *(Parity maps $T$ to $T^\dagger$)*. By Proposition 3.2(4),
$\mathcal{P}\,E_{ij}\,\mathcal{P}^{-1} = E_{\sigma(i)\,\sigma(j)}$. For a
parity operator that exchanges paired coordinates, $\sigma$ maps each
position to its pair partner, so $E_{\sigma(i)\,\sigma(j)}$ reverses the
direction of transfer relative to $E_{ij}$. This reversal is precisely
$E_{ji} = E_{ij}^\dagger$.

**Step 3** *(Conclusion)*. Combining Steps 1 and 2:
$$\mathcal{P}\, T\, \mathcal{P}^{-1} = T^\dagger \neq T. \qquad \square$$

| $T \neq T^\dagger$ (A6: DAG constraint)               | Weak force couples only to left-handed fermions    |

## Nilpotency

**Proposition 3.5** *(Nilpotency)*. In the minimum two-state subsystem
($N=2$, $w=1$, $M = \{|1,0\rangle, |0,1\rangle\}$):

$$T^2 = 0, \qquad (T^\dagger)^2 = 0.$$

*Proof.* $T = E_{12}$ moves an activated bit from position 1 to position 2.
After one application, the resulting state has $x_1 = 0$, so the condition
$x_1 = 1$ required for a second application is not met. Hence $T^2 = 0$.
The argument for $(T^\dagger)^2 = 0$ is symmetric. $\square$

**Remark 3.6** *(Nilpotency as the algebraic signature of maximal parity
violation)*. The nilpotency $T^2 = 0$ implies that $T$ has spectral radius
zero — all eigenvalues vanish. As a consequence, the Hermitian component
$H_1 = (T + T^\dagger)/2$ and the anti-Hermitian component $H_2 = (T -
T^\dagger)/2i$ have equal matrix norms: $\|H_1\| = \|H_2\|$. This means the
V and A coupling strengths are not merely equal but are *structurally
constrained to be equal* by the nilpotent nature of the transfer operator.
There is no intermediate state of "predominantly vector with a small axial
correction" — the parity violation is maximal. This is consistent with the
physical observation that weak interactions exhibit maximal, not partial,
parity violation.

# $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ Closure and Numerical Verification

## The Minimum Two-State Subsystem

Consider the minimum non-trivial subsystem: $N = 2$, middle section
$w = 1$, with two states:

$$M_2 = \{|1,0\rangle,\; |0,1\rangle\}.$$

The A6-allowed directed transfer operator in this system is:

$$T = E_{12} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad T^\dagger = E_{21} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$

Non-Hermiticity is immediate: $T|1,0\rangle = |0,1\rangle$ while
$T^\dagger|1,0\rangle = 0$, so $T \neq T^\dagger$. Nilpotency:
$T^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. $\checkmark$

## Generator Definitions and Hermiticity

**Definition 4.1** *(Polar decomposition generators)*. Define:

$$H_1 = \frac{T + T^\dagger}{2}, \qquad H_2 = \frac{T - T^\dagger}{2i}, \qquad H_3 = \frac{1}{2}[T^\dagger,\, T].$$

**Remark 4.2** *(Why $H_3$ carries no factor of $i$)*. We verify that
$[T^\dagger, T]$ is Hermitian. Since $T^\dagger T$ and $TT^\dagger$ are each
self-adjoint (as $(AB)^\dagger = B^\dagger A^\dagger$ gives
$(T^\dagger T)^\dagger = T^\dagger T$), their difference is also Hermitian:

$$[T^\dagger, T]^\dagger = (T^\dagger T - TT^\dagger)^\dagger = T^\dagger T - TT^\dagger = [T^\dagger, T].$$

Therefore $H_3 = \frac{1}{2}[T^\dagger, T]$ is Hermitian without any
additional factor. If instead one defined $H_3 = \frac{i}{2}[T^\dagger, T]$,
the result would be anti-Hermitian, and the three generators would not all be
Hermitian, preventing the algebra from closing in standard $\mathfrak{su}(2)$
form.

In the two-state system:

$$T^\dagger T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad TT^\dagger = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad [T^\dagger, T] = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The three generators in the two-state system are:

$$H_1 = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad H_2 = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad H_3 = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

These are precisely $\frac{1}{2}\sigma_x$, $\frac{1}{2}\sigma_y$,
$\frac{1}{2}\sigma_z$, where $\sigma_x, \sigma_y, \sigma_z$ are the Pauli
matrices. The identification $T = E_{12} = H_1 + iH_2 = J_+$ (the raising
operator) makes the connection to standard angular momentum algebra explicit.

## Theorem 2: $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ Closure

**Theorem 4.3** *($\mathfrak{su}(2)$ closure)*. In the minimum two-state
subsystem ($N=2$, $w=1$), the generators $\{H_1, H_2, H_3\}$ of Definition
4.1 satisfy the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ commutation relations:

$$[H_i,\, H_j] = i\varepsilon_{ijk}\, H_k.$$

*Proof.* Direct computation using the Pauli matrix representation:

$$[H_1, H_2] = \frac{1}{4}[\sigma_x, \sigma_y] = \frac{1}{4}(2i\sigma_z) = \frac{i}{2}\sigma_z = iH_3. \checkmark$$

$$[H_2, H_3] = \frac{1}{4}[\sigma_y, \sigma_z] = \frac{1}{4}(2i\sigma_x) = \frac{i}{2}\sigma_x = iH_1. \checkmark$$

$$[H_3, H_1] = \frac{1}{4}[\sigma_z, \sigma_x] = \frac{1}{4}(2i\sigma_y) = \frac{i}{2}\sigma_y = iH_2. \checkmark \qquad \square$$

**Structural observation.​** The $6 \times 6$ system decomposes into two
independent two-state subsystems $\{|2⟩, |4⟩\}$ and $\{|3⟩, |5⟩\}$, plus
two decoupled states $|1⟩$ and $|6⟩$ on which all generators act trivially.

embedding stability result of Section 4.5.

**Verification of $[H_1, H_2] = iH_3$.​**

Computing the diagonal entries of $[H_1, H_2]$ (off-diagonal entries vanish
by the block decomposition):

$$[H_1, H_2]_{22} = (H_1)_{24}(H_2)_{42} - (H_2)_{24}(H_1)_{42}
= \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{i}{2} - (-\tfrac{i}{2})\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}
= \tfrac{i}{4} + \tfrac{i}{4} = \tfrac{i}{2}.$$

$$[H_1, H_2]_{44} = (H_1)_{42}(H_2)_{24} - (H_2)_{42}(H_1)_{24}
= \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot(-\tfrac{i}{2}) - \tfrac{i}{2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}
= -\tfrac{i}{2}.$$

By symmetry, $[H_1,H_2]_{33} = \tfrac{i}{2}$ and $[H_1,H_2]_{55} = -\tfrac{i}{2}$.
All other entries vanish. Therefore:

$$[H_1, H_2] = \mathrm{diag}\!\left(0,\,\tfrac{i}{2},\,\tfrac{i}{2},\,-\tfrac{i}{2},\,-\tfrac{i}{2},\,0\right) = i \cdot H_3. \quad \checkmark$$

**Verification of $[H_2, H_3] = iH_1$.​**

Since $H_3$ is diagonal with $(H_3)_{22} = (H_3)_{33} = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and
$(H_3)_{44} = (H_3)_{55} = -\tfrac{1}{2}$:

$$[H_2, H_3]_{24} = (H_2)_{24}(H_3)_{44} - (H_3)_{22}(H_2)_{24}
= (-\tfrac{i}{2})(-\tfrac{1}{2}) - \tfrac{1}{2}(-\tfrac{i}{2})
= \tfrac{i}{4} + \tfrac{i}{4} = \tfrac{i}{2} = i(H_1)_{24}. \quad \checkmark$$

The remaining non-zero entries follow by the same computation. $\checkmark$

**Verification of $[H_3, H_1] = iH_2$.​**

$$[H_3, H_1]_{24} = (H_3)_{22}(H_1)_{24} - (H_1)_{24}(H_3)_{44}

= \tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{1}{2}.$$

$$i(H_2)_{24} = i\cdot(-\tfrac{i}{2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}. \quad \checkmark$$

The remaining entries follow identically. $\checkmark$

$$[H_i,\, H_j] = i\varepsilon_{ijk}\, H_k \qquad \text{(exact, zero error).}$$

The $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra closes precisely, with no approximation.

## Embedding Stability

**Proposition 4.4** *(Embedding stability)*. For any $N \geq 2$ and any pair
of active positions $\{a, b\} \subset \{1, \ldots, N\}$, the restriction of
$E_{ab}$ to the two-dimensional subspace

$$V_{ab} = \mathrm{span}\bigl\{|x\rangle \in M_N : x_a = 1,\, x_b = 0\bigr\} \cup \bigl\{|x\rangle \in M_N : x_a = 0,\, x_b = 1\bigr\}$$

is unitarily equivalent to the $N=2$ transfer operator $T$. Consequently,
the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra generated by $\{H_1, H_2, H_3\}$ on $V_{ab}$
is isomorphic to the $N=2$ algebra of Theorem 4.3.

*Proof.* The restriction of $E_{ab}$ to $V_{ab}$ acts as
$E_{ab}|x\rangle_{x_a=1,x_b=0} = |x'\rangle_{x_a=0,x_b=1}$ and annihilates
the other basis vector. In the ordered basis
$\{|x\rangle_{x_a=1,x_b=0},\, |x\rangle_{x_a=0,x_b=1}\}$, this is the
matrix $\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$, identical to $T$ in the $N=2$
system. The commutation relations depend only on this matrix, hence the
resulting $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra is isomorphic. $\square$

This proposition, verified explicitly for $N=4$ in Section 4.4 (where the
$6 \times 6$ system decomposes into two independent copies of the $N=2$
algebra), establishes that the $N=2$ result is not a special case but a
*universal local structure*: the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra is embedded
stably in any larger system as the local algebra of any two active positions.

**Corollary 4.5.​** The $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra derived here is a

$\mathfrak{su}(2) \hookrightarrow \mathfrak{su}(3)$. The two algebras
operate at different structural levels: $\mathfrak{su}(3)$ is the full
algebra of all first-order transpositions on $M_N$ (three active positions),
while $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ is the local algebra of any two active positions
within that structure.

# V–A Structure and Parameter Locking

## The Unique Decomposition

The transfer operator $T = E_{12}$ admits a canonical decomposition into its
Hermitian and anti-Hermitian parts:

$$E_{12} = \underbrace{\frac{E_{12} + E_{21}}{2}}_{H_1} + i\underbrace{\frac{E_{12} - E_{21}}{2i}}_{H_2} = H_1 + iH_2.$$

The physical identification is:

- $H_1$ (Hermitian part) $\leftrightarrow$ **Vector current** (V component)
- $iH_2$ (anti-Hermitian part) $\leftrightarrow$ **Axial-vector current** (A component)

This decomposition is *unique*: every operator has a unique decomposition
into Hermitian and anti-Hermitian parts. There is no freedom in how $T$ is
split.

**Explicit verification on $\{|2⟩, |4⟩\}$ subspace.​**

$$E_{12}\big|_{\{|2⟩,|4⟩\}} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad
H_1\big|_{\{|2⟩,|4⟩\}} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad
H_2\big|_{\{|2⟩,|4⟩\}} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}0 & -i \\ i & 0\end{pmatrix}.$$

$$H_1 + iH_2 = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} + \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} = E_{12}\big|_{\{|2⟩,|4⟩\}}. \quad \checkmark$$

V-component coefficient: $\tfrac{1}{2}$. A-component coefficient: $\tfrac{1}{2}$.

$$|g_V| = |g_A| = \frac{1}{2}. \quad \checkmark$$

## Theorem 3: V–A Parameter Locking by A9

**Theorem 5.1** *(V–A parameter locking)*. Under axioms A4, A6, and A9, the
vector and axial-vector coupling constants satisfy

$$|g_V| = |g_A|$$

as a necessary algebraic consequence, not a free parameter.

*Proof.* The argument proceeds by a degree-of-freedom squeeze.

**Step 1** *(Physical degrees of freedom under A4 + A6)*. In the minimum
two-state system ($N=2$, $w=1$), the middle section contains two states
connected by two possible transfer operators: $E_{12}$ (allowed by A6) and
$E_{21}$ (forbidden by A6, as it reverses the DAG direction). Therefore,
under A4 + A6, there is exactly **one** independent physical transfer
operator:

$$\text{independent physical transfer operators} = 1 \quad (T = E_{12}).$$

**Step 2** *(Algebraic generators from $T$)*. The algebraic closure of $T$
under Hermitian conjugation and commutation produces exactly three linearly
independent generators:

$$\{H_1,\, H_2,\, H_3\} = \left\{\frac{T+T^\dagger}{2},\; \frac{T-T^\dagger}{2i},\; \frac{1}{2}[T^\dagger, T]\right\}.$$

These three generators span $\mathfrak{su}(2)$, which has $\dim(\mathfrak{su}(2)) = 3$.

**Step 3** *(A9 prohibits independent coupling constants)*. Suppose one
attempted to assign independent coupling constants $\alpha$ and $\beta$ to
the V and A components:

$$\mathcal{L}_\text{int} \propto \alpha\, \bar{\psi} H_1 \psi + \beta\, \bar{\psi} H_2 \psi.$$

For $\alpha \neq \beta$, this would require treating $H_1$ and $H_2$ as two
independent physical degrees of freedom. But $H_1$ and $H_2$ are not
independent: they are the Hermitian and anti-Hermitian parts of the *single*
operator $T$, uniquely determined by $T$ via $H_1 = (T + T^\dagger)/2$ and
$H_2 = (T - T^\dagger)/2i$. Introducing the ratio $\alpha/\beta$ as a free
parameter would constitute adding a degree of freedom — the "relative weight
between the two parts of $T$" — that has no axiom as its source. A9 explicitly
forbids this: $F = F_\text{axiom}$. Therefore $\alpha = \beta$, and:

$$|g_V| = |g_A|. \qquad \square$$

**Squeeze summary.​**

$$\underbrace{\text{independent physical d.o.f.} = 1}_{\text{A4 + A6}} \;\xrightarrow{\text{polar decomposition}}\; \underbrace{\text{algebraic generators} = 3 = \dim(\mathfrak{su}(2))}_{\text{closure}} \;\xrightarrow{\text{A9}}\; \underbrace{|g_V| = |g_A|}_{\text{locking}}.$$

# Discussion

## What This Paper Has Established

Three theorems have been proved within the discrete difference-space
framework:

**Theorem 1** establishes that parity violation is not an empirical
contingency but an algebraic necessity. Any system satisfying A6 (DAG
irreversibility) has non-Hermitian transfer operators, and any involutive
weight-preserving parity map applied to such operators yields
$\mathcal{P}T\mathcal{P}^{-1} = T^\dagger \neq T$. The physical fact that
the weak force violates parity is, in this framework, as inevitable as the
fact that a directed graph has asymmetric adjacency matrices.

**Theorem 2** establishes that the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra is the minimal
non-Abelian algebra that emerges from non-Hermitian local transfer under A4
and A6. The derivation requires no prior knowledge of gauge theory; the
algebra appears as the algebraic closure of a single non-Hermitian operator
under Hermitian conjugation and commutation. The $N=4$ numerical verification
confirms this with exact agreement (zero error).

**Theorem 3** establishes that the V–A coupling ratio is not a tunable
parameter. It is locked to $|g_V| = |g_A|$ by A9's prohibition on
introducing degrees of freedom beyond those mandated by the axioms. The
ratio $\alpha/\beta$ that one might naively assign to the relative V and A
strengths has no axiom as its source and is therefore forbidden.

## Open Problems in Emergent Bridge Construction

The discrete algebraic results of this paper are complete. The
following open problems concern the construction of rigorous emergent
bridges from the discrete framework to continuous physical theories.
These are not gaps in the present derivation but the natural next
steps in a research program that takes discrete difference relations
as ontologically fundamental.

**Emergent $SU(2)_L$ gauge field theory.** The most immediate open
problem is to prove that the discrete $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ generators
$\{H_1, H_2, H_3\}$ emerge, in the limit $N \to \infty$, as the
generators of a continuous $SU(2)_L$ gauge connection on a smooth
manifold. A natural strategy proceeds in three steps: (i) prove
structural stability of the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra under
coarse-graining (i.e., that the algebra is not diluted as $N$
increases, which Proposition 4.4 already establishes for fixed $N$);
(ii) prove universality of the V–A ratio under coarse-graining (that
$|g_V| = |g_A|$ holds for the averaged transfer operator
$\bar{T} = |S|^{-1}\sum_{T \in S} T$, not only for individual
operators); (iii) establish the connection to Wilson's lattice gauge
theory by identifying the precise correspondence between the
middle-section phase structure and Wilson's plaquette action, thereby
importing existing continuum limit results.

**$W^\pm$ and $Z^0$ boson masses.** In the Standard Model, gauge
boson masses arise from the Higgs mechanism. In the WorldBase
framework, A8 (symmetry preference) creates an effective potential
barrier at the boundary of the middle section $M_N$. A quantitative
analysis of this barrier in the scaling limit $N \to \infty$ may
provide the $W/Z$ mass scale without invoking spontaneous symmetry
breaking as an external input. This is a structural argument; the
quantitative derivation has not been completed.

**Left-handed fermion spinor representations.** The present framework
identifies the algebraic source of left-handedness (non-Hermitian
transfer under A6) but does not construct the full Weyl spinor
representation. This requires the emergent bridge of the first item
above as a prerequisite.

## The Precise Role of A9: From Vague Closure to Computable Constraint

Axiom A9 states that the system does not possess degrees of freedom beyond
those mandated by the axioms ($F = F_\text{axiom}$). A natural concern is
that this is too vague to be operational — how does one *count* degrees of
freedom?

In this paper, A9 is made computable through the following procedure. First,
count the independent physical transfer operators allowed by A4 and A6 (Step
1 of the proof of Theorem 5.1): this gives $F_\text{axiom} = 1$. Second,
identify all algebraic objects that can be constructed from this single
operator by Hermitian conjugation and commutation (Step 2): this gives three
generators. Third, A9 prohibits assigning independent parameters to any
decomposition of those generators that does not correspond to a new
independent operator: the ratio $\alpha/\beta$ would require a second
independent operator, which A4 + A6 do not allow.

This procedure — *count allowed operators, close algebraically, prohibit
extra parameters by A9* — is the same procedure used in the companion paper
to fix $D_{A1'} = 2$ via the squeeze $D_{A1'} \geq 2$ (from A1') and
$D_{A1'} \leq 2$ (from A9). A9 consistently plays the role of an *upper
bound* in squeeze arguments, converting open-ended algebraic possibilities
into unique determinations.

## Representativeness of the Two-State System

A potential concern is that the $N=2$ system is too minimal to be
representative of physical weak interactions. We address this concern at two
levels.

At the algebraic level, Proposition 4.4 (embedding stability) establishes
that the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra derived in the $N=2$ system is preserved
as a local sub-algebra in any larger $N$ system. The $N=4$ numerical
verification demonstrates this explicitly: the $6 \times 6$ system on $M_4$
decomposes into two independent copies of the $N=2$ algebra, with exact
agreement on all commutation relations.

At the physical level, the $N=2$ system captures the *minimal algebraic
content* of weak interaction structure: one directed transfer (A6), one
locality constraint (A4), one closure principle (A9). The specific value of
$N$ determines the size of the representation space but not the algebraic
structure. This is analogous to how the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra of angular
momentum is independent of the specific spin representation chosen to
illustrate it.

non-Hermitian operators, $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ emerges as the minimal

If the emergent bridge theorem — the weak-force analogue of Theorem CL —

# Appendix B: Proof of Proposition 4.4 (Embedding Stability)

**Proposition B.1.​** For any $N \geq 2$ and any pair $\{a,b\} \subset
\{1,\ldots,N\}$, the restriction of $E_{ab}$ to the subspace

$$V_{ab} = \mathrm{span}\bigl\{|x\rangle \in M_N : x_a=1,\, x_b=0\bigr\}
\cup \bigl\{|x\rangle \in M_N : x_a=0,\, x_b=1\bigr\}$$

is unitarily equivalent to the $N=2$ transfer operator
$T = \begin{pmatrix}0&0\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$.

*Proof.* Let $|u\rangle$ denote a basis vector of $V_{ab}$ with $x_a = 1$,
$x_b = 0$ (and all other active positions fixed), and $|v\rangle$ the
corresponding vector with $x_a = 0$, $x_b = 1$ (same other active
positions). The operator $E_{ab}$ acts as:

$$E_{ab}|u\rangle = |v\rangle, \qquad E_{ab}|v\rangle = 0$$

(since $x_a(|v\rangle) = 0$, the activation condition fails). In the ordered
basis $\{|u\rangle, |v\rangle\}$, this is:

$$E_{ab}\big|_{V_{ab}} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} = T.$$

The commutation relations $[H_i, H_j] = i\varepsilon_{ijk}H_k$ depend only
on the matrix entries of $T$ and $T^\dagger$ in this basis, which are
identical to those of the $N=2$ system. Therefore the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$
algebra generated on $V_{ab}$ is isomorphic to that of Theorem 4.3. $\square$

**Corollary B.2.​** The $N=4$ numerical verification of Section 4.4 is a
special case of Proposition B.1 with $N=4$, $\{a,b\} = \{1,2\}$. The two
independent two-state subsystems $\{|2\rangle, |4\rangle\}$ and
$\{|3\rangle, |5\rangle\}$ correspond to the two distinct choices of
remaining active position ($\{1,3\}$ and $\{1,4\}$ respectively), each
generating an independent copy of the $N=2$ algebra. $\square$
